# Meeting Summary

## Project direction and near-term scope
- The discussion opened with a review of the brainstorming output and the team’s desire to decide on next steps for the dispenser concept. Sam explained that Copilot had generated several architecture options, with some clearly weaker than others, while a few looked promising enough to pursue further. [00:54] [01:07] [04:13]
- Sterling agreed that the main mechanisms to keep in view were an auger, a solenoid, and a vibration motor, and asked whether the team was comfortable constraining the design around those elements. The group agreed that this was a reasonable direction. [04:46] [05:18]

## Cross-contamination and powder-handling constraints
- Sterling emphasized that avoiding cross-contamination was a key requirement: powder should not repeatedly touch shared surfaces unless those surfaces were effectively single-use. This constraint shaped the rest of the architecture discussion. [05:18]
- Two broad strategies were discussed for handling powder transfer without contamination: either use separate tubes feeding into a common container that then moves onward, or move the scale/container along a track to different dispensing stations. [06:17] [06:45] [08:10]
- Sterling also described a larger carousel-style concept where a robot arm or automated system could move cartridges/vials into a dispensing position, enabling many unique powder combinations without manual swapping at every step. [08:46] [21:43] [23:53]

## Review of existing commercial systems
- The team examined examples of existing dispensing systems, including a multi-station scale setup and a cartridge-based robotic dosing-head system, to better understand how industrial products manage powder handling and reconfiguration. [08:10] [08:46] [23:53]
- Sterling noted that these systems are already substantial investments, roughly in the $10,000–$30,000 range, and may not fully match the team’s workflow or powder requirements. He also pointed out that some appear to be auger-only, without the vibration-based mechanisms the team wants. [11:00] [11:26]
- Although one manual module was relatively affordable, the system as a whole still looked expensive, and Sterling suggested that buying a unit or sending powders to the vendor for testing could still provide valuable benchmark data if budget permits. [11:52] [12:33] [14:04]

## Recommended near-term design approach
- Sterling recommended focusing first on a single functional module containing the auger, solenoid, and vibration motor, rather than trying to solve every multi-powder layout problem immediately. The idea is to get one repeatable module working, then scale it by replication and later decide the layout strategy. [15:34] [16:43] [18:35]
- Sam confirmed the understanding that the current goal is to make one module work well and only later multiply that module into a larger system. [18:35] [18:44]
- Sterling stressed that the most valuable workflow is to move quickly from design to printed prototype to powder testing, so the team can feed real-world results back into future iterations. [17:29] [18:09]

## Modular versus integrated cartridge concept
- Sam raised an alternate cost-reduction concept: keep one shared set of electromechanical parts but swap in different cartridges or vials, each with a 3D-printed auger or internal dispensing part. This could potentially reduce cost dramatically while preserving the same core mechanism. [19:10] [19:58]
- Sterling said this modular cartridge concept was worth considering, but noted that it introduces extra engineering challenges, especially around rigid coupling, sealing, and ensuring good contact between the motor and the cartridge. [20:00] [21:12] [27:08]
- The team concluded that the integrated design and the modular swap-out design are similar enough that they can proceed in parallel for a while without needing to choose immediately. [21:12] [21:43]
- Sterling connected this cartridge idea to the industrial dosing-head example: the expensive part of the system is not the head itself, but the mechanism that picks up the head and mates it to the shared electromechanical components. [23:53] [26:09]

## Capping, shutters, and leak prevention
- A major concern in the modular approach was how to prevent powder leakage when cartridges are not in use. Sam suggested caps or a passive mechanical system that would keep cartridges sealed and only open them when aligned for dispensing. [28:28] [29:25]
- Sterling framed the sealing problem as similar to a normally closed valve and proposed three possible approaches: a normally closed mechanism, a cap/seal design, or a shutter system. [29:52] [31:36]
- He explained that a shutter does not stop leakage itself, but can protect the receiving area from contamination. He also said automated cap removal/insertion would be a tougher AI/design challenge than a shutter, though a shutter is less foolproof because it assumes some leakage may occur. [31:36] [32:54]

## Cost targets and scaling expectations
- Sterling gave a rough target of sub-$10,000 for a system supporting around 30 unique powders, with some flexibility up to about $15,000. He estimated that a dosing head could plausibly cost around $100 each when accounting for the stepper motor, solenoid, and vibration motor. [42:42]
- He also reminded the team to include assembly labor in cost estimates, suggesting roughly $20/hour for build effort when thinking about open-source reproducibility. [43:27]
- Sam clarified that the lab’s immediate alloy work usually involves no more than 7–8 powders, but Sterling still preferred designing toward 16 or more channels so that future campaigns could avoid manual swaps and support more ambitious automation goals. [44:05] [44:51] [46:09]
- Sterling expressed a long-term vision of supporting 100–200 unique powders over the next few years, enabling evening and weekend operation and reducing manual intervention between experiments. [45:42] [46:44]

## Copilot workflow, issue management, and cadence
- Sam asked when the team should start requesting visuals and CAD models from Copilot. Sterling recommended asking for visualizations or CAD outputs right away, while expecting the first results to be rough and iterative. [34:20] [34:47]
- Sterling challenged the team to aim for at least three Copilot feedback sessions per day, noting that this is a conservative benchmark and that the cycle could speed up once prints can be sent programmatically to the printer and tested more quickly. [34:47] [38:49]
- Sam asked whether creating new GitHub issues as needed was acceptable. Sterling confirmed that this was fine, with the caution that pull requests tied to Copilot sessions should not be merged prematurely without a quick check, since merging can block further Copilot iterations on that branch. [40:30] [41:48]
- The team agreed that the workflow should prioritize rapid iteration, practical testing, and continued refinement rather than waiting for a perfect design before moving ahead. [38:24] [39:59] [48:08]

## Action items discussed

- The team agreed to continue work on the single-module auger/solenoid/vibration design, while also spending some effort exploring the lower-cost modular cartridge path in parallel. [15:34] [19:58] [33:26]
