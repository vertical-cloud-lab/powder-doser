=== ABSTRACT UNDER REVIEW (150-word body) ===
Title: Agentic Systems Design for Laboratory Hardware: CAD, PCB, and Beyond in Developing an Open-Source Powder Doser

Agentic AI coding tools and generative design services promise to compress laboratory hardware development, yet their practical capabilities remain poorly characterized. We report a practitioner-level assessment spanning the systems-design stack of an open-source powder doser for laser powder bed fusion feedstock research: mechanical CAD, control electronics, firmware, and documentation. Generative-CAD workflows—CADSmith, zoo.dev, and GitHub Copilot Cloud Agents with Claude Opus 4.7 producing parametric CadQuery and OpenSCAD models—were complemented by agent-generated KiCad schematics and MicroPython firmware for stepper, solenoid, vibration-motor, and servo control with load-cell feedback. Fully AI-generated designs were not deployment-ready, so we adopted an engineer-led workflow: engineers defined geometry, dimensions, hand sketches, and manufacturing constraints; AI modeled the parts; engineers reviewed, printed, and iterated. Specificity substantially improved results, yet usable output still required many correction cycles, with weak spatial reasoning a recurring limitation. We present prototypes, workflow comparisons, and practical guidance for AI-assisted instrument development.

## Review 1 — Zachary Cordero (MIT)

**Reviewer background.** Cordero's research centers on additive manufacturing and powder processing of high-temperature structural materials for aerospace, including oxide-dispersion-strengthened Ni alloys fabricated via melt-based AM and the economics of 3D-printed ceramic cores for gas-turbine investment castings. He co-authored a major review on materials for extreme environments covering high-throughput fabrication methods such as additive manufacturing (hofmann2024developinggradientmetal pages 1-2).

**Symposium-fit assessment.** The abstract touches two symposium themes—"Advanced manufacturing and digital production systems" and "Emerging tools for accelerating materials development"—but does so obliquely. It describes an AI-assisted workflow for building an open-source powder doser for LPBF feedstock research, which is genuinely relevant to powder-AM researchers. However, the abstract reads more like a software-tools evaluation than a materials-innovation story. For the TMS audience at this symposium, the *why* (accelerating custom instrument development for LPBF research) must be at least as prominent as the *how* (which AI tools were tried).

**What this reviewer wants to see:**
- **The LPBF connection foregrounded.** Powder dosing is a critical step in powder-bed AM. Modular, open-source instrumentation for studying powder spreading and dosing has been shown to be important for developing robust process parameters and assessing feedstock characteristics (oropeza2021amodulartestbed pages 1-3). The abstract should make this materials significance explicit, not leave it as a parenthetical mention.
- **Quantitative results.** The abstract claims "specificity substantially improved results" and notes "many correction cycles," but provides no numbers. Even rough metrics—how many designs compiled on first pass, how many correction cycles were needed, or what time savings were achieved—would sharpen the contribution. The literature on generative AI for CAD emphasizes validation burden and manual engineer intervention as key bottlenecks (li2025generativeaiand pages 3-4, li2025generativeaiand pages 1-3), and quantifying these would position the work relative to the state of the art.
- **Spatial reasoning claim grounded.** The statement that "weak spatial reasoning [was] a recurring limitation" is well-supported in the broader LLM-CAD literature—spatial reasoning failures are documented across CadQuery and OpenSCAD generation studies (wendland2025exploringthepotentials pages 1-2, shui2026articadarticulatedcad pages 1-3)—but the abstract should signal whether this was characterized systematically or is an informal observation.
- **Lab-to-pilot framing.** One of the symposium's sessions addresses "scale-up challenges: from lab to pilot to production." A sentence connecting AI-assisted instrument development to faster lab-to-pilot iteration would strengthen fit.

**Where the abstract over-reaches:** The opening claim that agentic tools "promise to compress laboratory hardware development" implies time savings. If the study did not measure elapsed time, soften to "are being explored to accelerate" or similar.

**Where it under-reaches:** The powder-doser application is genuinely interesting to an AM audience—controlled handling of metal powders for LPBF feedstock studies is a real instrumentation need—but this is buried. The title says "Laboratory Hardware" when it could say "LPBF Feedstock Research." The open-source angle is also undersold for a symposium that discusses democratization of materials innovation.

## Review 2 — Douglas Hofmann (NASA JPL)

**Reviewer background.** Hofmann is a principal scientist at JPL whose work spans gradient metal alloys via laser-deposition AM, bulk metallic glass components for spacecraft, and additively manufactured tools for robotic space exploration. His research consistently emphasizes deployment readiness, qualification, and performance in extreme environments (hofmann2024developinggradientmetal pages 1-2, hofmann2024developinggradientmetal pages 8-8).

**Symposium-fit assessment.** The symposium's scope explicitly includes "qualification, certification, and adoption barriers," "translation of materials research into commercial products," and "critical materials and supply chain strategy." The abstract has latent connections to all three—an open-source instrument lowers supply-chain dependence on proprietary vendors; the finding that AI designs were "not deployment-ready" speaks directly to qualification barriers; and the engineer-led workflow is itself a translational methodology. But none of these connections are articulated. As written, the abstract is too insular to the AI-tooling side and risks being perceived as a computer-science contribution rather than a materials-innovation one.

**What this reviewer wants to see:**
- **Headline finding up front.** The most important result—"fully AI-generated designs were not deployment-ready"—is buried in the middle of the abstract. For an audience that cares about whether hardware actually works in the lab (or in space), this should be the central message, stated early and clearly. Agentic AI systems for hardware design have been shown to require task decomposition, deterministic tool augmentation, and adversarial specialist-verifier loops to achieve reliable automation (nallathambi2026a3dagenticai pages 14-14), and the abstract's finding aligns with this pattern.
- **Open-source as a supply-chain story.** The open-source powder doser is not just a software exercise; it is a potential pathway for materials labs to develop custom instruments without proprietary vendor lock-in. This maps directly to the symposium's supply-chain and commercialization themes and should be stated.
- **Qualification barriers articulated.** The gap between AI-generated output and deployment-ready designs is itself a qualification barrier. Naming it as such would resonate with the symposium audience and connect to Hofmann's own concern with hardware that must function reliably in demanding environments.
- **Broader implications.** The abstract should end with a sentence on generalizability: could this engineer-led, AI-assisted workflow paradigm accelerate custom instrument development across materials laboratories more broadly? Currently, the closing sentence ("practical guidance for AI-assisted instrument development") gestures at this but does not land the point.

**Where the abstract over-reaches:** Listing specific vendor names (CADSmith, zoo.dev, GitHub Copilot Cloud Agents with Claude Opus 4.7) spends precious words on details that will date quickly and are less important than the workflow architecture and results. The current literature on AI for electronic design automation similarly emphasizes that tool-specific findings struggle to generalize across platforms (chen2026reportfornsf pages 5-6).

**Where it under-reaches:** The abstract misses an opportunity to discuss what "deployment-ready" means in concrete terms—did designs fail to compile, fail to print, fail functionally, or require dimensional correction? This specificity would make the finding actionable for the audience.
