## Tier 1: Best-Fit / Primary Targets

### 1. Digital Discovery (RSC)

Digital Discovery is the leading dedicated venue for the intersection of automation, AI, and open-source tools in chemistry and materials science. It explicitly publishes SDL instrument papers, open-hardware designs, and software tools. Critically, it offers a **"Commit"** article type specifically designed for open-source hardware and software updates — as demonstrated by Yoshikawa et al. (2026), who published an open-hardware digital pipette v2 for SDL liquid transfer as a Commit paper, complete with 3D-printed CAD files, Arduino-based firmware, cost analysis, and ISO-standard validation data (yoshikawa2026commitdigitalpipette pages 2-3, yoshikawa2026commitdigitalpipette pages 1-2). The journal has also published a pellet dispensomixer and pellet distributor for automated nanocomposite material compounding as open hardware (Hernández-del-Valle et al., 2024), which directly parallels a multi-powder doser (hernandezdelvalle2024pelletdispensomixerand pages 9-9). Additional closely related work includes autonomous biomimetic solid dispensing (Jiang et al., 2023), the Chemspyd open-source interface for robotic chemistry platforms (Seifrid et al., 2024), and extensive SDL reviews and perspectives including the "frugal twin" concept for low-cost SDLs (Lo et al., 2024) and democratizing SDLs via 3D printing (Doloi et al., 2025) (pelkie2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 16-17, doloi2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 1-2, doloi2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 29-30, maffettoneUnknownyearwhatismissing pages 1-3). The journal's scope encompasses data-driven research, laboratory automation, and open tools for accelerated discovery, making it the single best fit for the described manuscript.

### 2. HardwareX (Elsevier)

HardwareX is Elsevier's dedicated open-access journal for open-source scientific hardware. Its structured article template explicitly requires build and operation instructions, validation and characterization data, a bill of materials, and linked repositories — precisely the elements this paper offers (miljkovic2109towardsfairprinciples pages 3-6). The journal enforces open-source licensing across both hardware and software components. HardwareX has published numerous automated laboratory instrument papers, including the OpenWorkstation modular open-source platform for automated workflows (Eggert et al., 2020), open-source microfluidic controllers and sensor platforms (Kehl et al., 2021), syringe pump systems, and various low-cost lab automation tools (wenzel2023openhardwarefrom pages 25-28, eggert2020openworkstationamodular pages 24-25, sarıyer2023openhardwarefor pages 14-15, pearce2020economicsavingsfor pages 2-4). A comprehensive review identified 86 open-source hardware articles from HardwareX spanning instruments using 3D printing and Arduino-based electronics (pearce2020economicsavingsfor pages 2-4). Crucially, the journal charges **no APC**, maximizing accessibility. While its CiteScore (~5.7) is moderate, it delivers maximum reproducibility signaling and community adoption potential. A HardwareX submission would complement a Digital Discovery paper by providing the full reproducibility-focused documentation.

## Tier 2: Strong Secondary Options

### 3. Review of Scientific Instruments (AIP)

RSI is the classic venue for instrument design, fabrication, and qualification papers. It has published directly relevant work including a modular testbed for mechanized powder spreading for AM (Oropeza et al., 2021) and automated electrodeposition systems for self-driving labs (Quinn et al., 2026) (pearce2020economicsavingsfor pages 15-16). RSI values rigorous instrument characterization and calibration data, which aligns well with the extensive gravimetric calibration dataset across many powders. However, RSI does not emphasize open-source licensing or repository-linked reproducibility the way Digital Discovery or HardwareX do, and its impact factor (~1.6) is lower than most other options. It is best suited if the paper is framed primarily as a precision instrumentation contribution rather than an SDL/open-hardware community resource.

### 4. Additive Manufacturing (Elsevier)

With an IF of ~11.0, Additive Manufacturing is the highest-impact domain-specific journal for AM research. It would be a strong choice if the manuscript is reframed to emphasize how the doser enables autonomous alloy composition design for AM processes, rather than presenting primarily as a hardware description. High-throughput AM alloy exploration is a very active topic in this journal's community. The fit improves substantially if the paper includes demonstration data showing how the instrument changes alloy exploration throughput or enables compositionally graded powder-bed feedstocks.

### 5. Additive Manufacturing Letters (Elsevier)

This newer companion journal offers rapid publication in a shorter letter format. It would suit a concise version of the paper focused on the AM-enabling capability (e.g., 50-powder autonomous dosing for combinatorial alloy design) rather than full open-hardware documentation. Its introductory policies may include reduced or waived APCs.

### 6. SoftwareX (Elsevier)

SoftwareX publishes software metapapers with linked code repositories and is free to publish in. It is **not suitable for the main combined paper** but would be an excellent venue for a companion metapaper focused solely on the control/calibration software package, providing a separate citable software DOI. This dual-publication strategy (main hardware paper + SoftwareX software paper) is increasingly common in the SDL community.

## Tier 3: Stretch / High-Impact Venues

### 7–9. Nature Communications, Science Advances, Matter

These journals have published landmark SDL papers: MacLeod et al. (2020, 2022) demonstrated SDLs for thin-film discovery in Science Advances and Pareto-front optimization in Nature Communications; Volk & Abolhasani (2024) published SDL performance metrics in Nature Communications (nematov2025thebrightfuture pages 9-11, nematov2025thebrightfuture pages 11-11); Canty et al. (2025) discussed SDL accessibility in Nature Communications; and Stach et al. (2021) published an autonomous experimentation community perspective in Matter (lookman2026materialsinformaticsemergence pages 37-39). However, **all of these require a major scientific discovery or paradigm-shifting result** enabled by the instrument, not just excellent hardware description and calibration. A purely instrument-focused paper would be returned without review at these venues. They become viable only if the paper includes a closed-loop autonomous alloy discovery campaign yielding significant new materials science results.

### 10–12. Cell Reports Physical Science, npj Computational Materials, ACS Central Science

These are progressively less natural fits. Cell Reports Physical Science occasionally publishes platform papers but expects a strong physical-science story. npj Computational Materials is more aligned with computational/data methods than physical hardware (nematov2025thebrightfuture pages 9-11, lookman2026materialsinformaticsemergence pages 37-39). ACS Central Science requires broadly transformative results. All would need the paper to de-emphasize hardware documentation and instead demonstrate paradigm-changing autonomous materials discovery.

## Tier 4: Domain-Specific & Fallback Options

### 13. Materials & Design (Elsevier)

A respectable materials-processing journal (IF ~7.6) that could accommodate the paper if written as a materials-processing/design-enabling contribution with the extensive powder calibration dataset as the primary data offering.

### 14. Lab on a Chip (RSC)

Despite being a strong RSC journal, its scope centers on microfluidics and miniaturized lab systems for biology/chemistry (sarıyer2023openhardwarefor pages 14-15). A macro-scale powder doser for AM alloy discovery is significantly off-scope.

### 15. IEEE/ASME Transactions on Mechatronics

Good for the mechatronic engineering aspects (auger auto-change mechanism, closed-loop control, firmware) but would require de-emphasizing the materials science calibration dataset and SDL framing. Lengthy review times (~3–6 months) are a drawback.

### 16. Journal of Open Hardware (Ubiquity Press/Western University)

Scope is excellent — an open-hardware journal accepting detailed hardware documentation. However, it is an emerging journal with no established impact factor, limited readership, and low visibility in the materials/AM communities (pearce2020economicsavingsfor pages 15-16).

### 17. PLOS ONE

A reliable fallback that accepts any technically sound paper (IF ~2.9). Open-hardware laboratory instruments have been published in the PLOS ecosystem (pearce2020economicsavingsfor pages 15-16, wenzel2023openhardwarefrom pages 25-28). Useful if other venues reject on novelty grounds, but provides no field-specific prestige.

### 18. Journal of Open Source Software (JOSS)

Publishes **software only** — no hardware description as the primary content. Excellent for a separate companion software publication for the calibration/control Python package, providing a citable software DOI. Fast review (~2–6 weeks), no APC.

### 19. Journal of Open Source Hardware (Community)

An emerging venue with very limited publication history and uncertain long-term viability. Not recommended as a primary target.

---

## Strategic Recommendations

**Primary submission:** Digital Discovery (RSC) is the clear top recommendation. Its scope explicitly encompasses open-hardware SDL instruments, it offers the Commit article type for open-source hardware, and it has published the most directly analogous work (pellet dispensomixers, digital pipettes, autonomous solid dispensing) (hernandezdelvalle2024pelletdispensomixerand pages 9-9, yoshikawa2026commitdigitalpipette pages 1-2). The journal also carries a respectable IF (~6.2) and fast turnaround.

**Parallel companion submissions:** Consider simultaneously submitting (i) the full hardware documentation and CAD files to HardwareX (free, maximum reproducibility), and (ii) the control/calibration software to JOSS or SoftwareX (free, citable software DOI). This three-paper strategy maximizes visibility, reproducibility, and citability across the open-hardware, SDL, and software communities.

**If rejected from Digital Discovery:** Review of Scientific Instruments (for an instrument-focused reframing) or Additive Manufacturing Letters (for a concise AM-application-focused reframing) are strong fallback targets.

**If discovery results are available:** If the paper can be expanded to include a substantial autonomous alloy discovery campaign, Nature Communications becomes a realistic stretch target given its track record of publishing SDL work (nematov2025thebrightfuture pages 9-11, nematov2025thebrightfuture pages 11-11).
