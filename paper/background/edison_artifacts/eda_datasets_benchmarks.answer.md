# Datasets, Benchmarks, and Evaluation Methodologies for Machine-Learning and Generative EDA (2025 Survey)

## 1. Introduction

The rapid application of large language models (LLMs) and other ML methods to electronic design automation (EDA) has created a pressing need for standardized datasets, benchmarks, and evaluation metrics spanning the full design stack—from HDL code generation through chip physical design to printed circuit board (PCB) schematic and layout synthesis. This survey catalogues the principal resources available as of 2025 and identifies critical gaps, with emphasis on their relevance to evaluating generative pipelines for open-hardware control PCBs.

## 2. HDL / RTL Generation Benchmarks

### 2.1 VerilogEval

**Citation:** Liu, M., Pinckney, N., Khailany, B., & Ren, H. (2023). VerilogEval: Evaluating Large Language Models for Verilog Code Generation. *ArXiv*, abs/2309.07544.

VerilogEval is a benchmark comprising 156 Verilog code-generation problems sourced from the HDLBits instructional website, ranging from simple combinational circuits to complex finite state machines (liu2023verilogevalevaluatinglarge pages 1-2). It provides two evaluation tracks—VerilogEval-machine (143 validated problems with auto-generated descriptions) and VerilogEval-human (156 problems with manually crafted descriptions) (liu2023verilogevalevaluatinglarge pages 3-4). Functional correctness is evaluated using the pass@k metric, where a problem is considered solved if at least one of *k* sampled LLM outputs passes simulation against a golden reference using the open-source Icarus Verilog simulator (liu2023verilogevalevaluatinglarge pages 3-4, liu2023verilogevalevaluatinglarge pages 5-7). The benchmark also includes 8,502 problem-code training pairs mined from GitHub Verilog repositories for supervised fine-tuning experiments (liu2023verilogevalevaluatinglarge pages 5-7). The updated VerilogEval v2 extends evaluation to specification-to-RTL tasks, adds in-context learning support, and provides automated failure classification (pinckney2025revisitingverilogevala pages 1-3). The benchmark is available at https://github.com/NVlabs/verilog-eval (liu2023verilogevalevaluatinglarge pages 2-3). **Relevance:** Directly applicable for evaluating generation of controller/FPGA logic modules that may accompany open control PCBs, though it does not assess board-level concerns such as component selection, connectivity, or manufacturability.

### 2.2 RTLLM and RTLLM 2.0

**Citation:** Lu, Y., Liu, S., Zhang, Q., & Xie, Z. (2024). RTLLM: An Open-Source Benchmark for Design RTL Generation with Large Language Model. *ASP-DAC 2024*, pp. 722–727. Liu, S., Lu, Y., Fang, W., Li, M., & Xie, Z. (2024). OpenLLM-RTL: Open Dataset and Benchmark for LLM-Aided Design RTL Generation. *ICCAD 2024*, pp. 1–9.

RTLLM collects 30 common digital designs (11 arithmetic and 19 logic) with three progressive evaluation goals: a syntax goal (verifiable via synthesis into a netlist), a functionality goal (verifiable via testbench simulation), and a design quality goal (evaluating post-synthesis PPA against human-designed references) (lu2024rtllmanopensource pages 1-3, lu2024rtllmanopensource pages 3-4). RTLLM 2.0 expands the suite to 50 hand-crafted designs covering a broader range of complexities and is accompanied by AssertEval (18 designs for assertion generation) and RTLCoder-Data (80K training samples, including a 7K verified high-quality subset) (liu2024openllmrtlopendataset pages 1-2, liu2024openllmrtlopendataset pages 2-3). Each design provides a natural-language description, testbench, and correct reference RTL. Available at https://github.com/hkust-zhiyao/RTLLM (lu2024rtllmanopensource pages 1-3). **Relevance:** More relevant than VerilogEval for evaluating synthesizable digital peripherals or glue logic that may reside on an open control board, but still misses board-level connectivity, ERC, and component/package constraints.

### 2.3 CVDP (Comprehensive Verilog Design Problems)

**Citation:** Pinckney, N. et al. (2025). Comprehensive Verilog Design Problems: A Next-Generation Benchmark Dataset for Evaluating Large Language Models and Agents on RTL Design and Verification. *ArXiv*, abs/2506.14074.

CVDP is a next-generation benchmark comprising 783 problems across 13 task categories authored by experienced hardware engineers, covering RTL generation, verification, debugging, specification alignment, and technical Q&A. It supports both non-agentic and agentic task formats, with state-of-the-art models achieving no more than 34% pass@1 on code generation. **Relevance:** Especially valuable for evaluating agentic HDL workflows involving iterative debug and verification loops, analogous to how a PCB design agent would need to iterate on ERC/DRC feedback.

### 2.4 VeriContaminated

**Citation:** Wang, Z. et al. (2025). VeriContaminated: Assessing LLM-Driven Verilog Coding for Data Contamination. *IEEE ICLAD 2025*, pp. 117–123.

VeriContaminated is a meta-evaluation study that analyzes data contamination across VerilogEval and RTLLM benchmarks for state-of-the-art LLMs, finding near-100% contamination rates for some commercial models (wang2025vericontaminatedassessingllmdriven pages 1-2). It employs detection methods including CCD and Min-K% Prob and explores mitigations and their trade-offs for code quality versus fairness. **Relevance:** A critical cautionary resource for any open-hardware generative pipeline—apparent HDL generation success may be inflated by benchmark data leaking into LLM pre-training corpora, undermining reproducibility and trustworthiness of evaluations.

### 2.5 TuRTLe

**Citation:** Garcia-Gasulla, D. et al. (2025). TuRTLe: A Unified Evaluation of LLMs for RTL Generation. *ArXiv*, abs/2504.01986.

TuRTLe is a unified evaluation framework that integrates multiple existing benchmarks (including VerilogEval and RTLLM) and adds synthesis-aware scoring. It evaluates LLMs across cascaded stages—syntax (STX), functional correctness (FNC), and synthesizability (SYN)—using PASS@1, and adds a post-synthesis PPA-score derived from OpenLANE and the open-source SKY130A PDK (garciagasulla2025turtleaunified pages 3-4). **Relevance:** By incorporating synthesis and PPA into the evaluation loop, TuRTLe provides a methodological precedent for PCB benchmarks that should similarly go beyond schematic correctness to assess ERC, DRC, BOM validity, and manufacturability.

## 3. Chip-Level Placement and Routing Benchmarks

### 3.1 CircuitNet and CircuitNet 2.0

**Citation:** Chai, Z. et al. (2022). CircuitNet: An Open-Source Dataset for Machine Learning Applications in Electronic Design Automation. *arXiv:2208.01040*. Jiang, X. et al. (2024). CircuitNet 2.0: An Advanced Dataset for Promoting Machine Learning Innovations in Realistic Chip Design Environment.

CircuitNet is the first large-scale open-source dataset for ML in VLSI physical design, built from RISC-V designs mapped to 28nm technology with 10,242 post-flow layouts from 12,960 backend design runs (chai2022circuitnetanopensource pages 1-2, chai2022circuitnetanopensource pages 2-2). It supports three prediction tasks—congestion, DRC violation, and IR drop—evaluated using NRMSE and SSIM for congestion, and ROC-AUC/PR-AUC for DRC and IR drop (chai2022circuitnetanopensource pages 2-2). CircuitNet 2.0 extends coverage to million-gate designs (CPU, GPU, AI chips) at 14nm FinFET and adds timing prediction tasks (jiang2024circuitnet2.0an pages 1-3, jiang2024circuitnet2.0ana pages 1-3). Available at https://circuitnet.github.io/ (jiang2024circuitnet2.0ana pages 1-3). **Relevance:** The strongest open chip-physical-design dataset family; it informs evaluation methodology for congestion/DRC prediction that could be adapted for PCB routing quality assessment, though it does not directly address board-level constraints.

### 3.2 ISPD/ICCAD Contest Benchmark Suites

**Citation:** Various, 2005–2025. See Kahng, A.B. (2021). Advancing Placement. *ISPD 2021*, pp. 15–22; and individual contest proceedings.

The ISPD and ICCAD conferences have hosted 14+ placement and routing contests since 2005, with evolving themes including routability-driven placement (ISPD 2011, DAC 2012), detailed placement (ICCAD 2013), timing-driven placement (ICCAD 2014/2015), and large-scale global routing (ISPD 2025) (kahng2021advancingplacement pages 5-6, jiang2023invitedpaperaccelerating pages 8-9). Standard metrics include half-perimeter wirelength (HPWL), routing congestion/overflow, timing slack (WNS/TNS), legality, and runtime (kahng2021advancingplacement pages 2-3, jiang2023invitedpaperaccelerating pages 8-9). Benchmarks are typically distributed in Bookshelf or LEF/DEF formats and are publicly available through conference archives (bustany2023the2023mlcad pages 3-4). **Relevance:** Essential methodological precedent for chip placement/routing evaluation; the benchmarking culture and progressive metric design serve as a model for establishing equivalent PCB benchmark contests, though chip-level benchmarks do not capture board-level assembly, EMI, or thermal constraints.

### 3.3 EDALearn

**Citation:** Pan, J., Chang, C.-C., Xie, Z., Chen, Y., & Li, H.H. (2024). EDALearn: A Comprehensive RTL-to-Signoff EDA Benchmark for Democratized and Reproducible ML for EDA Research. *ICCAD 2024*, pp. 1–8.

EDALearn is the first holistic, open-source benchmark suite spanning the complete RTL-to-signoff EDA flow, targeting FreePDK 45nm and ASAP 7nm technology nodes (pan2024edalearnacomprehensive pages 1-2, pan2024edalearnacomprehensive pages 3-4). It aggregates practical VLSI instances from multiple public sources (IWLS, BOOM/Chipyard, OpenCores, ISCAS, ITC'99) and provides standardized evaluation for routability prediction, IR-drop prediction, and cross-stage analysis with both image-like and vector-based features. **Relevance:** A strong template for how an open PCB benchmark should connect source design intent through intermediate stages to final manufacturable artifacts, supporting reproducibility and cross-technology transfer.

### 3.4 EDA-Schema-V2

**Citation:** Shrestha, P. et al. (2025). EDA-Schema-V2: A Multimodal Schema, Open Datasets, and Benchmarks for Machine Learning in Digital Physical Design.

EDA-Schema-V2 provides approximately 7,800 physical design instances from 18 IWLS'05 benchmark circuits across four open PDKs (SkyWater 130nm, Nangate 45nm, IHP SG13G2 130nm, ASAP 7nm), encompassing over 275 million gates, 75 million nets, and 36 million extracted timing paths (shrestha2605edaschemav2amultimodal pages 1-3, shrestha2605edaschemav2amultimodal pages 28-29). It offers stage-resolved representations from synthesis through detailed routing in a multimodal entity-relationship schema covering circuit graphs, spatial layout images, and QoR metrics. The dataset and schema are released on GitHub. **Relevance:** Particularly relevant methodologically as a multimodal, stage-aware, reproducible, and open benchmark—a strong model for how an open PCB benchmark could combine schematic, layout, netlist, images, and fabrication outputs in a unified schema.

## 4. Analog Circuit Datasets and Benchmarks

### 4.1 AnalogGym

**Citation:** Li, J. et al. (2024). AnalogGym: An Open and Practical Testing Suite for Analog Circuit Synthesis. *ICCAD 2024*, pp. 1–9.

AnalogGym provides 30 circuit topologies across five categories: amplifiers (16), low-dropout regulators (6), sensing front ends (6), voltage references (3), and phase-locked loops (1) (li2024analoggymanopen pages 2-3). It supports comprehensive evaluation including DC, AC, pole-zero, and transient analyses across process-voltage-temperature (PVT) corners (SS/FF/SF/FS/TT, 1.08–1.32V, −40 to 125°C) (li2024analoggymanopen pages 3-4). Compatible with Cadence Spectre, Synopsys HSPICE, and the open-source Ngspice simulator with the sky130 PDK for amplifier and LDO categories (li2024analoggymanopen pages 1-2). Available at https://github.com/CODA-Team/AnalogGym. **Relevance:** Directly relevant for analog portions of control PCBs (power rails, sensor front ends, voltage references), though it evaluates IC-level analog synthesis rather than discrete-component board schematics.

### 4.2 AICircuit

**Citation:** Mehradfar, A. et al. (2024). AICircuit: A Multi-Level Dataset and Benchmark for AI-Driven Analog Integrated Circuit Design. *ArXiv*, abs/2407.18272.

AICircuit comprises seven basic analog/RF circuits (common-source voltage amplifier, two-stage amplifier, cascode amplifier, LNA, power amplifier, VCO, and mixer) plus two complex heterogeneous wireless transceiver systems (mehradfar2024aicircuitamultilevel pages 2-4, mehradfar2024aicircuitamultilevel pages 1-2). Data is generated via Cadence schematic-level parameter sweeps with commercial simulation, and performance metrics include DC power, bandwidth, gain, noise figure, phase noise, and output power (mehradfar2024aicircuitamultilevel pages 6-7). The dataset and code are open-sourced on GitHub (mehradfar2024aicircuitamultilevel pages 1-2). **Relevance:** Useful for learning analog performance mappings and inverse design concepts that could inform board-level analog block selection, but it is IC-centric rather than a PCB schematic/layout benchmark.

## 5. PCB-Level Datasets and Benchmarks

### 5.1 PCBSchemaGen

**Citation:** Zou, H., Han, P., Nazerian, E., & Huang, A.Q. (2025). PCBSchemaGen: Constraint-Guided Schematic Design via LLM for Printed Circuit Boards (PCB). *ArXiv*, abs/2602.00510.

PCBSchemaGen is the most directly relevant benchmark for evaluating generative PCB design pipelines. It provides 23 unique PCB schematic-generation tasks spanning analog, digital, and power domains, with a curated KiCad-compatible component library containing 18 symbols, 21 footprints, and 14 reference datasheets (zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 5-6, zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 26-28). Evaluation uses Pass@k (k=1,5 with n=15 trials and up to 3 self-correction retries), topology verification via subgraph isomorphism (VF2), ERC checks, and semantic constraint checking grounded in a datasheet-derived knowledge graph (zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 6-7, zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 1-2). The framework outputs executable SKiDL Python code that produces KiCad-ready PCB layout files. The benchmark and knowledge graph are open-sourced on GitHub. **Relevance:** The most directly applicable resource for evaluating open-hardware control-PCB generation—it is board-level, datasheet-grounded, KiCad/SKiDL-compatible, and checks topology rather than text similarity alone.

## 7. Gaps and Challenges in Evaluating Generative Circuit/PCB Design

### 7.1 Lack of Standardized, Manufacturable, Open PCB Datasets

The most acute gap identified across the literature is the near-complete absence of large-scale, standardized, open PCB schematic and layout datasets suitable for training and evaluating generative models. As noted by multiple sources, "automated PCB schematic design remains unexplored due to the scarcity of open-source data and the absence of simulation-based verification" (zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 1-2). PCB schematic capture formats are vendor-specific, making large-scale corpus construction difficult (zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 2-3). Even at the chip level, "EDA and design data are limited and often not in the open domain," and generating ML-ready EDA data requires expensive and time-consuming flows that can take "weeks to months" per run, compounded by NDA restrictions that "prohibit the release of the original data" (chen2026reportfornsf pages 3-4, jiang2024circuitnet2.0ana pages 1-3). PCBSchemaGen's 23-task benchmark represents a pioneering but very small-scale effort; no "ImageNet-scale" open PCB dataset yet exists (chen2026reportfornsf pages 5-6).

### 7.2 Data Contamination and Benchmark Validity

Data contamination—where benchmark data inadvertently leaks into LLM pre-training corpora—is a critical concern for HDL generation benchmarks. VeriContaminated demonstrated near-100% contamination rates for some commercial LLMs on both VerilogEval and RTLLM, fundamentally undermining the validity of reported pass rates and limiting industrial adoption (wang2025vericontaminatedassessingllmdriven pages 1-2). The undisclosed nature of pre-training datasets, even for nominally open-source models, makes contamination detection essential but difficult. This concern extends directly to any PCB-level benchmarks that might be developed from publicly available KiCad repositories.

### 7.3 Surrogate vs. End-to-End Metrics

A recurring finding across chip-level work is that intermediate surrogate metrics frequently misalign with final design quality. ChiPBench demonstrated that even if a placement algorithm achieves dominant intermediate metrics, "the final PPA results are unsatisfactory" (from paper search results). At the HDL level, prior work critiques BLEU-like metrics noting they "do not correlate well with functional correctness" (liu2023verilogevalevaluatinglarge pages 3-4). For PCB generation, this implies that evaluating only schematic correctness is insufficient—a complete evaluation must also verify that the generated design can be routed, passes DRC, has a valid BOM, and is actually manufacturable.

### 7.4 Reproducibility Concerns

Multiple sources highlight reproducibility as a systemic challenge. Proprietary PDKs, closed-source EDA tools, and restricted benchmark data limit who can reproduce experimental results (shrestha2605edaschemav2amultimodal pages 1-3, jiang2024circuitnet2.0an pages 1-3). The NSF Workshop on AI for EDA explicitly calls for "open-source initiatives for developing shared datasets, tools, and benchmarks" to "enable reproducibility and innovation" (chen2026reportfornsf pages 3-4). Open toolchains such as OpenROAD, Ngspice, KiCad, and open PDKs (sky130, Nangate45, ASAP7) are enabling a more reproducible ecosystem, but coverage gaps remain—especially for advanced nodes and board-level design rules.

### 7.5 Missing Dimensions for PCB Evaluation

Board-level design introduces evaluation dimensions absent from chip-level benchmarks: component sourcing and BOM validation, mechanical constraints (connector placement, enclosure fit), thermal and EMI compliance, creepage/clearance requirements, return-current path analysis, and integration with firmware/software. Existing analog simulators like SPICE are insufficient for PCB-level correctness validation because they model idealized components rather than real IC packages with pin-level constraints (zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 1-2). No current benchmark comprehensively addresses these board-specific dimensions, representing the largest gap for evaluating a generative open-hardware control-PCB pipeline.

## 8. Conclusions

The landscape of datasets and benchmarks for ML/generative EDA has matured substantially for HDL code generation (VerilogEval, RTLLM, CVDP) and chip physical design (CircuitNet, ISPD/ICCAD suites, EDALearn, EDA-Schema-V2), with growing resources for analog circuit synthesis (AnalogGym, AICircuit). However, the PCB domain remains severely underserved: PCBSchemaGen's 23-task benchmark is a pioneering but small-scale first step. For evaluating a generative pipeline targeting open-hardware control PCBs, the community urgently needs (a) large-scale, standardized, manufacturable PCB corpora in open formats such as KiCad, (b) multi-stage evaluation frameworks that assess schematic correctness, routability, DRC/ERC compliance, BOM validity, and downstream manufacturability, and (c) contamination-aware benchmarking practices that ensure evaluation integrity. The methodological lessons from chip-level evaluation—particularly the importance of end-to-end metrics over surrogates and the value of open, stage-aware, multimodal benchmark schemas—provide a strong foundation for building the missing PCB evaluation infrastructure.
