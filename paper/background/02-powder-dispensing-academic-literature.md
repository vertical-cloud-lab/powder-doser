## Detailed Discussion by Topic Area

### (a) Multi-Material and High-Throughput Metal AM Powder Dispensing

The fundamental challenge in multi-material powder bed fusion (PBF) is the selective placement of different powders within a single build layer. Neirinck et al. (2021) provide the most focused review of powder deposition hardware for multimetal PBF, cataloging approaches ranging from dual-feed pistons and split-reservoir recoaters to pipet-based point dosing, vacuum suction heads for powder removal/replacement, and drum-based selective deposition (e.g., Aerosint) (neirinck2021powderdepositionsystems pages 4-5, neirinck2021powderdepositionsystems pages 1-2, neirinck2021powderdepositionsystems pages 2-4, neirinck2021powderdepositionsystems pages 5-6). Each approach trades off resolution, deposition rate, and cross-contamination risk. Schneck et al. (2021) complement this by benchmarking holohedral, nozzle-based, and masked deposition concepts against quantitative metrics, reporting that multi-material deposition rates in PBF typically range from 60–140 mm³/s (versus 240–560 mm³/s for mono-material builds) and that minimum in-layer feature sizes are approximately 130–240 µm, with the technology at manufacturing readiness level 4–5 (schneck2021reviewonadditive pages 4-5).

Wei and Li (2021) review the physics and hardware of multi-material laser PBF specifically, describing blade-based dual-powder containers and in-situ powder mixing strategies for vertical functionally graded material (FGM) builds, and contrasting L-PBF powder sizes (10–50 µm) and layer thicknesses (<100 µm) with coarser DED feedstocks (wei2021recentprogressand pages 1-3). Ermurat and Ziba (2025) provide a directly actionable hardware precedent: an Arduino-controlled dual-component continuous powder feeder tested with Ti6Al4V, Zirconium, and Inconel 625. They report dosing deviations as low as 2.9% for spherical Ti6Al4V at 15 g/min, but as high as 28.8% for irregular Zr at 20 g/min, demonstrating that powder morphology and cohesiveness strongly determine feeding accuracy (ermurat2025developmentofelectronically pages 10-12, ermurat2025developmentofelectronically pages 1-3).

### (b) Compositionally Graded Alloy Discovery and Combinatorial Materials Science

The enormous compositional space of high-entropy and refractory alloys demands high-throughput synthesis methods. Li et al. (2018) frame the four principal combinatorial approaches—rapid alloy prototyping, diffusion multiples, laser additive manufacturing, and thin-film co-deposition—establishing the scientific case for multi-powder dosing as a throughput enabler for HEA discovery (li2018combinatorialmetallurgicalsynthesis pages 1-2). Melia et al. (2020) demonstrate this in practice, using laser-based DED with blended elemental Mo, Nb, Ta, and W powders to rapidly explore refractory HEA composition space, while noting that compositional heterogeneity arising from differing powder morphologies and preferential melting remains a key challenge (melia2020highthroughputadditivemanufacturing pages 1-2). Pegues et al. (2021) extend this to multiphase HEAs by grading CoCrFeMnNi with refractory additions (Ta, Nb, Ti-6Al-4V) through independently controlled multi-hopper DED, reducing screening timelines from months to days (pegues2021exploringadditivemanufacturing pages 1-5).

Chen et al. (2020) provide a comprehensive review of elemental powder blending for laser powder DED, establishing that in-situ mixing enables both cost reduction (versus pre-alloyed powders) and compositional flexibility, while identifying composition control as the primary technical hurdle (chen2020areviewon pages 1-3). Bresnahan and Poerschke (2024) demonstrate a complementary bulk approach using composition-gradient spark plasma sintering (CAPAD/SPS) of MoNbTaWHfₓ, requiring precise discrete-layer powder loading—a process currently manual and labor-intensive, underscoring the need for automated dosing (bresnahan2024highthroughputmultiprincipalelement pages 3-6, bresnahan2024highthroughputmultiprincipalelement pages 11-13, bresnahan2024highthroughputmultiprincipalelement pages 1-2). Liu et al. (2024) review the state of high-throughput AM preparation comprehensively, reporting that gradient powder spreading methods can achieve composition control precision better than 0.1 wt.%/mm—a benchmark target for any autonomous dosing system (liu2024high‐throughputpreparationfor pages 13-13).

### (c) Self-Driving Laboratories and Autonomous Experimentation

The self-driving laboratory (SDL) community has identified automated solid/powder dispensing as one of the most persistent hardware bottlenecks. Tom et al. (2024), in their landmark Chemical Reviews article, explicitly note that quantitative dispensing of powders is far more challenging than liquid handling, because real-time mass measurement is complex and costly; many SDLs resort to dissolving solids into stock solutions as a workaround (tom2024selfdrivinglaboratoriesfor pages 4-5). Seifrid et al. (2022) reinforce this, noting that dispensing below ~20 mg is especially unreliable and that the wide variation in powder properties makes universal calibration impractical for discovery-mode workflows (seifrid2022autonomouschemicalexperiments pages 8-9).

Emerging open-hardware solutions are beginning to address this gap. Pelkie et al. (2025) describe a workshop demonstrating 14 user-developed automation tools, among which a ~$300 powder dispensing module uses a stepper-motor-driven precision auger with an integrated balance for closed-loop gravimetric feedback, dispensing into a disposable syringe body. The design prioritizes modularity—interchangeable dispensing heads avoid cross-contamination—and repeatability over absolute single-shot accuracy, leveraging iterative optimization to compensate for dosing variability (pelkie2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 3-6, pelkie2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 1-3). Hernández-del-Valle et al. (2024) report a complementary approach with their open-hardware "dispensomixer" for nanocomposite pellets and powders, which uses 3D-printed rotors with specific geometries (semicircular holes, ramps, fillets) rather than augers to reduce clogging, paired with firmware-driven shaking routines and external balance monitoring. Their DIY approach achieved approximately 94% cost savings compared to commercial dispensers (hernandezdelvalle2024pelletdispensomixerand pages 2-3, hernandezdelvalle2024pelletdispensomixerand pages 1-2). Lo et al. (2024) review low-cost SDL architectures more broadly, noting that 3D-printed hardware, Arduino microcontrollers, and RepRap-derived platforms provide accessible building blocks, though they acknowledge significant design and testing overhead remains (lo2024reviewoflowcost pages 10-11).

### (d) Powder Flowability and Dosing Accuracy Characterization

Reliable dosing of metal AM powders—which span from free-flowing spherical gas-atomized particles to cohesive, irregular, fine powders—requires understanding the relationship between powder properties and feeding performance. Fathollahi et al. (2020) evaluate a high-precision volumetric micro-feeder for low-dose powders and introduce the "displacement-feed factor" concept, which characterizes the dynamic changes in powder bulk density during the feeding stroke. They find that fine, compressible powders densify during feeding while coarser particles exhibit both densification and elastic expansion, directly impacting mass-flow accuracy (fathollahi2020performanceevaluationof pages 1-2). This displacement-feed factor methodology offers a practical calibration strategy adaptable to an open-hardware doser.

Blackshields and Crean (2018) review continuous powder feeding for pharmaceutical manufacture, establishing that loss-in-weight (LIW) gravimetric screw feeders are the gold standard, but that cohesive, low-density materials cause bridging, rat-holing, and electrostatic adhesion. They recommend concave "self-cleaning" screws, mechanical agitators, static eliminators, and microfeeders for problematic powders, and report that feeding RSDs can be reduced from ~12.5% to ~8% with static elimination at low feed rates (blackshields2018continuouspowderfeeding pages 5-6, blackshields2018continuouspowderfeeding pages 1-2, blackshields2018continuouspowderfeeding pages 6-7, blackshields2018continuouspowderfeeding pages 2-4, blackshields2018continuouspowderfeeding pages 7-8). Bostijn et al. (2019) extend this by developing multivariate models that predict volumetric and gravimetric feeding behavior from raw material properties, finding that volumetric responses (maximum feed factor, feed factor decay) are more predictable from powder characteristics than gravimetric responses, because gravimetric control actively compensates for material variability (bostijn2019amultivariateapproach pages 1-6).

## Synthesis and Design Implications

Taken together, these 15 references illuminate the design space for a low-cost open-hardware multi-powder doser for autonomous AM alloy discovery:

1. **Multi-channel architecture**: Independent control of multiple powder streams is essential for compositional grading, as demonstrated by DED-based combinatorial workflows (pegues2021exploringadditivemanufacturing pages 1-5, chen2020areviewon pages 1-3).

2. **Closed-loop gravimetric feedback**: Loss-in-weight or balance-integrated dosing significantly improves accuracy for powders with variable bulk density (fathollahi2020performanceevaluationof pages 1-2, blackshields2018continuouspowderfeeding pages 2-4, pelkie2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 3-6).

3. **Powder-specific hardware adaptations**: Irregular, cohesive, and fine powders (common in refractory and HEA feedstocks) require anti-clogging features such as vibration, rotor geometries, self-cleaning screws, and static eliminators (ermurat2025developmentofelectronically pages 10-12, hernandezdelvalle2024pelletdispensomixerand pages 2-3, blackshields2018continuouspowderfeeding pages 5-6).

4. **Composition control targets**: State-of-the-art gradient methods achieve ~0.1 wt.%/mm precision (liu2024high‐throughputpreparationfor pages 13-13), providing a benchmark; however, for many combinatorial screening applications, coarser step-wise gradients with ~3–10% accuracy are sufficient for initial materials exploration.

5. **Cost and accessibility**: Open-hardware approaches at ~$300 per dispensing module, using stepper motors, 3D-printed components, and Arduino/Raspberry Pi control, are feasible and being actively developed by the SDL community (pelkie2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 3-6, hernandezdelvalle2024pelletdispensomixerand pages 2-3, pelkie2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 1-3).
