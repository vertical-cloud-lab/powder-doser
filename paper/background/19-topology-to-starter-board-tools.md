# Bridging the Topology+BOM → Starter Board Gap for Autonomous PCB Routers: A 2025 Survey

## 1. Introduction: The Intermediate Step Problem

Autonomous placement and routing engines such as Quilter and DeepPCB/InstaDeep require, as their minimum input, a complete PCB project containing a captured schematic with a netlist, real manufacturer parts with assigned physical footprints, a board outline, and design rules. They cannot start from a bare circuit topology or block diagram. This survey examines the tools, services, and research methods that attempt to bridge this "topology+BOM → starter board" gap as of 2025, covering commercial platforms (Section 2), design-as-code frameworks (Section 3), recent research on automated placement and LLM-driven schematic generation (Section 4), and an honest gap assessment for a representative mixed-signal board (Section 5).

### 2.1 CELUS Design Platform (CUBOs, Design Assistant)

CELUS operates primarily as a requirements-to-reference-design accelerator, mapping high-level functional requirements to pre-validated solution blocks ("CUBOs") drawn from its component library. Generative AI is applied to reference-design processing for embedded systems, where the platform classifies schematic elements and assembles designs from vetted building blocks (krishnamoorthy2025leveraginggenerativeai pages 3-4, krishnamoorthy2025leveraginggenerativeai pages 1-2). The approach leverages iterative LLM prompting and maps high-level functions to vendor-preferred BOMs using modular reuse (krishnamoorthy2025leveraginggenerativeai pages 3-4). CELUS is strongest in the Altium ecosystem and does not prominently feature headless KiCad export or a public CLI. Pricing is enterprise SaaS. For the topology→starter-board gap, CELUS is most useful when the design maps to its known reference designs; it is weaker for novel or highly custom topologies.

### 2.2 Flux.ai

Flux.ai provides a browser-native ECAD environment with AI copilot features for schematic and PCB editing. It is best characterized as a collaborative design tool with AI assistance rather than a topology-to-board compiler. It does not prominently offer headless/CLI access or native KiCad round-trip export, making it less suitable for a version-controlled CI workflow.

### 2.3 JITX

JITX is among the most mature programmatic PCB design systems, using a Stanza-based hardware description language where generators can elaborate high-level parametric specifications into concrete circuits with selected parts and footprints. Related academic work on Polymorphic Blocks demonstrates how HDL-based systems can support block-level polymorphism (abstract-typed blocks refined into concrete implementations), electronics-model constraint propagation, and netlist export to layout tools (lin2020polymorphicblocksunifying pages 1-2, ramesh2023betterembeddeddesign pages 45-47, ramesh2023betterembeddeddesign pages 42-45). JITX is strongly scriptable and code-first, making it one of the most credible options for automated starter-board generation. However, it is commercial/proprietary, and KiCad is not its primary export target.

### 2.4 Circuit Mind and Cofactr/Diadem

Circuit Mind provides AI-assisted architecture suggestions and schematic concepts but lacks strong public evidence for complete KiCad starter-project generation. Cofactr/Diadem is supply-chain and BOM-intelligence focused—valuable for grounding component selection in availability and lifecycle data but not itself a schematic-capture or floorplanning tool.

### 2.5 Quilter and DeepPCB/InstaDeep: Input Requirements

HiePlace benchmarks explicitly compare against Quilter on academic PCB placement tasks, demonstrating that Quilter operates as a downstream placement/routing engine that consumes an already-prepared board design with components, footprints, nets, and board outline (li2025hieplaceefficienthierarchical pages 9-11, li2025hieplaceefficienthierarchical pages 3-4). Quilter achieved placement costs ranging from 19.46×10³ to 35.70×10³ on four academic benchmarks, with runtimes of 34–75 minutes, while HiePlace reduced costs by 76.7%–91.3% with 99.4%–99.9% runtime reduction (li2025hieplaceefficienthierarchical pages 9-11). DeepPCB/InstaDeep similarly requires board-level physical-design inputs. Neither tool fills the topology→starter-board gap; they are explicitly consumers of the output that this gap analysis seeks to produce.

### 3.1 SKiDL (Python)

SKiDL is the most widely adopted open-source code-first schematic/netlist layer for KiCad automation. It serves as the backend representation for multiple LLM-driven systems: PCBSchemaGen generates executable SKiDL code from natural-language specifications and verifies it against a datasheet-driven knowledge graph (zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 3-4, zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 2-3, zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 1-2), while SchGen and HWE-Bench also reference SKiDL workflows (luo2605schgenpcbschematic pages 8-9, zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 2-3). SKiDL generates netlists that can produce KiCad schematics, supports real parts and footprint assignment, and is fully scriptable/CI-ready. It does not, however, provide board-outline definition or automatic placement.

### 3.2 BHDL (Racket eDSL)

BHDL is a declarative, Racket-embedded DSL that uniquely combines schematic capture with automated placement. It models every circuit element as a function, supports hierarchical composition via wire/layout clauses, and includes an auto-placement engine consisting of a global analytical placer (based on RePlace, minimizing HPWL) and a simulated-annealing detailed placer (li2021bhdlalucid pages 1-2, li2021bhdlalucid pages 5-6, li2021bhdlalucid pages 3-5). BHDL exports to KiCad PCB format (exportable to Gerber), produces a BOM, and supports KiCad-format footprint libraries by default (li2021bhdlalucid pages 5-6). The auto-placer is implemented in Julia for GPU acceleration (li2021bhdlalucid pages 5-6). This makes BHDL the strongest research demonstration of a code→KiCad PCB + auto-placed layout pipeline, though it remains a research prototype rather than a production tool.

### 3.3 Polymorphic Blocks / EDG

Polymorphic Blocks extends hierarchical block diagrams with type polymorphism and electronics modeling. Users write Python HDL code that builds a hierarchical tree model; a compiler performs tree-walk lowering, applies refinements, invokes generators, and updates a constraint graph (ramesh2023betterembeddeddesign pages 52-56, ramesh2023betterembeddeddesign pages 50-52). The system can export netlists with footprint annotations for import into KiCad (lin2020polymorphicblocksunifying pages 1-2, ramesh2023betterembeddeddesign pages 42-45). It does not directly generate board outlines or perform automatic placement, leaving those steps downstream (lin2020polymorphicblocksunifying pages 2-2).

### 3.4 atopile

atopile is a fast-moving open-source hardware description language strongly oriented to Git/version-control and CI workflows. It generates netlists, schematic/PCB project artifacts, and BOMs via libraries, with KiCad as its primary target. Board outline and floorplan support exists but remains limited compared to schematic/netlist generation. atopile is one of the most promising tools for headless starter-board generation in a coding-agent workflow.

### 3.5 tscircuit (TypeScript/JavaScript)

tscircuit provides software-engineer-friendly programmatic circuit and PCB descriptions in TypeScript. It can emit schematic/PCB data structures, footprints, and web-native rendered artifacts. KiCad interoperability is improving but not yet canonical. It is highly scriptable and CI-friendly but still earlier-stage for production mixed-signal boards.

### 3.6 KiCad Python/IPC API + kicad-cli

KiCad's native Python API and CLI (kicad-cli) provide the production-grade backend for headless automation: DRC, ERC, plotting, export, library queries, and PCB manipulation. They do not constitute a high-level design language but are the essential execution layer for any code-first pipeline targeting KiCad output.

### 4.1 RL/ML-Based Placement and Floorplanning

**HiePlace** (Li et al., 2025, IEEE TCAD) is the current state of the art in PCB-specific placement. It uses hierarchical mathematical programming with dynamic programming for first-priority components and a relaxation algorithm to reduce Boolean variables. On 19 PCB benchmarks (15 industrial, 4 academic), it achieves an average 66% cost reduction and 7.7× speedup versus baselines. Versus Quilter specifically, cost reductions of 76.7%–91.3% are reported with 99.4%–99.9% runtime reduction (li2025hieplaceefficienthierarchical pages 11-12, li2025hieplaceefficienthierarchical pages 9-11, li2025hieplaceefficienthierarchical pages 3-4, li2025hieplaceefficienthierarchical pages 1-2).

**Vassallo & Bajada** (DATE 2024) demonstrate end-to-end RL for PCB placement using TD3 and SAC agents, achieving 17% and 21% average post-routing wirelength reductions versus simulated annealing on unseen circuits, with convergence sometimes exceeding an order of magnitude faster (vassallo2024learningcircuitplacement pages 1-2).

**Vashisht et al.** (2020) combine cyclic RL with simulated annealing using sequence-pair representations, showing that RL-initialized SA consistently outperforms random initialization on ami49 and lattice benchmarks (vashisht2020placementinintegrated pages 5-7).

**Wang et al.** (2024) apply DRL with graph neural networks to chip floorplanning on ISPD 2005 benchmarks, achieving a 31.4% average HPWL reduction and 34.2% timing-violation reduction versus baselines including DREAMPlace (wang2024chipfloorplanningoptimization pages 7-12, wang2024chipfloorplanningoptimization pages 1-4, wang2024chipfloorplanningoptimization pages 12-15).

**Li et al.** (2025, PA-ICM) address double-layer PCB module placement with mixed-variable optimization, achieving automatic placement in minutes on industrial cases (li2502adoublelayerplacement pages 1-2, li2502adoublelayerplacement pages 7-8).

**Basso** (2026) combines R-GCN encoders with masked PPO for analog circuit floorplanning, reporting 67.3% layout-time reduction and 8.3% mean area reduction versus manual design on six industrial circuits (basso2026automatingthelayout pages 76-79, basso2026automatingthelayout pages 71-76).

### 4.2 LLM-Driven Schematic and Netlist Generation

**pcbGPT** (King et al., 2026) is a tool-augmented LLM synthesis agent that converts natural-language requirements into editable KiCad project drafts. It uses a custom Python circuit DSL, grounds generation in local KiCad libraries and datasheet-derived rules, and employs a generate–execute–repair validation loop. The best model achieves pass@1 of 0.90 and pass@5 of 1.00 on its benchmark. Critically, the authors explicitly state it "does not automate placement, routing, manufacturing preparation" (king2606pcbgptautomaticpcb pages 4-6, king2606pcbgptautomaticpcb pages 8-10, king2606pcbgptautomaticpcb pages 1-2, king2606pcbgptautomaticpcb pages 22-24).

**PCBSchemaGen** (Zou et al., 2025) is a training-free framework that uses LLMs to propose SKiDL code, verified by a datasheet-driven knowledge graph and subgraph-isomorphism checker. It compresses datasheet context from ~16k to ~300 tokens per component and covers 23 benchmark tasks across digital, analog, and power domains, claiming significant accuracy improvements (zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 3-4, zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 2-3, zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 1-2).

**SchGen** (Luo et al., 2025) fine-tunes a 20B-parameter LLM using a semantic-grounded code representation (Code-L1) to generate editable KiCad schematics. It achieves 82% valid circuit rate and 60.5% expert functional correctness, outperforming raw KiCad baselines (32% valid) and matching larger frontier models despite its smaller size (luo2605schgenpcbschematic pages 8-9, luo2605schgenpcbschematic pages 1-3, luo2605schgenpcbschematic pages 3-4).

### 4.3 Benchmarks Revealing the Gap

**HWE-Bench** (Qiu et al., 2025) is the most rigorous execution-driven benchmark for LLM board-level schematic design. Using 300 tasks across 8 domains with 2,914 IC datasheets, it applies static rule checking plus LTspice dynamic simulation. The best-performing model (Claude Sonnet 4.5) achieves only 8.15% end-to-end correct-design rate, with static pass rates around 71.84% and dynamic simulation pass rates around 58.65% (qiu2603hwebenchcanlanguage pages 5-6, qiu2603hwebenchcanlanguage pages 6-8, qiu2603hwebenchcanlanguage pages 1-2).

**PCB-Bench** (Li et al., 2026) provides a multimodal benchmark with ~3,700 text questions, ~500 image+text questions, and 170+ real PCB designs. Results show current LLMs struggle with spatial placement reasoning, rule adherence, and engineering artifacts, with no single model dominating all dimensions (li2026pcbbenchbenchmarkingllms pages 2-4, li2026pcbbenchbenchmarkingllms pages 8-11, li2026pcbbenchbenchmarkingllms pages 1-2).

**AMSnet-KG** (Shi et al., 2025) addresses the analog/mixed-signal domain with a knowledge-graph dataset of 894 circuits enabling LLM-based RAG for automated topology selection and Bayesian sizing. Case studies on op-amps and comparators demonstrate end-to-end design from specifications to sized netlists, with local annotations materially improving optimization convergence (shi2025amsnetkganetlist pages 1-2, shi2025amsnetkganetlist pages 9-11, shi2025amsnetkganetlist pages 17-19).

## 5. Gap Assessment: Mixed-Signal Motor + Sensor Control Board

For a concrete low-cost, open-hardware board with an MCU driving stepper drivers, solenoids, servos, vibration motors, and an HX711/load-cell front end:

### 5.1 What Is Automatable Today

**Schematic capture** of well-documented subcircuits (MCU breakout, standard voltage regulators, connectors) can be partially automated via SKiDL scripts, pcbGPT, or PCBSchemaGen. pcbGPT achieves 0.90 pass@1 on schematic drafts, making it the strongest NL→KiCad schematic generator (king2606pcbgptautomaticpcb pages 1-2, king2606pcbgptautomaticpcb pages 22-24). PCBSchemaGen's KG-verified SKiDL pipeline adds datasheet-grounded constraint checking (zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 3-4, zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 2-3).

**Component selection** for standard parts (resistors, capacitors, common ICs) can be partially grounded via Cofactr/Diadem supply-chain intelligence or PCBSchemaGen's knowledge graph. Krishnamoorthy (2025) recommends mapping high-level functions to vendor-preferred BOMs and using iterative LLM prompting with temperature control to reduce generation errors (krishnamoorthy2025leveraginggenerativeai pages 3-4, krishnamoorthy2025leveraginggenerativeai pages 1-2).

**Netlist and BOM generation** from code is mature via SKiDL or atopile pipelines feeding KiCad's CLI.

### 5.2 What Still Requires a Human

**Power-tree design and protection circuits** for motor drivers (high-current switching, flyback diodes, snubber networks for solenoids) require domain expertise that current LLMs handle poorly—HWE-Bench's 8.15% end-to-end correctness rate underscores this (qiu2603hwebenchcanlanguage pages 5-6, qiu2603hwebenchcanlanguage pages 1-2).

**Analog front-end design** (HX711 + load cell, precision reference, EMI filtering) is particularly challenging. AMSnet-KG demonstrates that even for simpler analog circuits like op-amps, LLM-generated topologies sometimes fail to meet target specifications and require topology regeneration (shi2025amsnetkganetlist pages 13-15).

**Board outline and mechanical integration** (connector placement, mounting holes, enclosure constraints) are not automated by any current tool. This is a fundamental gap: no system generates a board outline from functional requirements.

**Floorplanning and placement regions** (separating power-switching sections from sensitive analog sections, keeping stepper-driver heat sinks away from the HX711, managing ground-plane partitioning) require mixed-signal domain knowledge that none of the current AI systems reliably encode.

**Design-rule specification** (impedance-controlled traces for motor PWM, guard rings for the load-cell front end, current-carrying trace widths) must be manually defined.

### 5.3 Headless/CI-Compatible Tool Chain

For a version-controlled, coding-agent workflow with no interactive GUI:

- **Best current stack**: atopile or SKiDL (source language) → KiCad Python API + kicad-cli (execution/export) → manual or scripted board outline + design rules → Quilter or FreeRouting (downstream placement/routing).
- **Most promising research addition**: PCBSchemaGen (open-source, LLM→SKiDL→KiCad with verification) or pcbGPT (NL→KiCad project drafts) as a schematic-generation front end.
- **BHDL** is the only system demonstrated to produce auto-placed KiCad PCBs from code, but it remains a research prototype (li2021bhdlalucid pages 1-2, li2021bhdlalucid pages 5-6).
- **JITX** is the most mature commercial option for programmatic generation but is proprietary and not KiCad-native.

### 5.4 Realistic Assessment

For the described mixed-signal motor/sensor board, approximately 40–60% of the schematic capture could be accelerated by AI tools (standard subcircuits, MCU pin assignments, decoupling). The remaining 40–60% (power tree, analog front end, protection circuits, mixed-signal partitioning) requires human engineering review. Board outline, floorplan, and design rules remain essentially 100% manual. The complete "topology+BOM → KiCad starter board ready for Quilter" pipeline does not exist as a turnkey automated solution in 2025.

## 6. Conclusions and Recommendations

**Most credible gap-fillers for feeding Quilter/DeepPCB:**

1. **PCBSchemaGen** (open-source, training-free LLM→SKiDL→KiCad with KG+SI verification) is the most credible research system for producing verified KiCad schematics from natural-language specifications, with explicit grounding in real IC datasheets and pin constraints (zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 3-4, zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 2-3, zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 1-2).

2. **pcbGPT** achieves the highest reported schematic-synthesis accuracy (pass@1 = 0.90) and outputs native KiCad project files, but does not address placement/routing and remains a research prototype (king2606pcbgptautomaticpcb pages 1-2, king2606pcbgptautomaticpcb pages 22-24).

3. **SKiDL + KiCad CLI** is the most mature, scriptable, CI-ready stack for code-first KiCad project generation, though it requires the human (or an upstream AI) to author the circuit description (zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 3-4, zou2602pcbschemagenconstraintguidedschematic pages 2-3).

4. **atopile** is the most promising emerging open-source HDL for version-controlled, Git-native PCB generation with KiCad as primary target.

5. **BHDL** uniquely demonstrates auto-placement integrated with code-first KiCad PCB generation, making it the closest existing system to a complete code→placed-board pipeline (li2021bhdlalucid pages 1-2, li2021bhdlalucid pages 5-6).

6. **HiePlace** represents the state of the art in PCB-specific placement optimization and could serve as the placement stage between schematic generation and routing, though it is not publicly available as a packaged tool (li2025hieplaceefficienthierarchical pages 9-11, li2025hieplaceefficienthierarchical pages 3-4).

The fundamental bottleneck remains that no single system reliably converts a bare topology or block diagram into a complete, footprinted, outlined, design-rule-annotated KiCad project suitable for autonomous routing. The schematic/netlist generation layer is rapidly improving via LLM-based approaches, but board outline specification, mixed-signal floorplanning, and domain-specific design-rule encoding remain human-dependent. For a practical CI pipeline today, the recommended approach is to use a code-first framework (atopile or SKiDL) for the schematic/netlist layer, manually define the board outline and placement constraints, and then feed the resulting KiCad project to an autonomous router.
