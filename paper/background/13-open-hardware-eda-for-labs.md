## Electronics Design for Open-Source Scientific Hardware and Laboratory Automation: A Literature Review with Implications for a Multi-Channel Powder Dosing Device

### 1. Introduction

The design of low-cost, modular, microcontroller-driven control electronics for scientific instruments sits at the intersection of three rapidly evolving fields: open-source scientific hardware practice, modular motor/sensor electronics engineering, and emerging generative and automated electronic design automation (EDA). This review synthesizes 14 key references (2018–2025) spanning these domains, with explicit relevance to the design of manufacturable, easily replicated control PCBs for a multi-channel powder dosing device. The following sections are organized around the three requested topic areas, followed by a gap analysis of generative/automated EDA maturity.

### 2. Open-Hardware Electronics Design Practice and Reproducibility (Topic a)

The open scientific hardware community has developed a coherent set of design norms over the past decade, codified by projects, community roadmaps, and dedicated journals.

**Poseidon syringe pump system.** Booeshaghi et al. (2019) articulated seven design principles for open bioinstrumentation—functionality, robustness, safety, simplicity, modularity, benchmarking, and documentation—using the Poseidon open-source syringe pump as a worked example. The system uses an Arduino Uno with a CNC shield driving up to four stepper-motor pumps, paired with a Raspberry Pi and touchscreen for microscope control. The total cost is under $400 and assembly takes less than one hour. The authors emphasize that adoption depends on precise BOMs with vendor links, build videos, cross-platform executables, and firmware loaded via Arduino IDE (booeshaghi2019principlesofopen pages 2-3, booeshaghi2019principlesofopen pages 5-7, booeshaghi2019principlesofopen pages 1-2). Critically, the electronics architecture relies entirely on off-the-shelf boards rather than custom PCBs, using configurable microstepping jumpers on the CNC shield to set flow-rate ranges (booeshaghi2019designprinciplesfor pages 3-5, booeshaghi2019designprinciplesfor pages 5-7).

**HardOps for OpenFlexure.** Stirling et al. (2022) introduced HardOps, adapting software DevOps workflows—distributed version control, CI pipelines, issue tracking, and linked multi-repository management—to hardware design. The OpenFlexure microscope project maintains separate repositories for mechanical hardware, software, electronics, embedded OS, project website, and failure mode analysis, all integrated through GitLab CI pipelines that can automatically generate production files and track feedback from distributed manufacturing and testing (stirling2022hardopsutilisingthe pages 1-3, stirling2022hardopsutilisingthe pages 3-4, stirling2022hardopsutilisingthe pages 4-7). This approach is significant because it addresses process infrastructure rather than just device design, enabling reproducible manufacturing across multiple sites and countries.

**GOSH Roadmap.** The Global Open Science Hardware community roadmap (2018) defines open science hardware as hardware that can be "obtained, assembled, used, studied, modified, shared, and sold by anyone" and recommends recognized licenses (CERN OHL, TAPR OHL, Solderpad), documentation standards, contextual quality metrics, and support structures including shared fabrication facilities (nkoudou2018globalopenscience pages 1-12, nkoudou2018globalopenscience pages 29-34, nkoudou2018globalopenscience pages 19-25, nkoudou2018globalopenscience pages 25-29). The roadmap is governance-oriented rather than technical, but its norms directly shape how control PCBs should be documented and released.

**Open microscopy norms.** Hohlbein et al. (2022) synthesized best practices in a Nature Methods comment, stressing that open hardware should publish editable source files (not just STL or PDF exports), provide calibration references, and build plugin-oriented software interfaces for long-term device interoperability. They also emphasized that distributing hardware is fundamentally harder than distributing software because of non-zero replication cost and assembly complexity (hohlbein2022openmicroscopyin pages 12-16).

**Jubilee and Science-Jubilee.** Subbaraman et al. (2024) demonstrated the Duckbot, a plant biology automation system built on the Jubilee open-source tool-changing motion platform. Jubilee uses Duet3 controller boards running RepRap firmware, with a Raspberry Pi 4 for higher-level computation and notebook-driven experiment control. The system supports interchangeable 3D-printed and laser-cut tools (pipettes, cameras, syringes) attached via a tool-changing mechanism, with the Science-Jubilee Python package wrapping G-code control and exposing OpenTrons-compatible labware definitions (subbaraman2024theduckbota pages 4-5, subbaraman2024theduckbota pages 5-7, pelkie2025automationandautonomous pages 94-98, pelkie2025automationandautonomous pages 90-94). The base kit costs approximately $1,700 and can be assembled from off-the-shelf components.

**From DIY to global transformation.** Wenzel (2023) situated open hardware as a transition from ad hoc maker projects to globally distributed laboratory technology, emphasizing that reducing dependence on import logistics and proprietary supply chains is as important as headline cost reduction. Central drivers of adoption include open sharing, digital fabrication, standard parts, local production, and detailed documentation (hubbard2020opensourcedigitallyreplicable pages 1-3).

**Lessons from Wikimedia fellowships.** Keller et al. (2023) reflected on practical open-hardware development, noting recurring challenges in transitioning from "flying cable" prototypes to reproducible PCB-based builds. They described trade-offs between custom controller boards and reliance on poorly documented commodity modules, and stressed the need for early repository discipline, explicit module boundaries, and realistic attention to sourcing and proprietary dependencies (keller2023developmentandsharing pages 9-13).

### 3. Modular and Expandable Motor/Sensor Control Electronics (Topic b)

**Modular multi-axis stepper driver.** Fischer et al. (2025) described a low-cost (~$360) open-source multi-axis stepper controller for microscopy, built around a Raspberry Pi Pico 2 microcontroller and up to four Trinamic TMC5240 evaluation boards communicating via SPI. The system supports per-axis encoders and limit switches via DB-9 connectors, implements open-loop, open-loop with pull-in correction, and closed-loop control modes, and includes a separate remote controller with joystick and rotary encoder inputs linked via UART over RJ-45. Software includes C and Python libraries and a µManager driver. The authors explicitly note that they chose evaluation boards over custom PCBs to simplify construction, but propose future PCB integration to reduce soldering and cost (fischer2511modularmultiaxisstepper pages 13-15, fischer2511modularmultiaxisstepper pages 4-6, fischer2511modularmultiaxisstepper pages 1-4, fischer2511modularmultiaxisstepper pages 10-13, fischer2511modularmultiaxisstepper pages 18-20). This staged approach—module-based prototype first, custom PCB second—is a recurring pattern in open scientific hardware.

**Open-source lab-grade scales.** Hubbard and Pearce (2020) provided a thoroughly documented open-source digital scale using an Arduino Nano, HX711 load-cell amplifier, and 3D-printed enclosure. The design achieves 0.05 g repeatability (0.005 g with small-range load cells), supports serial logging compliant with the SMA SCP-0499 protocol, and can operate standalone with LCD or as a USB-connected logging instrument. Component placement uses entirely 3D-printed snap-fit features, minimizing fasteners (hubbard2020opensourcedigitallyreplicable pages 3-7, hubbard2020opensourcedigitallyreplicable pages 1-3). This is directly relevant to gravimetric feedback in multi-channel powder dosing.

**Pellet dispensomixer.** Hernández-del-Valle et al. (2024) presented the closest published analog to a multi-material powder dosing system: an eight-channel pellet dispensomixer using Arduino MEGA-controlled stepper motors with A4988 drivers, an external balance for mass-flow feedback, and iteratively designed 3D-printed rotors with anti-clogging features. The system costs approximately €250 and is intended for automated nanocomposite compounding and self-driving laboratory workflows. Control code communicates via serial with a host computer running Python notebooks that orchestrate dispensing and balance feedback (hernandezdelvalle2024pelletdispensomixerand pages 4-5, hernandezdelvalle2024pelletdispensomixerand pages 2-3, hernandezdelvalle2024pelletdispensomixerand pages 1-2).

**Open-source SECM.** Guver et al. (2019) demonstrated a scanning electrochemical microscope built under $300 from an open-source DStat potentiostat, Arduino-controlled stepper motors with driver shields, GRBL motion control software, and 3D-printed mechanics. The project exemplifies HardwareX publication norms of cost transparency, open source files, and clear buildability instructions (guver2019alowcostand pages 1-4).

### 4. Generative and Automated EDA for Scientific Instruments (Topic c)

**ML for EDA survey.** Huang et al. (2021) provided a comprehensive pre-LLM survey of machine learning applications in electronic design automation, covering estimation, optimization, verification, and other tasks across the IC design hierarchy. Critically, the entire survey focuses on VLSI/integrated-circuit flows; board-level PCB design for scientific instruments is not addressed (chen2024thedawnof pages 2-3).

**Large Circuit Models.** Chen et al. (2024) proposed AI-native EDA built around Large Circuit Models (LCMs)—multimodal foundation models that would jointly represent specification, RTL, netlist, floorplan, and layout information. While ambitious, the proposal is entirely framed around integrated-circuit design abstractions. There is no analogous effort for board-level scientific instrument PCBs (chen2024thedawnof pages 2-3, chen2024thedawnof pages 1-2).

**Generative AI for analog IC design.** Zadeh and Elamien (2025) surveyed the application of GNNs, VAEs, and LLMs to analog circuit sizing and topology exploration, highlighting tools such as AnalogCoder and AmpAgent. These approaches remain focused on IC-level design and do not address board-level instrument electronics (zadeh2025generativeaifor pages 12-13).

**SmartonAI for KiCad.** Han et al. (2023/2025) developed SmartonAI, an LLM-assisted interaction plugin for KiCad that uses retrieval-augmented generation and task decomposition to help users navigate documentation, invoke plugins, and execute design operations through natural language. This is the only published work directly touching open-source PCB tooling with AI assistance. However, SmartonAI assists with tool interaction rather than generating circuit designs: it lowers the skill barrier to using KiCad but does not solve component selection, circuit topology, EMI-aware placement, or manufacturable routing (han2023newinteractionparadigm pages 1-2).

### 5. Gap Analysis: How Mature Is Generative/Automated Electrical Design for Open Scientific Hardware?

The evidence reveals a stark gap between the ambitions of generative EDA research and the practical needs of open scientific hardware electronics designers. The key findings are:

1. **All published ML/AI EDA work targets VLSI/IC flows.** The surveys by Huang et al. (2021), Chen et al. (2024), Zadeh and Elamien (2025), and Xu et al. (2025) exclusively address chip-level design—standard cell placement, routing, parasitic extraction, and verification. Board-level PCB design for laboratory instruments is entirely absent from this literature (chen2024thedawnof pages 2-3, chen2024thedawnof pages 1-2, zadeh2025generativeaifor pages 12-13, xu2025largelanguagemodelsa pages 1-1).

2. **SmartonAI is the closest tool to open-hardware PCB automation, but it is an assistant, not a generator.** It helps users interact with KiCad through natural language but does not create schematics, select components, or produce layouts (han2023newinteractionparadigm pages 1-2).

3. **Open-hardware projects avoid custom PCB design by using off-the-shelf modules.** Poseidon uses Arduino + CNC shield (booeshaghi2019designprinciplesfor pages 3-5); Fischer et al. use TMC evaluation boards (fischer2511modularmultiaxisstepper pages 18-20); Hernández-del-Valle et al. use Arduino MEGA + A4988 driver modules (hernandezdelvalle2024pelletdispensomixerand pages 4-5); Hubbard and Pearce use Arduino Nano + HX711 breakout (hubbard2020opensourcedigitallyreplicable pages 3-7). This module-stacking approach is effective for prototyping but creates reproducibility and reliability issues at scale, as noted by Keller et al. (keller2023developmentandsharing pages 9-13).

4. **What remains entirely manual** for scientific hardware PCBs includes: requirements-to-schematic translation, component selection and sourcing, schematic capture, power-integrity and signal-integrity analysis, PCB layout and routing, design-rule checking, Gerber generation, and DFM review. The HardOps CI pipeline automates downstream steps (building CAD, generating documentation) but not the core electrical design (stirling2022hardopsutilisingthe pages 4-7).

5. **The transition from module prototype to integrated PCB is a known bottleneck.** Fischer et al. explicitly propose future PCB integration as a manufacturing improvement (fischer2511modularmultiaxisstepper pages 18-20), and Keller et al. describe the difficulty of moving from flying-wire prototypes to reliable, documented boards (keller2023developmentandsharing pages 9-13).

In summary, generative or automated electrical design for open scientific hardware is essentially nonexistent as a fielded capability. The AI/EDA community is making rapid progress on IC-level automation, but board-level instrument PCB design—the kind needed for a multi-channel powder doser—remains a manual engineering discipline. LLM-assisted tools like SmartonAI may reduce learning curves, but architecture, safety, analog front-end design, motor-power staging, connector strategy, and design-for-manufacture decisions require human judgment for the foreseeable future.

It is best understood as AI-assisted tooling, not generative electrical design. (han2023newinteractionparadigm pages 1-2) | Highly relevant to the gap analysis. For a multi-channel powder doser, SmartonAI-like tools may help a designer work faster in KiCad, but the architecture, safety, load-cell front-end design, motor-power staging, connectorization, and DFM decisions still need human engineering judgment. |

### 7. Implications for Multi-Channel Powder Doser PCB Design

Based on this literature, a manufacturable control PCB for a multi-channel powder dosing device should:

- **Adopt Poseidon-style design principles**: functionality, robustness, simplicity, modularity, benchmarking, and documentation (booeshaghi2019principlesofopen pages 2-3).
- **Use a Jubilee/Duckbot-inspired architecture**: stable controller backbone with swappable dosing channel modules and a Python API for host-side orchestration (subbaraman2024theduckbota pages 5-7, pelkie2025automationandautonomous pages 94-98).
- **Follow the Fischer et al. staged approach**: prototype with commodity modules (Arduino/Pico + TMC driver boards + HX711 breakouts), then consolidate onto an integrated PCB for manufacturing (fischer2511modularmultiaxisstepper pages 18-20).
- **Integrate gravimetric feedback** using the Hubbard & Pearce HX711 + load-cell architecture and serial logging protocol (hubbard2020opensourcedigitallyreplicable pages 3-7).
- **Reference the pellet dispensomixer** for multi-channel motor control, anti-jam routines, and balance integration patterns (hernandezdelvalle2024pelletdispensomixerand pages 4-5).
- **Apply HardOps CI workflows** for version-controlled schematics, automated BOM/Gerber generation, and traceable manufacturing revisions (stirling2022hardopsutilisingthe pages 4-7).
- **Release design files** per GOSH and OSHWA norms: editable KiCad sources, BOM with vendor links, assembly instructions, calibration procedures, and recognized open-hardware licenses (nkoudou2018globalopenscience pages 1-12, nkoudou2018globalopenscience pages 29-34, hohlbein2022openmicroscopyin pages 12-16).
- **Expect fully manual electrical design** for the PCB itself, with potential assistance from LLM-based tools for KiCad interaction but not for core engineering decisions (han2023newinteractionparadigm pages 1-2).
