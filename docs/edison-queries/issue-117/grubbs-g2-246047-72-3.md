## Answer

## Physical and Handling Properties of Grubbs Second-Generation Catalyst (CAS 246047-72-3) for Automated Milligram Powder Dispensing

### Introduction

The second-generation Grubbs catalyst (Grubbs G2), benzylidene[1,3-bis(2,4,6-trimethylphenyl)-2-imidazolidinylidene]dichloro(tricyclohexylphosphine)ruthenium (C₄₆H₆₅Cl₂N₂PRu, MW 848.97 g/mol), is a widely used olefin metathesis catalyst. While its catalytic properties are extensively documented, its bulk powder handling characteristics—critical for automated milligram-scale dispensing via auger-based dosers inside gloveboxes—are largely absent from the peer-reviewed literature. The following assessment integrates published catalyst stability data with general powder-dosing science to provide a practical guide for this application.

### Density

The true (crystallographic) density of Grubbs G2 can be estimated at approximately 1.36–1.40 g/cm³ from its known single-crystal X-ray structure. No published pycnometric true density or measured bulk/tapped density values were identified in the academic literature. For engineering purposes, the bulk density of a fine crystalline organometallic powder of this type is expected to be substantially lower than the true density—likely in the range of 0.3–0.5 g/cm³—though this is lot-dependent, as particle packing density is dramatically affected by particle size distribution, humidity, and electrostatic effects rather than just composition (yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 4-6).

### Particle Size Distribution and Morphology

No standardized particle size distribution (PSD) for commercial Grubbs G2 has been reported in the literature. The material is supplied as a crystalline powder, with crystal habit depending on the recrystallization solvent and post-processing. Particles may range broadly from approximately 10–500 µm depending on vendor and lot. The monoclinic crystal system can yield prismatic or needle-like habits. Lot-to-lot PSD variability is a key concern for automated dispensing, as small differences in particle size distribution can disproportionately impact packing efficiency and volumetric dosing accuracy (yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 4-6, hou2024developmentofa pages 112-115).

### Hygroscopicity

Grubbs G2 is not classically hygroscopic; it does not readily absorb atmospheric moisture onto the solid surface. However, this should not be confused with moisture insensitivity. Ruthenium-based metathesis catalysts can be deactivated by water, particularly in solution, even though the solid-state tolerance is considerably greater than that of early transition metal (Mo, W) metathesis catalysts, which require rigorously dried and degassed conditions (trnka2001thedevelopmentof pages 3-4, trnka2001thedevelopmentof pages 2-3).

### Air/Moisture Sensitivity and Decomposition in Ambient Atmosphere

Grubbs G2 represents a significant advance in air and moisture tolerance relative to early-metal catalysts. It is described as "air-stable as a solid" and maintains catalytic activity when briefly exposed to water, alcohols, and acids (trnka2001thedevelopmentof pages 3-4). The catalyst demonstrates increased tolerance of organic functionality, moisture, and oxygen compared to its predecessors (grubbs2013thegenerationof pages 4-6, kajetanowicz2021nitroandother pages 1-5). However, prolonged ambient exposure does cause degradation, evidenced by a visible color change from the characteristic pink/mauve/green color to gray or brown (fickert2013nanocapsulesforselfhealing pages 83-88). In self-healing polymer applications, encapsulation in wax microspheres or handling under glovebox conditions was found necessary to preserve catalytic activity for extended periods, confirming that while short-term solid-state air exposure is tolerated, long-term exposure is detrimental (wilson2010selfhealingpolymers pages 13-15, fickert2013nanocapsulesforselfhealing pages 83-88). All synthetic manipulations in research settings are typically carried out in N₂-filled gloveboxes with dry, oxygen-free solvents (lummiss2014amine‐mediateddegradationin pages 1-5).

### Thermal Stability

Grubbs G2 is thermally robust relative to other metathesis catalysts. In solution (C₆D₆ at 55 °C), the benzylidene form of the second-generation catalyst exhibits a half-life exceeding one month, compared to approximately 8 days for the first-generation analog under identical conditions (monfette2009equilibriumringclosingmetathesis. pages 13-14). The catalytically relevant methylidene intermediate, while less stable, still has a half-life of approximately 6 hours at 55 °C versus only ~40 minutes for the first-generation methylidene (monfette2009equilibriumringclosingmetathesis. pages 13-14). The mesityl substituents on the N-heterocyclic carbene (NHC) ligand provide considerable steric protection and contribute to the high thermal stability, and the NHC ligand itself forms a strong, substitutionally inert bond to the ruthenium center (trnka2001thedevelopmentof pages 7-9, grubbs2013thegenerationof pages 4-6). The first-generation Grubbs catalyst melts at 153 °C (kamphaus2008anewselfhealing pages 1-3, wilson2010selfhealingpolymers pages 13-15); the second-generation catalyst decomposes without a clean melting point at higher temperatures (~220 °C based on commercial supplier documentation). In practical terms, thermal stability is not a limiting factor for room-temperature glovebox dispensing operations.

### Electrostatic Charging Tendency

No direct electrostatic charge measurements for Grubbs G2 powder have been published. However, as a dry, electrically insulating organic/organometallic crystalline solid, it is expected to exhibit moderate-to-high tribocharging tendency. This concern is particularly acute inside a glovebox, where the inert atmosphere is typically very dry (<1 ppm H₂O), removing any surface moisture that would otherwise dissipate charge. Electrostatic forces, along with van der Waals forces, become increasingly dominant for particles smaller than ~10 µm and can significantly alter powder flow behavior, cause adhesion to equipment surfaces, and affect apparent bulk density (yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 3-4, hou2024developmentofa pages 64-67). Use of grounded, conductive metal contact surfaces and minimization of polymer components in the powder path is advisable.

### Powder Flowability and Cohesion

While no direct flowability measurements (e.g., Hausner ratio, flow function coefficient, or shear cell data) have been reported for Grubbs G2, its expected behavior can be assessed by analogy with similar fine crystalline powders. The powder-dosing literature demonstrates that cohesive materials—classified by Hausner ratios above ~1.35 or low flow function coefficients—present major challenges for micro-feeding applications (hou2024developmentofa pages 112-115). Fine crystalline organometallic powders are anticipated to fall in the cohesive regime (Geldart Class C/A), where interparticle forces (van der Waals, electrostatic) outweigh gravity, leading to bridging in hoppers, adhesion to auger flights, and high shot-to-shot variability (yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 3-4, hou2024developmentofa pages 101-106). At the 0.1–10 mg target dose range, published auger-based micro-dosing systems for cohesive pharmaceutical powders achieved relative standard deviations (RSD) from <2.3% at higher doses to >11% at the lowest doses, with performance strongly dependent on powder properties, stirrer design, drum bore size, and operating speed (mehta2025optimizationofcarrierbased pages 13-14).

### Practical Recommendations

For automated dispensing of Grubbs G2 at 0.1–10 mg quantities inside a glovebox:

1. **Gravimetric feedback** is strongly recommended rather than relying on purely volumetric auger dosing, given the anticipated cohesive behavior and lot-to-lot PSD variability (yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 4-6, yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 10-12).
2. **Incoming lot characterization** (PSD, bulk/tapped density, and visual morphology) should be performed for each new lot to establish dispensing parameters (hou2024developmentofa pages 112-115, hou2024developmentofa pages 106-108).
3. **Minimize air/moisture exposure** by keeping the hopper reservoir sealed during non-dispensing periods and establishing a maximum hold time for each lot based on visual inspection for color change (fickert2013nanocapsulesforselfhealing pages 83-88, trnka2001thedevelopmentof pages 3-4).
4. **Grounded conductive surfaces** throughout the powder path will reduce electrostatic adhesion, which is exacerbated in the dry glovebox atmosphere (yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 3-4, hou2024developmentofa pages 64-67).
5. **Vibratory or agitation assistance** at the hopper and auger inlet can help overcome bridging and ensure consistent powder delivery to the screw (hou2024developmentofa pages 101-106, hou2024developmentofa pages 112-115).

### Limitations of This Assessment

It is important to note that many of the physical powder properties of Grubbs G2 relevant to automated dispensing—bulk and tapped density, particle size distribution, Hausner ratio, flow function coefficient, electrostatic charge density, and specific surface area—have not been formally reported in the peer-reviewed literature or patent literature. The values and assessments provided here for these properties are engineering estimates based on the compound's molecular characteristics, crystal system, and analogy with similar fine crystalline solids, supplemented by published data from the powder-dosing and catalyst stability literature. Users should measure these properties for their specific commercial lots before qualifying a dispensing process.
