# Powder-Doser Material Decision Table

> This table summarizes likely material and handling risks for four powders in a printed auger microdoser, separating evidence-backed findings from engineering judgement where the retrieved corpus lacked direct citations.

| Powder | Key interaction risks with PLA | Static/handling risk drivers in dry atmosphere | Recommended contact-surface material/coating options | Cleaning/containment notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| AIBN | No direct evidence of dry solid-solid chemical reaction with PLA in retrieved corpus. Main PLA risk is generic ester-polymer hydrolysis/degradation if moisture is present or if parts were poorly dried; PLA is moisture-sensitive and ester bonds are cleaved by hydrolysis (pqac-00000004, pqac-00000005). Wear debris contamination from FDM surfaces: no direct citation in retrieved corpus. | Likely high static adhesion/clinging at mg scale in very dry glovebox; no direct triboelectric ranking for AIBN vs PLA in retrieved corpus. Smoothness and low charging likely matter more than bulk chemistry here: no direct citation in retrieved corpus. | Prefer parylene C over printed substrate when possible because it is chemically inert, moisture/gas impermeable, and used as a barrier on 3D-printed parts (pqac-00000003, pqac-00000014, pqac-00000015). If reprinting, favor nonpolar/low-surface-energy polymers such as PP over PLA/nylon: no direct citation in retrieved corpus. Avoid standard SLA resin as final powder-contact surface unless sealed/coated; resin leachables/barrier concerns supported indirectly by parylene-resin literature (pqac-00000003, pqac-00000014). | Treat as thermally sensitive energetic initiator; minimize friction, motor stall, and local heating: no direct citation in retrieved corpus. Prefer disposable or dedicated wetted path inserts and low-retention geometry: no direct citation in retrieved corpus. |
| Grubbs 2nd-gen catalyst | Key risk is not PLA ester groups per se, but catalyst exposure to water/protic contamination; Ru metathesis productivity drops in presence of water due to decomposition, and even relaxed drying can matter (pqac-00000007, pqac-00000008, pqac-00000010). PLA is moisture-sensitive and can retain/encounter moisture issues if not dried (pqac-00000004, pqac-00000005). Adsorption/poisoning by rough porous FDM surface: no direct citation in retrieved corpus. | In glovebox, dry conditions help catalyst stability but worsen powder charging; likely strong adhesion to rough insulating PLA and dose-to-dose memory from hold-up in layer lines: no direct citation in retrieved corpus. | Strongest supported option: parylene C-coated contact path over thoroughly dried substrate, because parylene is chemically inert and provides moisture/leachate barrier protection on printed parts (pqac-00000003, pqac-00000014, pqac-00000015). If uncoated polymer is needed, prefer smoother, less polar, low-uptake materials over PLA/nylon; PP likely preferable by engineering judgement, no direct citation in retrieved corpus. | Use dedicated inert-atmosphere-only parts; pre-dry printed parts thoroughly before glovebox entry because PLA degradation and moisture issues are documented (pqac-00000005). Minimize hold-up and dead zones to reduce expensive catalyst loss: no direct citation in retrieved corpus. |
| Sodium cyanide | No direct evidence of dry NaCN-PLA reaction in retrieved corpus. Generic concern is avoid any absorbent/porous, hard-to-clean polymer because PLA degrades with moisture and has rough FDM surfaces that may trap toxic residues (pqac-00000004, pqac-00000005). | Fine toxic salt powder can statically adhere to insulating rough walls in dry air, increasing contamination and decon burden: no direct citation in retrieved corpus. | Prefer chemically inert barrier surface such as parylene C if using printed substrate, supported by its chemical inertness and barrier function (pqac-00000003, pqac-00000014). If reprinting, prefer nonpolar, smoother, easier-to-clean polymers (e.g., PP) over PLA; no direct citation in retrieved corpus. Avoid porous standard SLA without barrier coating; parylene is specifically used to block leachates and ingress in 3D-printed resins (pqac-00000003, pqac-00000014). | Highest priority is containment and cleanability. Use dedicated NaCN-only wetted parts, secondary containment, validated decontamination workflow, and avoid designs with crevices/threads in powder path: no direct citation in retrieved corpus. Humidity control matters operationally, but no direct citation in retrieved corpus. |
| Sodium chloride | Chemically low concern for dry contact; main issue with PLA remains generic moisture sensitivity and potential wear debris contamination rather than reaction with NaCl (pqac-00000004, pqac-00000005; wear debris: no direct citation in retrieved corpus). Good benchmark powder for mechanical testing. | Useful benign probe for static/clumping behavior of design. In very dry atmosphere, still may cling to insulating rough PLA surfaces; no direct citation in retrieved corpus. | Acceptable for early benchtop debugging on PLA, but for transferable performance data prefer same final surface class intended for hazardous powders, ideally parylene C-coated or low-energy polymer surface (pqac-00000003, pqac-00000014, pqac-00000015; low-energy polymer preference: no direct citation in retrieved corpus). | Use as test powder to quantify retention, carryover, and dose drift before introducing hazardous powders: no direct citation in retrieved corpus. Cleaning burden is low, so it is suitable for screening geometry/coating choices. |
