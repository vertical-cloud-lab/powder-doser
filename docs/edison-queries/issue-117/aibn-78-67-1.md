## Answer

## Physical and Handling Properties of Azobisisobutyronitrile (AIBN, CAS 78-67-1) Relevant to Automated Milligram-Scale Powder Dispensing

### Overview

Azobisisobutyronitrile (AIBN; 2,2′-azobis(2-methylpropionitrile); C₈H₁₂N₄; MW 164.21 g/mol) is a white crystalline organic solid widely used as a radical initiator in polymerization and as a chemical blowing agent in foam manufacturing. Its status as a self-reactive substance (UN Class 4.1) makes it an unusual candidate for automated powder dispensing, and a thorough understanding of its powder properties, thermal stability, and mechanical sensitivity is essential before deploying it in an auger-based dosing system.

It should be noted that extensive searching of the primary scientific literature yielded no publications specifically characterizing AIBN as a bulk powder for dispensing purposes (e.g., flowability indices, shear cell data, or Hausner ratios). The properties described below are compiled from established safety reference data, safety data sheets, the thermal hazards literature (which uses AIBN as a benchmark self-reactive substance), and general principles from the powder dispensing and micro-feeder literature.

### 1. Density Properties

AIBN has a true (crystal) density of approximately 1.10–1.13 g/cm³, consistent with a low-molecular-weight organic nitrile crystalline solid. No published bulk or tapped density data specific to AIBN were found; however, based on general correlations for crystalline organic powders with irregular morphology and particle sizes in the tens-to-hundreds of micrometers range, bulk density is estimated at roughly 0.45–0.65 g/cm³ and tapped density at approximately 0.55–0.75 g/cm³. These values imply a moderate Hausner ratio (~1.2–1.3), suggesting fair-to-passable flowability for coarser grades but potentially poor flowability for fine grades. For auger-based dosing of milligram quantities, the actual bulk density and its variability between lots directly affect volumetric dose accuracy and should be measured on the specific lot being dispensed (hou2024developmentofa pages 106-108).

### 2. Particle Size Distribution and Crystal Morphology

Commercial AIBN is supplied as a white crystalline powder with particle sizes generally spanning tens to several hundreds of micrometers (approximately 50–500 µm, grade-dependent). AIBN crystallizes in a monoclinic crystal system, and commercial particles are typically irregular/angular rather than spherical. Studies on AIBN crystallization from methanol have demonstrated secondary nucleation phenomena, confirming the potential for a broad particle size distribution depending on the crystallization process. Angular morphology is known to reduce flowability relative to spherical particles, increase interparticle friction, and promote bridging in narrow orifices — all significant concerns for miniaturized auger dosers.

### 3. Hygroscopicity

AIBN is practically insoluble in water (<2 g/L at 20 °C) and is generally classified as non-hygroscopic. Under standard ambient conditions, moisture uptake is not expected to be a primary concern for powder handling. However, even non-hygroscopic powders can develop surface moisture films in humid environments (>60% RH), which can form capillary liquid bridges and significantly increase interparticle cohesion (hou2024developmentofa pages 64-67). Storage and dispensing in humidity-controlled environments (e.g., <50% RH) is recommended to maintain consistent flow behavior.

### 4. Electrostatic Charging Tendency

As an organic, electrically insulating crystalline powder, AIBN is expected to exhibit moderate triboelectric charging during handling operations such as hopper filling, vibratory agitation, and auger rotation. Triboelectrification — electron transfer during contact between dissimilar materials — is a well-documented phenomenon in organic powder handling and arises during sieving, mixing, and conveying (yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 2-3). Electrostatic charge can cause powder to adhere to equipment surfaces (auger flights, hopper walls, dispensing nozzles), increase dose variability, and hinder gravity-assisted flow. Mitigation strategies include ionization bars, conductive or anti-static equipment surfaces, and humidity control. No AIBN-specific charge density measurements were found in the literature.

### 5. Powder Flowability and Cohesion

While no AIBN-specific flow function coefficient (ffc), angle of repose, or shear cell data were identified, general principles from the pharmaceutical micro-feeder literature indicate that fine organic crystalline powders with irregular morphology and particle sizes below ~100 µm are dominated by cohesive forces (van der Waals, electrostatic) and exhibit poor flowability (hou2024developmentofa pages 64-67, hou2024developmentofa pages 112-115). For coarser AIBN grades (d₅₀ > 200 µm), flowability is expected to be fair, but fines present in the lot distribution can disproportionately degrade flow.

In auger-based systems, cohesive powders present several well-documented challenges: bridging and ratholing in the hopper, inconsistent volumetric fill per screw revolution, adherence to screw flights, and increased tendency to clog as augers are miniaturized (yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 9-10). Auger overrun — continued powder delivery after the screw stops — is also exacerbated by cohesive or compressible powders (yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 9-10). For feed rates below 50 g/h (well above what is needed for 0.1–10 mg doses), literature shows that cohesive powders cause high flow rate variability and reduced feeder performance (hou2024developmentofa pages 101-106). Agitator-assisted screw feeders and inclined screw designs have been developed specifically to address ratholing and arching in cohesive pharmaceutical powders (hou2024developmentofa pages 1-5).

### 6. Thermal Stability and Decomposition

AIBN is extensively studied as a benchmark self-reactive substance in thermal hazards calorimetry. Key thermal parameters include:

- **DSC decomposition onset**: approximately 100–103 °C at typical heating rates (2–10 °C/min). The decomposition is strongly exothermic and generates nitrogen gas plus organic radical-derived products (primarily tetramethylsuccinonitrile).
- **Heat of decomposition**: approximately 1050–1400 J/g, depending on polymorph and measurement conditions. This is a significant energy release for an organic powder.
- **Self-Accelerating Decomposition Temperature (SADT)**: approximately 50–55 °C (commonly cited as ~52 °C) for a 25 kg package. The SADT is package-size dependent; for milligram quantities, the thermal inertia is negligible and self-heating is not a practical concern at room temperature. However, the low SADT underscores the narrow thermal safety margin — any localized heating must be avoided.
- **Polymorphism**: AIBN exists in at least two polymorphic forms. A low-temperature polymorph has been identified with even lower thermal stability than the standard commercial form, which is a relevant consideration for lot-to-lot variability.
- **Melting point**: reported as 103–105 °C with decomposition — i.e., AIBN does not melt cleanly but decomposes near its melting point.

The relatively low decomposition onset and high exothermicity mean that any sustained frictional heating from an auger stall, seized bearing, or compacted powder bed could in principle raise local temperatures toward the decomposition threshold. For milligram quantities, however, the thermal mass is extremely small, and heat dissipation to the equipment body would dominate over any significant temperature rise from shear alone.

### 7. Friction and Impact Sensitivity

AIBN is **not** classified as a primary explosive and is not especially sensitive to impact or friction by standard BAM test criteria (impact sensitivity typically reported >40 J; friction sensitivity >360 N). This places it well below the sensitivity thresholds of primary explosives or even many secondary explosives. The classification as UN Class 4.1 Self-Reactive Substance, Type D, reflects its self-decomposition hazard rather than shock or friction sensitivity (raines2023analternativemethodology pages 1-3). Self-reactive substances are classified according to a sequence of tests addressing detonation propagation, deflagration, and effect of heating under confinement; Type D allows transport in packages up to 50 kg (raines2023analternativemethodology pages 1-3).

Azo/diazo compounds are recognized as a class of organic explosive compounds (zapata2021chemicalclassificationof pages 7-11), and AIBN dust can form explosive dust-air mixtures. Dust explosion parameters (Kst, Pmax, minimum ignition energy, minimum explosible concentration) for AIBN are not widely published in the open literature, but energetic organic dusts with particle sizes below 100 µm generally present significant dust explosion risks (khudhur2021mechanismsseverityand pages 1-3). For milligram-scale dispensing, the quantity of dust generated is negligibly small relative to minimum explosible concentrations, but accumulated dust from repeated dispensing cycles should be managed.

### 8. Practical Implications for Auger-Based Milligram Dispensing

For automated dispensing of 0.1–10 mg quantities of AIBN with an auger-based doser, the following considerations are paramount:

1. **Dose accuracy and precision**: Volumetric dosing via auger is sensitive to bulk density variations, powder compressibility, and cohesive bridging. For cohesive organic powders, gravimetric feedback or verification weighing is recommended to compensate for packing density changes (yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 4-6). Auger systems can achieve dosing rates below 1 mg/s, but show increased clogging tendency as they are miniaturized (yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 9-10).

2. **Hopper and feed design**: Anti-arching measures (agitators, vibrators, inclined screws) are advisable to prevent bridging and ratholing (hou2024developmentofa pages 1-5, hou2024developmentofa pages 44-47). Hopper geometry and the pressure balance at the outlet affect feed consistency (hou2024developmentofa pages 64-67).

3. **Electrostatic management**: Ionization, grounded conductive surfaces, and controlled humidity will help reduce wall adhesion and dose scatter.

4. **Thermal safety**: While the thermal hazard is dominated by the small mass involved (milligrams), the equipment should avoid dead zones where powder could accumulate and stagnate at elevated ambient temperatures. Temperature monitoring is prudent. Auger shear forces at milligram-scale throughput are unlikely to generate meaningful frictional heating, but stalled or jammed mechanisms should trigger automatic shutoff.

5. **Dust management**: Keep dust generation minimal; avoid creating suspensions in enclosed spaces. Good housekeeping to prevent dust accumulation over many dispensing cycles.

6. **Material compatibility and storage**: Store AIBN refrigerated (2–8 °C) or below its SADT; dispense at room temperature but avoid prolonged exposure to temperatures above ~40 °C. Protect from strong light (UV can initiate decomposition).

### Limitations

This review is partially based on established reference data (SDS, handbooks, known calorimetric benchmarks) because the primary scientific literature does not contain AIBN-specific powder characterization studies relevant to automated dispensing. Key data gaps include: measured bulk/tapped density values, shear cell flowability data (ffc, cohesion, wall friction angle), measured electrostatic charge density during handling, and AIBN-specific dust explosion parameters (Kst, Pmax, MIE, MEC). Users intending to deploy AIBN in an automated auger dosing system should perform lot-specific powder characterization and a formal process hazard analysis prior to operation.
