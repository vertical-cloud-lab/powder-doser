# Continuous Control for Gravimetric Powder Dosing: MPC, Rate-PID, and Smooth Profiles vs. Phase-Switched Policies

## 1. MPC Formulations for This Rig

### 1.1 Hybrid / Mixed-Integer MPC

The powder doser mixes continuous inputs (auger speed ω, tilt angle θ) with impulsive discrete events (solenoid taps). This places the system squarely in the domain of hybrid MPC. The Mixed Logical Dynamical (MLD) framework, introduced by Bemporad and Morari for predictive control of constrained hybrid systems, casts binary and continuous variables into a single mixed-integer optimization at each time step. Applying MLD to this rig would treat the tap decision as a binary variable δ_tap ∈ {0,1} at each control interval, with continuous states for cumulative mass m, lip reservoir level r, and tilt angle θ. However, for seconds-scale doses with ≤10 control intervals total and only three actuators, a mixed-integer program per step is computationally feasible but almost certainly over-engineered relative to the information available from a ~1 Hz noisy mass signal.

### 1.2 Integrating Process and Asymmetric Constraints

Dispensed mass is a pure integrator: ṁ = f(ω, θ, δ_tap, r). The no-overshoot constraint (m ≤ m_target) is a hard one-sided terminal constraint. In MPC, this is naturally handled by imposing m_k ≤ m_target − ε at each predicted step, with ε being a safety margin accounting for model mismatch and in-flight powder. Reference governors offer a complementary approach: a separate supervisory layer filters the reference signal to the inner controller such that constraints are never violated, even under worst-case model uncertainty. Cimini et al. (2025) demonstrated an adaptive MPC-based reference governor for DC-DC converters that prevents overshoot while minimizing rise time, showing the general viability of this architecture for one-sided constraint satisfaction (bascone2019modellingandnonlinear pages 3-5, bascone2019modellingandnonlinear pages 5-6).

### 1.3 Economic MPC Trading Accuracy Against Time

Economic MPC (EMPC) replaces setpoint tracking with a direct cost function, e.g., J = α|m_final − m_target| + β·t_dose, subject to m ≤ m_target. This naturally encodes the accuracy-vs-time trade-off. With a short prediction horizon, EMPC degenerates to a one-step-ahead greedy rule: "choose the actuator action that maximizes expected mass deposited per unit time while keeping predicted cumulative mass below the target minus a margin." This is, in effect, what a well-tuned phase-switched policy already does heuristically.

### 1.4 Realistic Horizons and Sampling Rates

For pharmaceutical twin screw feeders operating on minutes-scale time constants, Bascone et al. (2019) used NMPC with a prediction horizon of N_P = 5 steps and control horizon N_C = 2 steps at a 30-second sampling interval, driven by a First Order Plus Dead Time (FOPDT) model (bascone2019modellingandnonlinear pages 3-5, bascone2019modellingandnonlinear pages 5-6). They selected horizons based on the rule N_P − N_C ≥ θ_max/Δt, where θ_max was the maximum system delay (bascone2019modellingandnonlinear pages 5-6). For the present rig, the effective sampling period is ~1 s (the balance settling time). A seconds-scale dose therefore affords only N_P ≈ 3–8 prediction steps—enough for a meaningful MPC formulation, but leaving very little room for the optimizer to outperform a structured heuristic policy. A model order of 2–3 states (cumulative mass, estimated dose rate, lip reservoir) with 2–3 inputs is the practical ceiling.

## 2. Modeling for MPC on Cheap Hardware

### 2.1 Control-Oriented Model Identification

Bascone et al. demonstrated that an FOPDT model with only four parameters (time constant τ, dead time θ, and two volumetric-efficiency parameters α and β) could be identified from 16 experiments across two feeder types and six bulk solids, capturing the mass-flow-rate response of twin screw feeders well enough for NMPC (bascone2019modellingandnonlinear pages 2-3). Parameters including dead time were found to vary across operating conditions, necessitating per-material identification—but the data requirements were modest (bascone2019modellingandnonlinear pages 3-5, bascone2019modellingandnonlinear pages 2-3).

For the present rig, a Hammerstein model structure is natural: a static nonlinearity g(ω, θ, δ_tap) mapping actuator commands to an effective instantaneous deposition rate, followed by a linear integrator (mass = ∫rate·dt). The nonlinearity captures the strong dependence of per-action mass on tilt angle and the rotate-then-tap synergy. A grey-box variant adds a hidden "lip reservoir" state r with dynamics:

  dr/dt = k_rot·ω − k_tap·δ_tap − k_drain(θ)·r

  dm/dt = k_tap·δ_tap·r + k_rot_direct·ω·f(θ) + k_drain(θ)·r

This explicitly models how rotation replenishes the reservoir while taps deplete it, and how tilt modulates drainage. The grey-box model has ~6–8 parameters identifiable from 20–40 calibration doses using nonlinear least squares.

### 2.2 GP-MPC and Learning-Based Approaches

Gaussian Process MPC (GP-MPC) is attractive for powder-dependent dynamics because GPs provide uncertainty-aware predictions from small datasets. The L4acados framework integrates GPyTorch models into the acados optimal control solver, demonstrating real-time GP-MPC with 100–400 data points on autonomous racing platforms with computation times of 6.6–11.9 ms per step (lahr2026l4acadoslearningbasedmodels pages 7-8, lahr2026l4acadoslearningbasedmodels pages 4-5, lahr2026l4acadoslearningbasedmodels pages 8-10). GP-MPC can learn residual dynamics atop a nominal grey-box model, capturing powder-specific behavior (lahr2026l4acadoslearningbasedmodels pages 6-7, lahr2026l4acadoslearningbasedmodels pages 5-6). However, with only 1 Hz effective feedback and short batch durations, the GP has very few training features per dose. A practical approach would be to train the GP across doses (using context variables like powder type, fill level, humidity), not within a single dose.

### 2.3 State Estimation

The 1 Hz settling-limited, vibration-corrupted balance signal is the key sensing bottleneck. In pharmaceutical micro-feeding, mass flow rate is estimated using either a 60-second moving-mean filter (hou2024developmentofa pages 156-161) or Savitzky-Golay derivative filters with 10-minute windows (fathollahi2021developmentofa pages 2-4). These window lengths are far too long for seconds-scale batch dosing.

For this rig, a Kalman filter with two states (true mass m and in-flight mass m_inflight) is appropriate. The measurement model is: y_k = m_k + v_k, where v_k has elevated variance during actuation (vibration corruption) and nominal variance during settling. A switching-covariance Kalman filter that inflates R during actuation and deflates it during quiet intervals would allow the estimator to discount corrupted readings automatically. Dose rate can be estimated as the first difference Δm_k/Δt, smoothed by the Kalman gain. Moving horizon estimation (MHE) is theoretically superior for handling the nonlinear measurement noise profile but is likely overkill for this rig given the computational and implementation overhead.

## 3. PID / Cascaded Control on Dose Rate

### 3.1 Pharmaceutical Loss-in-Weight Feeder Control

Loss-in-weight (LIW) feeders in continuous pharmaceutical manufacturing employ a hierarchical control architecture (huang2023digitaltwindevelopment pages 59-64, huang2023digitaltwindevelopment pages 64-68). Level 0 controls basic equipment parameters; Level 1 uses PID feedback loops regulating mass flow rate by adjusting screw speed. The plant model is typically FOPTD with process gain K ≈ 1, time constant τ ≈ 1 s, and dead time θ ≈ 5 s (huang2023digitaltwindevelopment pages 64-68). PID tuning is material-dependent, with material properties (permeability, cohesion, bulk density) predicting feeder performance (li2020predictiveperformanceof pages 82-87).

### 3.2 Transfer to Finite-Dose Batch Tasks

The key difference between continuous LIW feeding and batch dosing is the stopping problem: the controller must not only regulate rate but must halt actuation before overshoot. This maps to a cutoff prediction problem: the controller stops the actuator when m_measured + m_predicted_inflight ≥ m_target − ε. The in-flight mass model depends on the settling dynamics of the last action (rotation or tap) and the tube/tilt geometry. A simple first-order decay model of residual dripping, calibrated per powder, suffices for a one-step-ahead predictive cutoff.

### 3.3 Rate-Based Trickle Control and Cutoff Prediction

In industrial checkweighers and auger fillers, the standard approach is a two-speed fill (bulk + dribble) with a cutoff setpoint determined by the average in-flight correction. The rate-PI approach extends this: rather than switching at a fixed threshold, the controller continuously regulates dose rate as a function of remaining mass, asymptotically approaching zero rate as m → m_target. Anti-windup is essential because the integrator must not accumulate error that drives post-target actuation.

### 3.4 Smith Predictor for Dead-Time Compensation

Gyürkés et al. (2023) demonstrated a residence-time-distribution-based Smith predictor for dead-time-dominated continuous powder blending, achieving up to 50% reduction in response time compared to standard PI control (gyurkes2023residencetimedistributionbased pages 8-11, gyurkes2023residencetimedistributionbased pages 1-2). For the batch dosing case, the "dead time" corresponds to the balance settling delay. A Smith predictor structure that predicts mass without the settling lag can allow the PI controller to react faster during the trickle phase, directly reducing dose time.

## 4. Interaction-Aware Control

### 4.1 The Lip Reservoir as a Shared Depletable Buffer

The empirically observed interaction—a tap after rotation dispenses more than repeated taps alone—is mechanistically analogous to a surge tank or buffer coupling two processing steps. Rotation replenishes the powder accumulated near the tube lip; tapping depletes it. At steady state, the lip reservoir level determines the effective gain of each actuator. This coupling is multiplicative, not additive: tap effectiveness scales with reservoir level, which in turn depends on recent rotation history and tilt-dependent gravitational drainage.

### 4.2 RGA-Style Interaction Analysis

A formal Relative Gain Array analysis requires a linearized steady-state gain matrix G relating inputs [ω, θ, δ_tap] to outputs [ṁ]. For this system, the gain matrix is strongly state-dependent (through the lip reservoir r) and operating-point-dependent (through tilt). At steep tilt with a full reservoir, all three actuators have high gain and are strongly coupled—the RGA would show off-diagonal dominance, indicating that independent SISO tuning is inappropriate. At shallow tilt with a depleted reservoir, taps have very low gain while rotation has moderate gain, and the RGA approaches a more diagonal structure. This tilt-dependent interaction structure suggests gain scheduling: at steep tilt, control all actuators jointly; at shallow tilt, rotation is the primary actuator with taps as a secondary perturbation channel (yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 7-9, yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 9-10).

### 4.3 Scheduling One Actuator as a Function of Another

The literature on vibratory powder feeding supports the concept of amplitude (or intensity) as a gain-scheduling variable for flow rate (yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 7-9, yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 9-10). Yang and Evans (2007) documented that vibration amplitude inversely controls flow rate in capillary feeders, and that a critical vibration intensity is required to initiate flow for non-free-flowing powders (yang2007meteringanddispensing pages 7-9). By analogy, tilt angle serves as a natural gain-scheduling variable for this rig: it modulates the effectiveness of both rotation and tapping. Rather than optimizing tilt, rotation speed, and tap timing independently, the recommended approach is to parameterize tap and rotation gains as explicit functions of tilt, reducing the effective dimensionality of the control problem.

## 5. Smooth Parameterizations of Dose Profiles

### 5.1 Continuous Decay Profiles Replacing Discrete Phases

The three-phase policy introduces discontinuities at phase boundaries that can cause abrupt changes in dose rate and require careful threshold tuning. A smooth alternative parameterizes the actuator commands as continuous functions of remaining mass Δm = m_target − m_current:

- **Auger speed**: ω(Δm) = ω_max · min(1, (Δm/m_knee)^α), an exponential/power-law decay that reduces rotation speed as the target is approached.
- **Tilt angle**: θ(Δm) = θ_min + (θ_max − θ_min) · min(1, Δm/m_tilt), a linear ramp-down.
- **Tap probability/intensity**: p_tap(Δm) = p_max · (Δm/m_tap)^β · (r_est/r_max), coupling tap likelihood to both remaining mass and estimated lip reservoir.

### 5.2 Use in Filling and Dosing Machines

Fathollahi et al. (2021) demonstrated that feed-forward control using material-specific displacement feed factor profiles—essentially powder-dependent speed curves parameterized by position (analogous to remaining mass)—significantly improved feeding performance for three pharmaceutically relevant materials (fathollahi2021developmentofa pages 4-5, fathollahi2021developmentofa pages 13-14, fathollahi2021developmentofa pages 1-2). The feed factor profile is a reproducible, material-specific function that captures density variations and is used to proactively adjust the actuator speed (fathollahi2021developmentofa pages 4-5). This is precisely the type of smooth, material-specific speed profile that replaces discrete phase thresholds.

### 5.3 Interaction with Bayesian Optimization

Smooth profiles with 4–6 shape parameters (ω_max, α, m_knee, θ_max, m_tilt, β) are more amenable to BO than discrete phase thresholds because: (a) the objective function landscape is smoother—small parameter changes produce small behavioral changes, unlike phase threshold shifts that can cause abrupt mode transitions; (b) the parameter space dimensionality is comparable (~6 vs. ~8–12 for three phases with per-phase settings); and (c) smoothness provides better GP surrogate model fit in BO, which assumes a degree of continuity in the response surface.

## 6. Concrete Recommendation

### 6.1 Is Full MPC Worth It for This Rig?

**No, not at this stage.** The fundamental bottleneck is the 1 Hz effective measurement rate with vibration corruption during actuation. With seconds-scale doses, the MPC optimizer has only 3–8 decision points per batch. The NMPC study by Bascone et al. operated at 30-second sampling intervals on minutes-scale processes—two orders of magnitude more temporal headroom than this rig provides (bascone2019modellingandnonlinear pages 3-5, bascone2019modellingandnonlinear pages 5-6). GP-MPC frameworks like L4acados demonstrate impressive real-time performance, but they operate with far faster sensors (≥50 Hz) and longer episodes that provide rich within-episode data for model updates (lahr2026l4acadoslearningbasedmodels pages 7-8, lahr2026l4acadoslearningbasedmodels pages 4-5).

The commissioning cost is also prohibitive: identifying a powder-specific state-space model with lip reservoir dynamics, validating constraint satisfaction under model mismatch, and tuning the estimator's noise parameters would require 50–100+ doses per powder—unacceptable when BO is already tuning the simpler policy from ≤30 doses.

### 6.2 What Would Change This Answer?

Full MPC becomes worth reconsidering if: (a) the balance settling time drops below 200 ms (e.g., by switching to a strain-gauge load cell with active vibration filtering), yielding ≥5 Hz effective feedback and ≥15–25 decision points per dose; (b) a validated grey-box model with the lip reservoir state is shown to transfer across powders with only 2–3 recalibration doses; or (c) the BO outer loop is found to systematically under-perform because the phase-switched policy structure is too rigid to capture optimal behavior across the powder portfolio.

### 6.3 Recommended Intermediate Step: Rate-PI Trickle + Predictive Cutoff Inside the Three-Phase Skeleton

The highest-value, lowest-risk upgrade is to replace the deterministic fine/tap phases with a closed-loop trickle phase:

1. **Keep the bulk phase** (steep tilt, fast rotation) as-is, switching to trickle when m_measured ≥ m_target − m_margin (BO-tuned).

2. **Trickle phase**: Reduce tilt to a BO-tuned shallow angle. Run a PI controller on estimated dose rate (computed as Kalman-filtered Δm/Δt from 1 Hz mass readings). The PI setpoint is a smooth decaying function of remaining mass, e.g., rate_target = k · (m_target − m_est)^α. Anti-windup is critical: the integrator is clamped to prevent negative (reverse) commands.

3. **Predictive cutoff**: Stop all actuation when m_est + m_inflight_predicted ≥ m_target − ε, where m_inflight is modeled as a first-order exponential decay calibrated from the last few doses. The safety margin ε is set conservatively (e.g., 0.5 mg) and tightened by BO.

4. **Safety governor**: A hard interlock prevents any actuation if m_measured > m_target − ε_hard (e.g., 0.2 mg below target). This provides a formal no-overshoot guarantee independent of the PI/cutoff logic.

5. **Tilt-scheduled tap policy**: Within trickle, allow occasional single taps at a rate proportional to estimated lip reservoir level (inferred from recent rotation history), with tap effectiveness gain-scheduled by current tilt angle.

This architecture preserves the existing MicroPython inner loop (which executes actuator commands and reads the balance) while adding only a PI update, Kalman filter step, and cutoff check at 1 Hz on the Python host—computationally trivial. The BO outer loop now tunes ~8 parameters: m_margin, trickle tilt angle, PI gains (K_p, K_i), rate decay exponent α, cutoff in-flight model constant, safety margin ε, and tap scheduling gain. This is comparable in dimensionality to the current three-phase parameter set but yields smoother dose profiles and eliminates the most time-consuming terminal behavior.

The Smith predictor concept from Gyürkés et al. (2023) can be incorporated to compensate for the ~1 s balance settling lag within the trickle phase PI loop, potentially reducing response time by up to 50% (gyurkes2023residencetimedistributionbased pages 8-11, gyurkes2023residencetimedistributionbased pages 1-2).

### 6.5 Minimal Viable MPC Specification (for Future Reference)

If conditions change and MPC is pursued, the minimal viable implementation would be:

- **Model**: 3-state grey-box (cumulative mass, dose rate, lip reservoir), Hammerstein structure with static nonlinearity g(ω, θ, δ_tap) → rate, plus first-order reservoir dynamics. ~8 parameters identified from 30–50 doses per powder.
- **Estimator**: Switching-covariance Kalman filter, 2 states (mass, rate), R inflated 10× during actuation.
- **Horizon**: N_P = 5, N_C = 3, Δt = 1 s.
- **Solver**: OSQP or scipy.optimize.minimize (SLSQP) on the Python host, solving a small QP/NLP (~15 variables, ~10 constraints) in <100 ms.
- **Constraints**: m_k ≤ m_target − ε(k) with tightening terminal margin; ω ≥ 0, θ ∈ [0, 45°], δ_tap ∈ {0, 1} (relaxed to [0, 1] and rounded, avoiding full MIQP).
- **Fallback**: Hard safety governor that overrides MPC if m_measured > m_target − ε_hard.
- **GP-MPC upgrade path**: After accumulating ~100 doses across powders, fit a GP residual model on top of the grey-box using GPyTorch. Use L4acados-style integration with acados if the host can run a compiled solver, or a simpler one-step lookahead GP prediction for cutoff improvement (lahr2026l4acadoslearningbasedmodels pages 7-8, lahr2026l4acadoslearningbasedmodels pages 2-3, lahr2026l4acadoslearningbasedmodels pages 1-2).

In summary, the practical bottleneck for this rig is not computational power but measurement bandwidth and powder-specific model uncertainty. The rate-PI trickle with predictive cutoff captures the majority of the time savings available from continuous control, while remaining within the information constraints of a 1 Hz vibration-corrupted balance signal and the commissioning budget of tens of doses per powder.
