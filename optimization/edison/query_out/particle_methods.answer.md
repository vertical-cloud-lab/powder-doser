## Methods for simulating powder behavior in screw/auger feeders (physics + numerics)

### Scope and evidence limits
This response focuses on established *physics-based and numerical* methods used to simulate granular/powder behavior in screw conveyors/auger feeders, plus lower-order alternatives suitable for control-oriented digital twins. Several attempts to extract figures/tables via the image-retrieval tool failed (tooling limitation), so the answer is text-only.


## 1) Discrete Element Method (DEM) fundamentals for powders

### 1.1 Contact models: Hertz–Mindlin vs linear spring–dashpot
In DEM, particles are typically treated as rigid bodies whose interactions are governed by contact force laws integrated explicitly in time. A common “high-fidelity” choice is Hertz normal contact plus Mindlin–Deresiewicz tangential contact (history-dependent tangential stiffness) for spheres, often with Coulomb friction limiting tangential force. Ji & Liu summarize Hertz theory for normal loading and Mindlin–Deresiewicz tangential contact relationships for incremental tangential force vs tangential displacement, including the dependence on elastic constants (e.g., effective shear modulus) and loading history. (ji2020computationalgranularmechanics pages 71-75)

Linear spring–dashpot models (linear normal and tangential springs plus damping) are widely used as cheaper approximations. Ji & Liu explicitly note the contrast between linear spring models and nonlinear Hertz–Mindlin contact and discuss the limitations of applying these sphere/disc contact theories to irregular shapes. (ji2020computationalgranularmechanics pages 82-85)

### 1.2 Cohesion for fine/humid powders: JKR, DMT, liquid-bridge/capillary
For fine powders and/or humidity-exposed powders, cohesive forces can dominate, changing flow regimes and feeder performance. Ji & Liu present adhesive contact theories including DMT and JKR as extensions of Hertz contact, including pull-off behavior and unloading distinctions. (ji2020computationalgranularmechanics pages 93-97)

For wet or humid powders where liquid bridges form, Ji & Liu describe capillary (liquid-bridge) forces comprising surface tension and pressure contributions, governed by Laplace–Young geometry, with practical calculation routes (e.g., Gorge method) and fitting forms for liquid bridge forces including dependence on liquid volume and contact angle. (ji2020computationalgranularmechanics pages 93-97)

At the continuum (bulk) level, Deboeuf & Fall show that wet cohesion cannot always be treated as “dry rheology + constant cohesion”; they propose an inertial-flow extension of Mohr–Coulomb 
\(\tau = \tau_c + \mu(I) P\) and emphasize that wet granular assemblies can have a different \(\mu(I)\) relationship than dry ones, and that cohesive rheology often requires both inertial number \(I\) and a reduced pressure \(P^*\) comparing confinement to cohesion. (deboeuf2106unsaturatedwetgranular pages 1-3)

### 1.3 Rolling friction and non-spherical particles
Rolling resistance is commonly added to spherical DEM as a surrogate for angularity/roughness (to prevent unrealistically easy rolling). While the retrieved excerpts emphasize non-sphericity more than rolling-resistance equations, Ji & Liu provide an overview of non-spherical particle modeling and the associated contact-model complications (contact curvature/area dependence, sharp edges/vertices). (ji2020computationalgranularmechanics pages 62-64)

For non-spherical particle representations, Ji & Liu list common geometric models including ellipsoids, superquadrics, polyhedral/dilated-polyhedral, and other star-shaped/spline-based models. (ji2020computationalgranularmechanics pages 62-64)

For irregular shapes, they note that straightforward extensions of sphere contact theory can work for ellipsoids but become complex for superquadrics and polyhedra, and that some “arbitrary shape” overlap-volume/contact computations are too inefficient for massively parallel computation. (ji2020computationalgranularmechanics pages 82-85)

Fransen et al. further emphasize that particle-shape variability is a major driver of DEM cost and motivates coarse-graining and surrogate approaches. (fransen2026towardsscientificmachine pages 4-7)

### 1.4 Time step limits (Rayleigh/contact) and why 100 µm metal powder is expensive
Explicit DEM time integration requires a time step small enough to resolve the highest-frequency contact dynamics. Ji & Liu highlight the Rayleigh-wave-based time step criterion: a critical time step is bounded by the time for stress waves to propagate across a particle, with Rayleigh wave velocity dependent on elastic modulus, density, and Poisson’s ratio. (ji2020computationalgranularmechanics pages 71-75)

For stiff, small particles (e.g., ~100 µm metal AM feedstocks), two compounding effects make device-scale simulation expensive:
1) particle count scales roughly with \(d^{-3}\) for fixed volume, and
2) stable explicit time step shrinks as stiffness increases and size decreases (per Rayleigh/contact limits).
This is why coarse-graining and multi-scale/ML acceleration are active research areas for digital twinning. (ji2020computationalgranularmechanics pages 71-75, fransen2026towardsscientificmachine pages 4-7)


## 2) DEM of screw conveyors and auger dosing specifically

### 2.1 Screw conveyors: throughput vs fill level and inclination
A canonical DEM study is Owen & Cleary (Powder Technology, 2009), which used DEM to predict screw conveyor performance under varying rotational speed, fill level, and inclination. The study reports that mass flow rate increases approximately linearly with fill level across inclinations, and mass flow rate decreases strongly and approximately linearly with inclination up to ~60°, then approaches a constant at steeper angles where particles form a more uniform bed. (owen2009predictionofscrew pages 10-11, owen2009predictionofscrew pages 11-13)

They also report that rotational speed changes have a stronger effect on mass flow and particle velocity than inclination changes, though the scaling is not a simple universal power law. (owen2009predictionofscrew pages 10-11, owen2009predictionofscrew pages 15-15)

### 2.2 Flow regimes relevant to dosing variability near the outlet
Owen & Cleary identify distinct internal flow patterns (recirculatory/heap and avalanching behavior at lower fill/angles versus more shearing/swirl bed flow at higher fill/angles), with avalanching in low-fill cases and reduced free space for avalanching at high fill. These regime changes are directly relevant to your observed “lip reservoir” concept: near-exit discharge can be limited either by (i) availability of a locally loose/free-surface region (avalanche-capable) or (ii) a more sheared, uniform bed that replenishes differently. (owen2009predictionofscrew pages 8-10, owen2009predictionofscrew pages 2-4)

### 2.3 Validation against experiments (and typical errors)
Owen & Cleary compare DEM predictions to experimental throughput data (Roberts and Willis experiments): agreement is described as excellent for horizontal and vertical cases across screw speeds, while for intermediate inclinations (e.g., 30° and 60°) the DEM underpredicts mass flow rate by ~16–24%, attributed plausibly to friction and particle shape differences (particle–wall vs particle–particle friction and shape effects). This is a critical caution for your application: even well-regarded DEM can show geometry/parameter sensitivity at the same inclinations you are actively using for bulk vs fine phases. (owen2009predictionofscrew pages 14-15, owen2009predictionofscrew pages 8-10)

### 2.4 Auger micro-dosing of cohesive powders (pharma perspective)
Hou’s dissertation on micro-feeding cohesive pharmaceutical powders emphasizes that achieving low feed rates with cohesive powders is challenging; key factors include volumetric efficiency/feed factor dependence on screw geometry, screw fullness, and frictional interactions. Hou also notes DEM is widely used to study powder flow behavior in screw feeding, but gaps remain for high-accuracy prediction in complex feeder dynamics—especially relevant when trying to predict mg-scale increments and transient outlet effects. (hou2024developmentofa pages 101-106, hou2024developmentofa pages 57-61)


## 3) Vibration- and impact-driven powder dynamics (taps, compaction, unjamming)

Your solenoid tapper is operating in a regime where vibration/impulse can both (i) mobilize a local free surface/loosely packed region and (ii) compact or stabilize the packing, leading to diminishing returns unless the outlet region is “recharged” by screw rotation.

### 3.1 DEM evidence for cyclic loading compaction/stabilization
Ji & Liu summarize DEM simulations of granular beds under cyclic loading where axial strain/settlement accumulates with cycles and depends on loading frequency; effective stiffness increases over time as the granular structure stabilizes, with early cycles exhibiting larger fluctuations due to unstable particle arrangement that later stabilizes. This provides a mechanistic template for “tapping diminishing returns”: repeated impacts can drive the system toward a more stable/compacted local state with reduced incremental rearrangement per tap unless another action perturbs/replenishes the local loose region. (ji2020computationalgranularmechanics pages 366-369)

### 3.2 Coupling with outlet buildup / cohesion history
For cohesive powders, state-dependence can be amplified by electrostatics or humidity. Liu reports that powders can become more cohesive after passing through a twin-screw feeder due to electrostatic charge acquisition, and that substantial powder buildup can be observed at the feeder exit if significant charge is acquired. This is relevant to the “unobserved lip reservoir” and suggests that outlet-region state (local packing + adhesion) can evolve with throughput history and environment. (liu2020understandingpowderbehavior pages 33-37)

## 4) Cheaper-than-DEM methods (adequate for control-oriented digital twins)

A key distinction for your self-driving-lab control loop is whether you need (a) grain-scale fidelity or (b) accurate *dispensed-mass trajectories* with uncertainty and no-overshoot constraints.

### 4.1 Continuum/constitutive granular models (µ(I), NGF-style, cohesive extensions)
Kim & Kamrin show that classical \(\mu(I)\) rheology (stress ratio vs inertial number) can break down outside homogeneous shear, and propose an augmented \(\mu(I,\Theta)\) relation incorporating a dimensionless granular temperature \(\Theta\) (velocity fluctuation measure) that collapses DEM data across different flow geometries and can predict velocity fields in complex flows (e.g., inclined chutes). This supports continuum modeling when you primarily need bulk transport fields rather than discrete outlet events. (kim2020powerlawscalingin pages 1-2, kim2020powerlawscalingin pages 3-5)

For wet/cohesive powders, Deboeuf & Fall argue that continuum closure needs both inertial flow dependence and cohesion-sensitive parameters (e.g., \(\tau=\tau_c+\mu(I)P\), and reduced pressure \(P^*\)); importantly, liquid bridges can modify the friction law itself, not just add a constant cohesion. (deboeuf2106unsaturatedwetgranular pages 1-3)

### 4.2 MPM, SPH and other continuum numerics
Fransen et al. list continuum numerical approaches including FEM, MPM, and SPH as cheaper alternatives to full DEM for some granular problems, at the cost of relying on phenomenological constitutive laws and potentially losing discrete effects. They also highlight DEM coarse-graining as a strategy (meso-particles representing aggregates) and discuss the difficulty of finding equivalent mesoscopic properties. (fransen2026towardsscientificmachine pages 4-7)

### 4.3 Reduced-order / surrogate models (ML)
Fransen et al. frame ML surrogates, reduced-order modeling, and neural operator approaches as routes to bypass DEM’s prohibitive cost and enable digital twinning, emphasizing dimensionality reduction, surrogate regression (including Gaussian processes), and uncertainty quantification workflows. (fransen2026towardsscientificmachine pages 1-3, fransen2026towardsscientificmachine pages 14-16, fransen2026towardsscientificmachine pages 16-17)

### 4.4 Control-oriented compartment/reservoir models (recommended for your use case)
For your “tilted auger + tapper” behavior (including the unobserved lip reservoir and the strong interaction that *rotation replenishes* while *taps deplete/compact*), the literature-motivated modeling structure is a lumped “inventory + transfer” model: hopper inventory → screw-channel inventory/fill fraction → lip reservoir (loose fraction) → discharged mass, with tap and rotation as distinct inputs coupling into different state transitions. This structure is consistent with the regime changes and fill/inclination sensitivity observed in screw-conveyor DEM studies and with cyclic compaction/stabilization under impacts. (owen2009predictionofscrew pages 8-10, ji2020computationalgranularmechanics pages 366-369)


## 5) Calibration and validation methodology (what to fit, and what errors to expect)

### 5.1 Process-level validation (feeder throughput curves)
Owen & Cleary provide an example of *process-level* validation: DEM throughput compared to experiments across operating points. They report excellent agreement in some orientations and systematic underprediction (~16–24%) at intermediate inclinations, which highlights that DEM parameter sets may not transfer perfectly across operating regimes without careful friction/shape calibration. (owen2009predictionofscrew pages 14-15, owen2009predictionofscrew pages 8-10)

### 5.2 Micro/meso mechanical calibration examples (inverse calibration)
Ji & Liu show an explicit calibration strategy for DEM bond/contact parameters in a different granular context (sea ice as a bonded granular composite): uniaxial compression and three-point bending tests are used to calibrate DEM parameters (e.g., bond strength), illustrating the general inverse-calibration pattern: choose measurable tests, run DEM, and tune parameters to match macroscopic response. (ji2020computationalgranularmechanics pages 255-259)

### 5.3 Powder characterization tests to support calibration and context variables
For powders relevant to AM, Muñiz-Lerma et al. demonstrate how powder characterization ties flowability and density to humidity exposure (e.g., moisture sorption and flow time changes), and how fines can strongly increase moisture pickup and cohesion—directly relevant to your context variables (humidity exposure history, temperature). (munizlerma2018acomprehensiveapproach pages 5-8)

In pharmaceutical powder handling, Liu describes using shear cell tests, compressibility tests, and dynamic flow energy tests to characterize flowability changes before/after a screw feeder and relates increased cohesion to tribo-electrostatic charging; exit buildup is observed for strongly charged powders. These tests suggest practical calibration/validation inputs for your digital twin: measure pre/post-feeding property shifts and incorporate them as latent “state-of-cohesion” variables. (liu2020understandingpowderbehavior pages 33-37)


## 6) Recommendation: simulation-method ladder for a tilted auger + tapper doser

The literature supports a *ladder* approach that matches model fidelity and cost to the decisions you need to make:

1. **Compartment/reservoir + stochastic outlet events (control twin):** Use an interpretable state model that explicitly represents the lip reservoir and distinguishes (i) screw-driven replenishment and (ii) tap-driven discharge/compaction. Fit the model directly to dose trajectories (mass vs time) under designed experiments across tilt, fill, and humidity. This will be the most effective for controller design, no-overshoot constraint handling, and BO inside your autonomous loop, while still reflecting regime changes identified in screw DEM (heap/avalanche vs shear/swirl). (owen2009predictionofscrew pages 8-10, ji2020computationalgranularmechanics pages 366-369)

2. **Coarse-grained DEM (physics insight across tilts/fills, outlet region studies):** Use for understanding how inclination changes flow pattern and how refill (rotation) changes local packing near the exit, consistent with Owen & Cleary’s regime transitions and inclination dependence. Coarse graining is explicitly motivated as a computational necessity. (fransen2026towardsscientificmachine pages 4-7, owen2009predictionofscrew pages 8-10)

3. **Full DEM (selective, geometry-change validation):** Reserve for major geometry modifications (tube lip geometry, auger pitch, clearances, tapper location) and for diagnosing outlet variability mechanisms; expect that without careful friction/shape/cohesion calibration you can see regime-dependent systematic errors similar to the intermediate-inclination underprediction reported by Owen & Cleary. (owen2009predictionofscrew pages 14-15, owen2009predictionofscrew pages 8-10)

4. **Continuum rheology (optional backbone for filled sections):** If you later need a PDE-scale model of the screw/tube region (e.g., for design exploration of long screws or different hopper coupling), µ(I)-type models and cohesive extensions provide a path, but they will generally need augmentation to represent the lip reservoir and discrete tap-triggered avalanches. (kim2020powerlawscalingin pages 3-5, kim2020powerlawscalingin pages 1-2, deboeuf2106unsaturatedwetgranular pages 1-3)

5. **Surrogate acceleration (optional):** Once you have either DEM datasets or large experimental trajectory datasets, ML surrogate approaches are explicitly motivated in the literature for granular digital twins to reduce cost and support uncertainty quantification. In your setting, surrogates are best used to emulate either (i) DEM-derived correction terms or (ii) the mapping from context/history → reduced-model parameters, rather than replacing the reduced model entirely. (fransen2026towardsscientificmachine pages 1-3, fransen2026towardsscientificmachine pages 14-16, fransen2026towardsscientificmachine pages 16-17)
