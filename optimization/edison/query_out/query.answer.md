# Multi-Parameter, Multi-Objective Control and Bayesian Optimization for an Autonomous Powder Dosing Rig: A Literature Review

## 1. MIMO / Multivariable Control Foundations

### From Single-Loop PID to Multi-Input Systems

The powder doser described here—with auger speed, tapper duty, vibration amplitude, servo tilt, and balance settings as simultaneously commandable actuators—is a classic multi-input, single-output (MISO) or multi-input, multi-output (MIMO) control problem if one considers both dispensed mass and dispense time as outputs. Classical single-loop PID control applies to each actuator independently but ignores coupling: changing auger speed alters the effective feed factor, which changes the regime in which tapping or vibration is beneficial. In classical MIMO control, decentralized (multi-loop) PID controllers are designed by first analyzing loop interactions via the Relative Gain Array (RGA), which quantifies how much each input-output pair is affected by other loops. When interactions are strong, decoupling compensators or full multivariable designs (LQR, LQG, H∞) are warranted.

### Model Predictive Control as the Natural Framework

Model Predictive Control (MPC) is widely recognized as the standard approach for handling multiple manipulated variables, competing objectives, and hard constraints simultaneously. MPC solves a finite-horizon constrained optimal control problem at each time step, using cost-function weighting matrices Q (state) and R (input) to trade off competing objectives, while directly encoding input and state constraints (jong2025deepoperatorneural pages 12-14, chan2024apracticalmultiobjective pages 6-7). For MIMO systems, MPC naturally coordinates multiple actuators through its optimization-based formulation. The MPC cost function is typically a weighted quadratic:

J = Σ (x_k − x_ref)ᵀ Q (x_k − x_ref) + Σ u_kᵀ R u_k

subject to u_min ≤ u_k ≤ u_max and state constraints, which can directly encode a no-overshoot requirement as m_dispensed ≤ m_target at all times.

### When Is MPC Appropriate vs. Overkill?

For the described dosing rig, real-time MPC may be over-engineered for several reasons: (i) the system dynamics are slow (seconds-scale dose, balance settling times of order 1 s), (ii) the dimensionality is low (4–5 actuators), and (iii) the process model is highly uncertain and powder-dependent. MPC computation times of 0.01–0.17 s per step have been demonstrated for small MIMO systems (jong2025deepoperatorneural pages 12-14), so real-time feasibility is not the barrier. Rather, the difficulty lies in obtaining a reliable process model for each powder. Explicit MPC (where the control law is pre-computed offline as a piecewise-affine lookup table) or even a simple phase-switched rule-based controller with BO-tuned parameters is likely more practical (chan2024apracticalmultiobjective pages 6-7). MPC becomes justified if the rig later scales to higher throughput or if a data-driven model (e.g., Gaussian process or neural network) can be fitted online from accumulated dose data.

## 2. Multi-Objective Control: Handling Competing Objectives

### Scalarization and Weighted-Sum Approaches

The most common approach to multiple objectives in control practice is scalarization: forming a single cost function as a weighted sum of individual objectives. In MPC, the Q and R matrices serve this role, with diagonal entries reflecting relative importance (jong2025deepoperatorneural pages 12-14). For the dosing rig, a scalarized cost might be: J = w₁·|m_error| + w₂·t_dose + w₃·Σ|Δu|, combining accuracy, time, and actuator effort. The Guided Bayesian Optimization approach of Nobar et al. similarly aggregates multiple performance metrics into a single cost using weighted combination for controller tuning (nobar2024guidedbayesianoptimization pages 2-3).

### Constraint Reformulation: Demoting Objectives to Constraints

A critical insight from both control theory and BO literature is that **not all performance criteria should be objectives**. The no-overshoot requirement is paradigmatic: because powder cannot be removed once dispensed, overshoot is not a symmetric penalty but an asymmetric hard constraint. The literature strongly supports treating overshoot as a constraint (m_dispensed ≤ m_target) rather than an objective, using either hard constraints in MPC or safety constraints in BO (berkenkamp2023bayesianoptimizationwith pages 8-10, berkenkamp2023bayesianoptimizationwith pages 1-3, gelbart2014bayesianoptimizationwith pages 1-3). Similarly, cross-contamination risk and minimum repeatability standards are better modeled as feasibility constraints than optimized objectives.

### Pareto-Front Approaches and Multi-Objective MPC

When objectives genuinely conflict and the decision-maker's preferences are uncertain, Pareto-front exploration is appropriate. Multi-objective Bayesian optimization methods such as ParEGO (which uses augmented Tchebycheff scalarizations with random weight vectors) and qNEHVI (which directly maximizes expected hypervolume improvement of the Pareto front) provide principled approaches (wang2023recentadvancesin pages 11-12, daulton2021parallelbayesianoptimization pages 8-9, daulton2020differentiableexpectedhypervolume pages 1-2). However, the Pareto approach is most valuable during initial exploration of a new powder or rig configuration; once preferences are established, collapsing to a scalarized or constrained single-objective formulation improves sample efficiency (coutinho2024humanintheloopcontrollertuning pages 1-2).

### Guidance on Minimizing Objectives

The literature suggests keeping **as few objectives as possible**—ideally one or two—for sample efficiency. The recommended decomposition for the dosing rig is:
- **Primary objective**: Minimize |m_dispensed − m_target| (accuracy)
- **Secondary objective** (if Pareto exploration desired): Minimize t_dose (throughput)
- **Hard constraints**: m_dispensed ≤ m_target (no overshoot); repeatability ≥ threshold
- **Regularization terms** (folded into scalarized cost): actuator wear (penalty on tapping count, vibration duty), input smoothness (move suppression)

This reduces the problem from the original five or more competing concerns to a constrained one- or two-objective optimization, which is far more tractable for BO with limited experimental budget.

## 3. Time-Varying / Phased Control Policies

### Phase-Switched Control: Bulk Feed → Trickle Feed

The most directly relevant control paradigm for gravimetric dosing is **coarse/fine (bulk/trickle) phase-switched feeding**, which is standard practice in industrial weighing and filling machines and in loss-in-weight pharmaceutical feeders. During bulk feed, the auger runs at high speed to rapidly approach the target mass; near a switching threshold (e.g., m_target − Δ), the controller transitions to trickle feed at reduced speed to approach the setpoint slowly, minimizing overshoot risk. Loss-in-weight feeders in pharmaceutical manufacturing operate analogously, switching between gravimetric control (where screw speed is adjusted based on weight feedback) and volumetric mode (constant screw speed) during hopper refill when load-cell signals are unreliable (fathollahi2024improvingcontinuouslossinweight pages 1-2, fathollahi2024improvingcontinuouslossinweight pages 2-4, li2020predictiveperformanceof pages 66-73).

### Gain Scheduling and Feed Factor Estimation

In LIW feeder control, the **feed factor**—the mass delivered per screw revolution—serves as the key gain parameter and drifts with hopper fill level, powder densification, and environmental conditions (li2020predictiveperformanceof pages 36-40, fathollahi2024improvingcontinuouslossinweight pages 2-4, fathollahi2024improvingcontinuouslossinweight pages 1-2). Hopper-level-dependent gain scheduling is standard: the controller adjusts screw speed commands based on an estimated or calibrated feed factor that varies with fill level. Fathollahi et al. demonstrated that novel hopper designs can homogenize the stress profile and reduce feed factor drift with fill level, effectively reducing the need for aggressive gain scheduling (fathollahi2024improvingcontinuouslossinweight pages 2-4, fathollahi2024improvingcontinuouslossinweight pages 1-2). For the described rig, fill level is a natural scheduling variable, and the feed factor (mass per auger revolution) should be estimated and updated.

### Trajectory Parameterization

Rather than optimizing raw time-varying actuator commands (which creates a high-dimensional search space), the literature favors **parameterized control profiles**: piecewise-constant setpoints per phase (bulk speed, trickle speed, phase-switch threshold), ramp rates, or spline coefficients. This trajectory parameterization keeps the BO search space low-dimensional (typically 5–15 parameters) while allowing rich time-varying behavior (khosravi2021performancedrivencascadecontroller pages 1-2, chan2024apracticalmultiobjective pages 1-2). The "displacement-feed factor" profile concept from micro-feeder literature provides a material-specific calibration curve that can be used to parameterize the expected mass output as a function of actuator displacement (fathollahi2020performanceevaluationof pages 1-2, fathollahi2020performanceevaluationof pages 2-3).

### Iterative Learning Control and Run-to-Run Correction

For repetitive dosing tasks, **Iterative Learning Control (ILC)** and **run-to-run (R2R) control** are highly relevant. ILC updates the input trajectory between batches based on the error from the previous batch, converging toward a trajectory that produces the desired output profile. R2R control applies similar corrections at a coarser level—e.g., adjusting feed factor estimates, phase-switch thresholds, or bias corrections between doses based on the most recent dose error. This is directly analogous to the "smart refill" predictions in LIW feeders, where past experience informs volumetric-mode behavior during the next refill cycle (fathollahi2020performanceevaluationof pages 1-2). For the dosing rig, a simple exponentially-weighted moving average (EWMA) correction of the feed factor or target-approach offset between doses constitutes practical R2R control.

### Event-Triggered Switching

Near the setpoint, event-triggered control—where the controller switches behavior based on measured mass crossing a threshold rather than on elapsed time—is natural. The phase-switch from bulk to trickle feed is itself event-triggered (mass exceeds threshold), and a final "dribble and wait" phase where the auger is stopped and the balance is allowed to settle before declaring dose completion is standard in gravimetric filling practice.

## 4. The Bridge to Bayesian Optimization

### BO for Controller/Policy Tuning

Bayesian optimization is established as a sample-efficient, model-free method for tuning controller parameters from closed-loop performance data. Khosravi et al. demonstrated BO-based cascade PID tuning that converges to near-optimal gains within 5–18 iterations on real hardware, significantly outperforming classical methods (khosravi2021performancedrivencascadecontroller pages 1-2, khosravi2021performancedrivencascadecontroller pages 7-8). Nobar et al. further improved data efficiency by 46–57% using a digital-twin-guided BO approach that substitutes cheap simulation evaluations when GP uncertainty is high (nobar2024guidedbayesianoptimization pages 1-2, nobar2024guidedbayesianoptimization pages 2-3). For the dosing rig, the BO search space consists of the parameterized dose profile (bulk speed, trickle speed, phase-switch mass threshold, tapper/vibration schedules per phase, balance integration time)—typically 8–15 continuous parameters.

### Policy-Parameter BO vs. Direct Open-Loop Profile Optimization

The literature strongly favors **policy-parameter BO** ("learn the knobs of a feedback law") over direct open-loop profile optimization. A closed-loop policy with BO-tuned parameters inherits robustness from the feedback structure—it can react to within-dose disturbances even if the BO-selected parameters are imperfect. Direct open-loop optimization of actuator waveforms lacks this robustness and requires a much higher-dimensional search space. Chan et al. formalized this as optimizing a parameterized control policy using multi-objective BO, noting that deep-learning policy representations combined with MOBO enable efficient embedded control design (chan2024apracticalmultiobjective pages 1-2, chan2024apracticalmultiobjective pages 6-7).

### Multi-Objective BO: qNEHVI, ParEGO, and Alternatives

For Pareto exploration of accuracy-vs-time tradeoffs, the state-of-the-art acquisition function is **qNEHVI** (q-Noisy Expected Hypervolume Improvement), which is one-step Bayes-optimal for hypervolume maximization under noisy observations and supports parallel batch evaluation (daulton2021parallelbayesianoptimization pages 8-9, daulton2021parallelbayesianoptimization pages 9-10, daulton2021parallelbayesianoptimization pages 1-2). qNEHVI consistently outperforms ParEGO variants and Thompson-sampling-based approaches across benchmarks (daulton2021parallelbayesianoptimization pages 27-29). **ParEGO**, which scalarizes objectives with random Tchebycheff weights per iteration, remains a practical lightweight alternative when the number of objectives is small (wang2023recentadvancesin pages 11-12). For the dosing rig with at most two true objectives (accuracy and time) plus constraints, either method is appropriate; qNEHVI is preferred if computational resources permit, while ParEGO offers simplicity. The qEHVI variant provides exact differentiable hypervolume computation for parallel multi-objective BO (daulton2020differentiableexpectedhypervolume pages 1-2, daulton2020differentiableexpectedhypervolume pages 9-11). Evolution-guided BO (EGBO) integrating selection pressure with qNEHVI has shown improved Pareto front coverage and better constraint handling in self-driving lab applications (low2024evolutionguidedbayesianoptimization pages 1-2).

### Contextual BO for Observed-but-Uncontrolled Variables

**Contextual Bayesian optimization** is the principled approach for conditioning on environmental variables (fill level, humidity exposure, temperature) that shift process dynamics but cannot be controlled. The ENVBO algorithm of Diessner et al. fits a global GP surrogate over both controllable and environmental variables, then optimizes the acquisition function conditionally on measured environmental values at each iteration (diessner2024onthedevelopment pages 7-8, diessner2024onthedevelopment pages 2-4, diessner2024onthedevelopment pages 1-2, diessner2024onthedevelopment pages 18-21). This enables finding optimal controllable parameters across the entire range of environmental conditions in a single optimization campaign, rather than repeating optimization for each fixed condition. De Blasi et al. extended contextual BO with safety constraints for industrial self-learning machines, using a separable kernel k(x,z,x′,z′) = k(x,x′)·k(z,z′) that independently models controllable parameters and contextual variables (blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 5-6, blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 1-2, blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 2-3, blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 3-5). For the dosing rig, fill level, cumulative humidity exposure, and ambient temperature are natural context variables in the GP kernel, and the hybrid approach of Siska et al. combining mechanistic models with GPs may improve data efficiency when domain knowledge about powder behavior is available (siska2026aguideto pages 22-24).

### Safe BO for the No-Overshoot Constraint

**SafeOpt** and its extensions are directly applicable to the no-overshoot constraint. SafeOpt restricts optimization to a certified safe set of parameters, expanding this set only when GP confidence bounds guarantee that new parameters satisfy safety constraints with high probability (fiedler2024onsafetyin pages 7-9, berkenkamp2023bayesianoptimizationwith pages 8-10, berkenkamp2023bayesianoptimizationwith pages 1-3, berkenkamp2023bayesianoptimizationwith pages 3-5). Berkenkamp et al. generalized SafeOpt to handle multiple independent safety constraints separate from the performance objective and demonstrated context-dependent transfer of safety knowledge across tasks (berkenkamp2023bayesianoptimizationwith pages 1-3, berkenkamp2023bayesianoptimizationwith pages 19-21). For the dosing rig, the safety constraint g(θ) = m_target − max(m_dispensed(θ)) ≥ 0 ensures no overshoot; SafeOpt requires an initial safe parameter set (e.g., a very slow, conservative dose profile) from which to expand. Fiedler et al. identified practical limitations of SafeOpt—particularly the reliance on RKHS norm bounds—and proposed LoSBO, which guarantees safety using only Lipschitz assumptions (fiedler2024onsafetyin pages 1-2). Bottero et al. proposed ISE-BO, an information-theoretic safe exploration criterion that is naturally applicable to continuous domains and achieves improved sample efficiency over SafeOpt (bottero2024informationtheoreticsafebayesian pages 21-23). StageOpt separates safe region expansion from optimization into distinct stages, which may be practical for initial commissioning of a new powder (sui2018stagewisesafebayesian pages 1-2).

### Transfer/Multi-Task BO Across Powders

When optimizing dosing parameters for multiple powders (AlSi10Mg, silicon, stainless steel, etc.), **transfer learning BO** and **multi-task Gaussian processes** can accelerate optimization of each new powder by leveraging data from previous powders. Feurer et al. described practical transfer BO methods that fit GP base models to completed optimization runs and combine predictions with a target-task model using learned weights (feurer2018practicaltransferlearning pages 3-6). Wagner et al. demonstrated sample-efficient Bayesian transfer learning for machine parameter optimization across different materials (sheet thicknesses, steel types), achieving superior performance with minimal target-task data (wagner2025sampleefficientbayesiantransfer pages 5-6). Multi-task BO (MTBO) uses the Linear Model of Coregionalization or similar multi-output GP structures to share information across tasks (wang2023recentadvancesin pages 12-13). For the dosing rig, each powder defines a task, and the shared structure across tasks reflects commonalities in auger-powder mechanics. The BOLT framework of Zeng et al. scales multi-task BO to hundreds of tasks using LLM-generated initializations (zeng2025largescalemultitask pages 1-2), though this is likely unnecessary for the modest number of powders in an alloy-discovery campaign.

### Sample Efficiency Considerations

Sample efficiency is paramount when each dose costs real time (minutes) and material. The guided BO approach reduces required hardware experiments by 46–57% through digital-twin substitution (nobar2024guidedbayesianoptimization pages 1-2). Preferential BO (PBO), where a human provides pairwise preference judgments rather than quantitative metrics, can focus optimization on desired tradeoff regions with fewer experiments than full Pareto exploration (coutinho2024humanintheloopcontrollertuning pages 5-6, coutinho2024humanintheloopcontrollertuning pages 1-2). For the dosing rig, a budget of 20–50 doses per powder for initial optimization is realistic, with transfer learning reducing this for subsequent powders.

## 5. Domain-Specific Literature

### Loss-in-Weight Feeder Control

Loss-in-weight (LIW) feeders are the pharmaceutical industry's standard for continuous powder metering and provide the closest industrial analogue to the described dosing rig. LIW feeders operate in gravimetric mode during steady-state (adjusting screw speed based on load-cell feedback to maintain target mass flow rate) and switch to volumetric mode during hopper refill when weight signals are unreliable (fathollahi2024improvingcontinuouslossinweight pages 1-2, fathollahi2024improvingcontinuouslossinweight pages 2-4, li2020predictiveperformanceof pages 66-73). The **feed factor**—mass delivered per screw revolution—is the key calibration parameter and depends strongly on powder bulk density, which in turn varies with hopper fill level, powder compressibility, and environmental conditions (li2020predictiveperformanceof pages 36-40, fathollahi2024improvingcontinuouslossinweight pages 2-4). Refill disturbances cause transient overfeeding as incoming material compresses the powder bed and increases screw fill fraction, with 10–15 second post-refill delays before gravimetric control resumes (li2020predictiveperformanceof pages 66-73). Li developed predictive models using partial least squares regression to estimate maximum feed-rate deviation during refills from material flow properties (li2020predictiveperformanceof pages 13-16, li2020predictiveperformanceof pages 30-36).

### Micro-Feeders and Low-Dose Precision

Fathollahi et al. developed a micro-feeder for low-dose cohesive pharmaceutical powders (rates < 100 g/h) using a volumetric displacement principle with outlet balance monitoring. Their **displacement-feed factor** profile characterizes density variation during processing and enables material-specific calibration (fathollahi2020performanceevaluationof pages 1-2, fathollahi2020performanceevaluationof pages 2-3). Powders with finer particles show densification during feeding, while larger particles exhibit both densification and elastic recovery ("powder bed expansion"). Hou developed a pneumatic micro-feeder specifically for cohesive powders, using controlled air flow to entrain particles with forces that overcome cohesion variability (hou2024developmentofa pages 112-115).

### Powder Flowability and Environmental Effects

Moisture content is a critical determinant of powder flowability and feeding performance. Water acts through two competing mechanisms: lubrication (reducing friction) and cohesion enhancement (through liquid bridge formation via capillary forces) (crouter2014theeffectof pages 1-2, crouter2014theeffectof pages 2-4). At low moisture levels, water may improve flow, but above a material-specific threshold, increased cohesion dominates and flowability decreases significantly—small changes of 2–3% moisture content can substantially degrade feeding uniformity (crouter2014theeffectof pages 2-4). Water activity (>0.60) leads to liquid bridge formation that significantly reduces flowability (suhag2024factorsinfluencingfood pages 6-7). For metal powders like AlSi10Mg, oxidation and moisture absorption over cumulative air exposure are analogous concerns. Temperature also affects interparticle cohesive forces, generally reducing cohesion at higher temperatures (suhag2024factorsinfluencingfood pages 4-6). These environmental sensitivities motivate the inclusion of humidity exposure history and temperature as contextual variables in the BO framework.

### Self-Driving Lab Solid Dispensing

Solid powder dispensing remains one of the most significant motor-function challenges in self-driving laboratories (seifrid2022autonomouschemicalexperiments pages 8-9, seifrid2022autonomouschemicalexperiments pages 1-2, tom2024selfdrivinglaboratoriesfor pages 4-5). Seifrid et al. noted that accurate dispensing of small solid amounts (particularly below 20 mg) requires substantial calibration and testing, and that in discovery-mode synthesis this investment is often infeasible when hundreds of different solids require customized settings (seifrid2022autonomouschemicalexperiments pages 8-9). Many SDLs work around this by using stock solutions and liquid-handling technologies (tom2024selfdrivinglaboratoriesfor pages 4-5). The HeinSight 4.0 platform integrates commercial powder dosing with real-time gravimetric feedback and vision-guided experimental control (elkhawaldeh2025closedloopvisionguidedexperimental pages 3-6). The "frugal twin" concept advocates low-cost SDL prototypes as testbeds for software and optimization algorithm development (doloi2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 1-2). The present work on BO-driven auto-tuning of a low-cost auger doser addresses a critical gap: no published work was found that applies multi-objective or contextual BO specifically to optimizing powder dosing parameters in an SDL context.

## 6. Synthesis and Recommendations

### Recommended Layered Architecture

Based on the reviewed literature, the following three-layer architecture is supported for the low-cost dosing rig:

**Layer 1 (Inner loop): Phase-switched gravimetric dose profile with real-time feedback.** A simple deterministic controller executes each dose in two or three phases: (i) bulk feed at high auger speed with active tapping/vibration, (ii) trickle feed at reduced speed with attenuated agitation, and (iii) a settle-and-verify phase where the auger is stopped and the balance reading is allowed to stabilize. The phase-switch threshold, per-phase auger speeds, tapper/vibration schedules, and balance integration time are all parameters of this inner policy. This mirrors the coarse/fine practice universal in gravimetric filling and LIW feeder control (fathollahi2024improvingcontinuouslossinweight pages 1-2, li2020predictiveperformanceof pages 66-73, fathollahi2020performanceevaluationof pages 1-2).

**Layer 2 (Outer loop): Contextual, safe, multi-objective BO over the inner policy's parameters.** The BO loop tunes the ~8–15 parameters of the inner-loop policy by observing closed-loop dose outcomes (accuracy, time, overshoot occurrence). Context variables (fill level, humidity exposure, temperature) are included in the GP kernel using a separable or product kernel structure (blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 5-6, blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 3-5). Safety (no-overshoot) is enforced using SafeOpt-style constraints with a conservative initial safe recipe as the seed (berkenkamp2023bayesianoptimizationwith pages 8-10, berkenkamp2023bayesianoptimizationwith pages 1-3). If Pareto exploration of accuracy vs. time is desired, qNEHVI or constrained ParEGO can be used (daulton2021parallelbayesianoptimization pages 8-9, low2024evolutionguidedbayesianoptimization pages 1-2); otherwise, a scalarized single-objective with overshoot as a hard constraint is more sample-efficient. BoTorch provides a mature open-source implementation supporting all required capabilities: multi-objective optimization, constrained optimization, contextual modeling, and parallel batch evaluation (balandat2019botorchprogrammablebayesian pages 6-7, balandat2019botorchprogrammablebayesian pages 4-6, balandat2019botorchprogrammablebayesian pages 1-3).

**Layer 3 (Cross-dose / cross-powder): Run-to-run corrections and transfer learning.** Between doses, simple EWMA-based feed-factor correction updates the inner-loop's expected mass-per-revolution, compensating for slow drift due to fill-level changes or environmental shifts—analogous to the feed-factor recalibration in LIW feeders (li2020predictiveperformanceof pages 36-40, fathollahi2024improvingcontinuouslossinweight pages 2-4). When switching to a new powder, transfer BO warm-starts the GP from previous powder campaigns using multi-task GP or RGPE-style ensemble methods (wagner2025sampleefficientbayesiantransfer pages 5-6, feurer2018practicaltransferlearning pages 3-6, wang2023recentadvancesin pages 12-13).

### Recommended Objective Structure (Minimizing Objectives)

The literature strongly supports reducing the number of explicit objectives:

- **Keep as primary objective**: Absolute mass error |m_dispensed − m_target|
- **Keep as secondary objective** (only if Pareto exploration desired): Dose completion time t_dose
- **Convert to hard constraint**: No overshoot (m_dispensed ≤ m_target with high probability)
- **Convert to constraint**: Repeatability (σ_mass ≤ threshold, assessed over rolling window)
- **Fold into scalarized cost or regularization**: Actuator wear (penalty on total tap count, vibration duty)
- **Handle via experimental design, not control**: Cross-contamination (cleaning protocol between powders)

This yields a constrained single-objective or constrained bi-objective problem, which is tractable for BO with budgets of 20–50 doses.

### Typical BO Parameter Vector

For the outer BO loop, the parameter vector θ typically includes:
- Bulk-feed auger speed (RPM or step rate)
- Trickle-feed auger speed
- Phase-switch mass threshold (m_target − Δ)
- Optional: second switch threshold for dribble phase
- Tapper duty cycle (bulk phase) and tapper duty cycle (trickle phase)
- Vibration amplitude (bulk phase) and vibration amplitude (trickle phase)
- Servo tilt angle
- Balance integration/filter time setting
- Cutoff anticipation offset (to account for in-flight material)

This results in approximately 8–12 continuous parameters, well within the effective range for GP-based BO (khosravi2021performancedrivencascadecontroller pages 1-2, khosravi2021performancedrivencascadecontroller pages 7-8, chan2024apracticalmultiobjective pages 1-2).

### Software and Implementation

BoTorch (built on PyTorch and GPyTorch) provides modular, differentiable implementations of all required BO capabilities: qNEHVI for multi-objective optimization, constrained optimization via feasibility-weighted objectives, and support for contextual modeling through flexible GP kernel design (balandat2019botorchprogrammablebayesian pages 6-7, balandat2019botorchprogrammablebayesian pages 4-6, balandat2019botorchprogrammablebayesian pages 1-3). The Ax platform provides a higher-level experiment management layer. For safe BO, the SafeOpt and LoSBO algorithms are available as research implementations (fiedler2024onsafetyin pages 7-9, fiedler2024onsafetyin pages 1-2), and the ENVBO algorithm for contextual optimization is available via the NUBO Python package (diessner2024onthedevelopment pages 2-4). The safe contextual BO framework of De Blasi et al. has been implemented as a generic software module for industrial edge control devices, providing a template for integration with the dosing rig's control system (blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 1-2, blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 11-12).

### Summary

The described powder dosing rig is best served not by a monolithic MIMO controller but by a hierarchical architecture that separates fast within-dose feedback (simple phase-switched policy) from slow between-dose parameter adaptation (contextual safe BO) and cross-powder knowledge transfer (multi-task BO). The literature on LIW feeder control, safe Bayesian optimization, and self-driving laboratory automation converges on this layered design. The key design choices—treating overshoot as a constraint rather than an objective, parameterizing the policy with a small number of interpretable knobs, including environmental variables as GP context rather than noise, and leveraging transfer learning across powders—are all well-supported by the cited literature and together yield a framework that is both principled and practical for a low-cost, noisy, slow-sampling experimental rig operating across diverse metal additive manufacturing feedstocks.
