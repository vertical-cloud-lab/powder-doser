## Detailed Critique of Musings

### M1. Framing — Joint State-Parameter Estimation via State Augmentation

**Confirmed.** The technique of appending slowly-varying model parameters (feed factor, lip-drain coefficient, tap ejection fraction, cohesion index) to the state vector as random-walk states and estimating them alongside fast states (mass, rate, lip mass, in-flight mass) is the textbook approach known as *joint state and parameter estimation via augmented state*. The augmented state vector combines the original state vector and parameter vector, transforming the estimation problem by incorporating parameter dynamics into the system and measurement models (chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 7-9). Even for originally linear systems, this augmentation creates nonlinear estimation problems because parameters appear multiplicatively in system matrices (chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 7-9). This is exactly the situation in your rig (rate = feed_factor × fill^0.7 × tilt_gain × ω).

Baldassarre et al. (2024) demonstrated precisely this approach—augmented-state EKF for a digital twin of an industrial production system—where unknown parameters were modeled using a discrete-time random-walk model and estimated online alongside system states (baldassarre2024digitaltwinwith pages 4-5, baldassarre2024digitaltwinwith pages 2-4). After convergence, the identified parameters were used for prediction without the filter, which maps directly onto your dose-to-dose parameter update workflow. The terminology you want is: *joint state-parameter estimation*, *augmented state filter*, *random-walk parameter dynamics*, and *dual estimation* (when using separate state and parameter filters) (chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 2-4).

### M2. Ensemble Methods vs. EKF

**Partially correct—but the recommended method is UKF, not EnKF.** Your reasoning that EKF is unattractive for bilinear/multiplicative dynamics (requiring Jacobians of simulation code) is well-founded. However, for a 4–8 dimensional state space, the literature consistently recommends the UKF rather than the EnKF. The UKF provides "Jacobian-free, second-order accuracy for smooth dynamics" via deterministic sigma-point propagation, whereas the EnKF is characterized as "scalable to large stochastic systems" with "high to very high" computational complexity (chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 11-12). The UKF uses sigma points instead of linearization, achieving second-order accuracy for nonlinear systems without explicit Jacobian calculations (jin2021thenewtrend pages 2-3, jin2021thenewtrend pages 5-6, chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 4-6).

The EnKF's Monte Carlo sampling introduces stochastic noise that is unnecessary at your state dimension. With n = 8 augmented states, the UKF needs only 2n+1 = 17 sigma points per step—deterministic, reproducible, and computationally trivial at 1 Hz. An EnKF would need 50–200 ensemble members to achieve comparable covariance estimates, each introducing sampling noise. Furthermore, the dual estimation architecture (DUKF)—where separate state and parameter filters run in parallel—has been shown to consistently outperform joint estimation (JUKF) in accuracy, robustness, and reduced divergence risk (chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 11-12, chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 9-11).

**Recommendation:** Replace EnKF with a Dual UKF (DUKF). The state filter estimates (mass, rate, lip mass, in-flight mass); the parameter filter estimates feed factor (and optionally one other parameter). This gives you the Jacobian-free benefit you seek without the stochastic overhead.

### M3. Within-Dose vs. Dose-to-Dose Split

**Confirmed.** This is a well-motivated architectural decision. With only ~30–60 usable observations per dose at 1 Hz effective rate, the within-dose window is too short and too poorly excited to calibrate more than the fast states plus at most one drifting parameter (feed factor). The heavier parameter learning belongs between doses, where each completed dose log provides a batch of observations for calibrating the full parameter set of the compartment model.

The analogy to reservoir history matching is apt. ES-MDA (Ensemble Smoother with Multiple Data Assimilation) performs iterative batch calibration using all historical observations simultaneously, with inflated observation error covariances to handle nonlinearity in forward models (zhao2025predictionofsoft pages 4-6, zhao2025predictionofsoft pages 2-4). Each ES-MDA iteration applies a smaller update than a single-pass smoother, better preserving nonlinear behavior. Typical implementations use ensemble sizes of ~500 members with ~8 iterations (zhao2025predictionofsoft pages 8-9), though your problem (4–6 parameters, ~60 observations per dose) may need far fewer.

### M4. DA-Estimated Parameters as Latent Context for BO

**Confirmed with important caveats.** The idea of feeding the BO layer with filter-estimated lumped parameters (feed factor, cohesion index, lip-drain rate) rather than raw, possibly unmeasurable context variables (humidity history, PSD) is conceptually elegant. These estimates absorb the combined effect of multiple physical causes "in exactly the combination that matters for control."

Contextual Bayesian optimization with environmental variables is established in industrial control. De Blasi et al. (2024) demonstrated safe contextual BO for a steel straightening machine where context variables (steel thickness, target shape, environmental temperature) were integrated as additional GP dimensions using separable kernels (blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 5-6, blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 3-5, blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 6-7). The GP leverages similarities in past observations when contexts are similar, enabling adaptation to changing conditions (blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 5-6).

However, **no direct precedent was found for using DA-estimated latent parameters as GP context inputs in a closed BO loop.** This coupling is novel, and you have correctly identified the key risks:

1. **Endogeneity:** The context input is estimated from data generated by the policy the BO is optimizing. This creates a feedback loop where policy affects excitation, which affects parameter identifiability, which affects context, which affects policy.
2. **Excitation collapse:** If the BO converges to a low-excitation policy (e.g., very conservative dosing), parameter estimates may become uninformative, degrading the context signal.
3. **Uncertain inputs:** The GP would need to handle noisy/uncertain context. Heteroscedastic GP methods exist for input-dependent noise (bostijn2019amultivariateapproach pages 10-14), but propagating filter covariance through the GP kernel is non-trivial.

**Recommendation:** Start with raw context variables. Promote DA-estimated latent context only after demonstrating that (a) feed-factor estimates are stable across doses and (b) raw context is insufficient.

### M5. Noise Hyperparameters as DA Targets

**Confirmed in principle, but defer in practice.** Anderson (2001) introduced multiplicative covariance inflation for ensemble filters, with empirically selected factors (e.g., 1.01–1.45) to prevent filter divergence from overly confident prior covariances (anderson2001anensembleadjustment pages 5-7, anderson2001anensembleadjustment pages 7-8). Anderson (2009) extended this to spatially and temporally varying adaptive inflation. For your small system, the spatially varying machinery is unnecessary—a single scalar inflation factor suffices.

Innovation-based Q/R estimation methods (autocovariance least squares, Bayesian approaches) are available but add complexity. Chebbi et al. (2025) note that Q/R tuning remains an open challenge, with inaccurate noise modeling leading to filter divergence (chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 11-12). For your rig, the switching-covariance measurement model (inflate R during actuation) with fixed process noise Q is likely sufficient initially. The Poisson clump size and arching probability updates per powder are better handled in the dose-to-dose batch calibration than in online Q/R adaptation.

### M6. Where DA is Overkill or Fragile

**(a) Identifiability with one scalar channel:** Your suspicion of 2–3 simultaneously identifiable augmented parameters is well-supported. The literature confirms that strongly coupled states and parameters reduce observability, and distinguishing state versus parameter contributions to measured outputs becomes increasingly difficult with augmented state dimension (chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 21-23). The augmented methodology increases system dimensions, leading to tuning issues that can result in biased parameter estimates or filter divergence (sereno2026iterativecalibrationof pages 3-4). Attempting more than 2–3 parameters within a dose risks "re-labeling model error as parameter drift"—this is precisely the *compensation bias* or *sloppy parameter* phenomenon.

**Excitation design:** The convergence of adaptive observers depends critically on *persistent excitation* of regressor signals (sereno2026iterativecalibrationof pages 2-3). In your system, this means the dose profile must exercise the parameters you want to identify. A dose that goes straight to trickle mode provides no excitation for the bulk-phase feed factor. This connects directly to optimal input design: occasional "exploratory" doses with varied tilt/speed profiles would improve parameter identifiability, at the cost of dose quality.

**(b) Non-Gaussianity:** If you use a UKF instead of EnKF, the non-Gaussianity concern is somewhat mitigated because the UKF propagates sigma points through the true nonlinear dynamics rather than relying on Gaussian ensemble analysis. For the clumped discharge and arching regimes, an explicit mode-switching architecture (e.g., interacting multiple model / IMM filter) is more principled than a particle filter. A particle filter would handle arbitrary distributions but with high computational cost and particle degeneracy risks in 60-observation windows (jin2021thenewtrend pages 2-3).

**(c) EWMA vs. full filter:** Your planned EWMA feed-factor update IS indeed the steady-state scalar Kalman filter limit. The marginal value of a full sequential filter is primarily *uncertainty quantification*—providing not just a point estimate of feed factor but a confidence interval that the predictive cutoff and BO layers can consume. If your control layers do not use uncertainty (just point estimates), EWMA suffices. If they do (e.g., for conservative cutoff margins), the UKF is worth the upgrade.

**(d) Observability during quiet phases:** When actuators are off and flow is zero, parameters are indeed unobservable. In a properly tuned Kalman filter, the innovation is zero during these periods, so the Kalman gain naturally drives the update toward zero. With small random-walk process noise on parameters, the posterior uncertainty will slowly grow (as it should), but the point estimate will freeze. No explicit handling beyond appropriate process noise tuning is needed—this is the natural behavior of the filter (baldassarre2024digitaltwinwith pages 4-5).

**(e) Runtime architecture:** Your proposed architecture—DA runs host-side at 1 Hz, advising cutoff thresholds and next-dose parameters, never in the ~10 ms MicroPython actuation path—is sound. Baldassarre et al. (2024) used a similar architecture where the ASEKF ran alongside the digital twin on a host computer, with identified parameters fed back to the model for prediction (baldassarre2024digitaltwinwith pages 5-7, baldassarre2024digitaltwinwith pages 1-2).

## Answers to Explicit Questions

### Q1. Precedents

No published application of EnKF, ensemble smoothers, MHE, or joint state-parameter estimation to powder feeders, loss-in-weight feeders, or granular dosing systems was found. The nearest analogues are:

- **Pharmaceutical continuous manufacturing:** Digital twin development with state-space models and parameter updating for loss-in-weight feeders in continuous tableting lines (huang2023digitaltwindevelopment pages 141-144, huang2023digitaltwindevelopment pages 175-178, huang2023digitaltwindevelopment pages 178-181). Huang (2023) developed hybrid state-space models for LIW feeder systems with first-order autoregressive dynamics and PAT-based monitoring. Moving horizon estimation combined with NMPC has been referenced for pharmaceutical process control (huang2023digitaltwindevelopment pages 178-181).
- **Industrial digital twins with ASEKF:** Baldassarre et al. (2024) demonstrated augmented-state EKF for online parameter identification in industrial production systems (coffee machines, HVAC), which is the closest architectural analogue to what you propose (baldassarre2024digitaltwinwith pages 5-7, baldassarre2024digitaltwinwith pages 4-5, baldassarre2024digitaltwinwith pages 2-4, baldassarre2024digitaltwinwith pages 1-2).
- **Feed factor modeling:** Bostijn et al. (2019) and Li (2020) characterized how feed factor varies with hopper fill level, densification, bulk density, and screw design in pharmaceutical feeders (bostijn2019amultivariateapproach pages 10-14, bostijn2019amultivariateapproach pages 14-18, li2020predictiveperformanceof pages 36-40, li2020predictiveperformanceof pages 66-73, li2020predictiveperformanceof pages 49-53). Feed factor correlates strongly with conditioned bulk density (R² > 0.9) and decreases as hoppers empty (bostijn2019amultivariateapproach pages 10-14, li2020predictiveperformanceof pages 49-53). These empirical relationships validate your fill^0.7 multiplicative model but are not themselves DA approaches.
- **Geotechnical/reservoir:** ES-MDA for soil parameter calibration (zhao2025predictionofsoft pages 1-2, zhao2025predictionofsoft pages 4-6, zhao2025predictionofsoft pages 8-9).

### Q2. Identifiability

The literature confirms that augmented-state estimation with strongly coupled parameters reduces observability, making it difficult to distinguish state versus parameter contributions (chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 21-23). Augmented methodology increases system dimensions, causing tuning issues, biased estimates, and potential divergence (sereno2026iterativecalibrationof pages 3-4). For a single-channel, short-window system, the practical limit is 1–2 augmented parameters within a dose. The key enabler for identifiability is *persistent excitation* of regressor signals (sereno2026iterativecalibrationof pages 2-3)—in your system, this means dose profiles that vary tilt, speed, and tapping patterns to exercise different parameters. When parameter estimates are compensating for structural model error rather than tracking true parameter drift, the diagnostic is that estimated parameters drift to physically implausible values or that adding a model discrepancy term absorbs the variation previously attributed to parameters (zhu2603sequentialbayesianexperimental pages 1-3, lartaud2023multioutputgaussianprocess pages 14-17).

### Q3. Method Selection

For a 4–8 dimensional, bilinear, jump-noise system observed through a 1 Hz sensor:

- **UKF** is the recommended first choice: Jacobian-free, deterministic sigma-point propagation, second-order accuracy for smooth nonlinearities, medium computational cost (chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 11-12, jin2021thenewtrend pages 5-6, chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 4-6, chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 6-7). With n = 8, only 17 sigma points are needed.
- **Dual UKF** (separate state and parameter filters) is preferred over joint UKF for reduced divergence risk and better robustness (chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 11-12, chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 9-11).
- **EnKF** is scalable but introduces unnecessary stochastic sampling noise at this dimension; it is better suited to systems with dimension >> 10 (chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 11-12, chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 7-9).
- **Particle filter** handles arbitrary non-Gaussian distributions but with high computational cost; at 60 observations per dose, particle degeneracy is a real risk (jin2021thenewtrend pages 2-3).
- **MHE** provides constraint handling (e.g., non-negative mass, bounded parameters) but is computationally heavier and requires solving an optimization problem at each step. It is a viable alternative if physical constraints are critical.

For the jump-noise (arching, clumping) aspect, consider an **interacting multiple model (IMM)** approach with a flowing-mode UKF and a zero-flow-mode UKF, rather than trying to handle bimodality within a single filter (jin2021thenewtrend pages 6-8).

### Q4. Dose-to-Dose Batch Calibration

ES-MDA is a legitimate tool for dose-to-dose calibration. It uses all observations from a dose simultaneously across multiple iterations with inflated measurement errors, handling nonlinearity better than a single-pass smoother (zhao2025predictionofsoft pages 4-6, zhao2025predictionofsoft pages 2-4). However, for your problem scale (~4–6 parameters, ~60 observations per dose), ES-MDA may be over-engineered. A simpler batch maximum likelihood or maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation—running the digital twin forward with candidate parameters and minimizing residuals against the dose log—is likely sufficient initially. Reserve ES-MDA for when you need posterior uncertainty characterization or when MLE/MAP fails to converge due to multimodality.

Regarding the Kennedy–O'Hagan (KOH) model discrepancy term: the KOH framework separates the physical observation into simulator output, a discrepancy function (modeled as a GP), and observation error (zhu2603sequentialbayesianexperimental pages 3-6). A known challenge is that calibration parameters become confounded with GP hyperparameters and the discrepancy term, creating identifiability problems (zhu2603sequentialbayesianexperimental pages 1-3). Projection-based methods have been developed to improve identifiability. To keep parameters physical, restrict the discrepancy GP's flexibility (e.g., short lengthscales, bounded variance) so it captures only high-frequency residuals that the compartment model structurally cannot represent, rather than absorbing low-frequency trends that should be attributed to parameters.

### Q5. DA + BO Coupling

Contextual BO with environmental variables is established (blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 5-6, blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 3-5, blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 2-3, blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 1-2, blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 11-12). De Blasi et al. (2024) demonstrated safe contextual BO for industrial control where context (steel thickness, temperature) enters the GP via separable kernels (blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 5-6, blasi2024safecontextualbayesian pages 6-7). However, using *DA-estimated latent parameters* as GP context inputs appears to be novel. The closest related work involves causal BO with endogenous variables and exogenous distribution learning, but this is not the same coupling.

Known failure modes of closing the DA–BO loop include: (1) endogeneity—the policy affects the data from which context is estimated; (2) excitation collapse—convergence to conservative policies that provide insufficient excitation for parameter identification; (3) uncertain/noisy context—the GP must handle inputs with known estimation uncertainty, which standard GP kernels do not natively accommodate. Heteroscedastic GP methods that model input-dependent noise exist but typically address output noise rather than input uncertainty.

### Q6. Adaptive Noise Estimation

Anderson's (2001) covariance inflation approach uses a multiplicative factor (slightly > 1) on the prior covariance to prevent filter divergence from sampling errors (anderson2001anensembleadjustment pages 5-7). For the Lorenz-96 system, optimal inflation factors of ~1.01 were found empirically (anderson2001anensembleadjustment pages 7-8). For your small system, a single scalar inflation factor is sufficient—no localization or spatially varying inflation is needed. Your regime-switching R (inflate ~10× during actuation) is the correct approach for handling vibration-corrupted readings. Innovation-based Q/R estimation and autocovariance least squares methods are available but add implementation complexity for marginal benefit at this scale. The recommendation is to tune Q and R empirically or via a small grid search on a few calibration doses, and apply a fixed inflation factor (e.g., 1.02–1.10) to the UKF.

In summary: build the within-dose UKF (items 1–2) first, as it provides filtered state estimates and feed-factor tracking that directly improve predictive cutoff accuracy. Add simplified dose-to-dose batch calibration (item 3) next. Defer the DA-to-BO coupling (item 4) until the simpler context approach is demonstrably insufficient. Skip online Q/R adaptation (item 5) unless cross-powder performance is unsatisfactory.

The single most important correction to your musings is the method choice: **use a Dual UKF, not an EnKF**, for within-dose estimation. The UKF is the standard Jacobian-free filter for low-dimensional nonlinear systems (chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 11-12, jin2021thenewtrend pages 5-6, chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 4-6), and the dual architecture reduces divergence risk relative to joint augmentation (chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 11-12, chebbi2025simultaneousstateand pages 9-11). The EnKF's strengths—scalability to high dimensions, robustness to severe model uncertainty—are not needed at your state dimension, and its weaknesses—stochastic sampling noise, need for inflation tuning—are unnecessary complications.
