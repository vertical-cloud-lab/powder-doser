\section{Introduction and Motivation}

Additively manufactured (AM) structural alloys are an increasingly
important class of materials for space applications.  Launch-mass
reduction, topology-optimized geometries, and rapid iteration on bespoke
parts all favor laser powder-bed fusion (L-PBF, the dominant metal-AM
process for aerospace structural parts) over conventional subtractive
machining \citep{wei2021multimaterial}.  The bottleneck for discovering
and qualifying \emph{new} aerospace alloys for these processes -- as
opposed to printing established compositions -- is not the printers
themselves.  It is the upstream workflow that prepares the metal powder
feedstock from which each candidate composition is built
\citep{vecchio2024htread,liu2024htprep}.

That upstream workflow is the focus of the BYU Vertical Cloud Lab, a
self-driving-laboratory group that uses Bayesian optimization (a
machine-learning method that picks the next experiment most likely to
improve a target property) to choose and run the next-best alloy
experiment \citep{tom2024sdl,lo2024frugal}.  This style of closed-loop
discovery is gated by how quickly and reproducibly the lab can prepare
new compositions.  Accurate, scriptable powder dosing is therefore the
front-end on which every downstream measurement depends.

The central, repetitive step that dominates that front-end is
\emph{powder dosing}: weighing out and combining individual elemental or
pre-alloyed powders into a target blend before each downstream alloy
experiment (Fig.~\ref{fig:workflow}a).  In a research lab this is
almost always done by hand, one powder at a time, with a balance and a
spatula.  This is slow, error-prone, exposes researchers to fine
reactive metal powders, and -- critically -- caps the
\emph{compositional throughput} of any downstream alloy-discovery
campaign \citep{vecchio2021htread}.

Powder dispensing is therefore an unmet need in the research-lab
setting.  The few automated dispensers that do exist are validated
mainly on pharmaceutical powders and aimed at industrial high-throughput
experimentation: Mettler Toledo Quantos/CHRONECT, Chemspeed
SWING/FLEX, and Unchained Labs systems all run roughly
\$50\,k--\$150\,k+ when fully integrated, and even at those price
points their precision degrades sharply below 10~mg per dose and varies
strongly with powder properties \citep{bahr2018collaborative,bahr2020recent}.
Lower-cost gravimetric trickler dispensers do exist -- our group has
benchtested an Autotrickler unit -- but they are designed for the
coarse, free-flowing grain sizes of reloading powders and are simply
unusable for fine, cohesive metal powders such as silicon.  At the
research-AM end of the spectrum, the most complete published
integration of automated dosing with metal AM is the HT-READ platform
from Vecchio at UC San Diego.  HT-READ relies on a ChemSpeed-class
dispenser, a custom 16-hopper feeder, and a directed-energy-deposition
(DED) print head -- a powder-blown alternative to L-PBF that is more
forgiving of feedstock variability but produces parts with very
different microstructures \citep{vecchio2024htread,vecchio2021htread}.
We have not identified any sub-\$10\,k multi-powder doser validated for
L-PBF aerospace-alloy feedstocks in either the commercial or the
open-hardware landscape, and DIY designs in the self-driving-lab
community have not been rigorously characterized for the volumes,
cohesive flow, and cross-contamination constraints of L-PBF
\citep{tom2024sdl,lo2024frugal,pelkie2025democratizing}.

This project will close that gap with two interlocking contributions.
First, we will produce an \textbf{open-source, accurate, low-cost,
reliable multi-powder dispenser} purpose-built for L-PBF research
workflows.  It will handle up to \textbf{30 unique powders} with total
per-blend dispense volumes up to \textbf{250~mL}, and is designed to
slot in as the front-end of an autonomous AM aerospace-alloy discovery
loop.

Second, we will use the design of that dispenser as a real,
non-trivial test article for an \textbf{honest study of the
capabilities and current limitations of agentic AI} applied to
electromechanical and generative CAD design.  Existing peer-reviewed
work on AI-driven CAD restricts itself either to single-component
generation or to short ``can the model produce a bracket?''
demonstrations \citep{wu2021deepcad,xie2025text2cadquery,rukhovich2025cadrecode}.
Whether a tightly-supervised agentic loop can deliver an integrated
mechanical, electrical, and firmware design for a real research
instrument is an open question, and that is the question this project
is built to answer.  We are not testing whether AI can build a product
on its own -- we expect to guide it heavily.  We are testing how much
further the human--AI partnership can push that boundary on a problem
that actually matters for NASA-relevant materials research.

\section{Project Objectives}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0.25em,topsep=0.2em]
  \item \textbf{Open-source multi-powder doser.}  A working hardware
        prototype handling up to 30 distinct powders with per-blend
        total volumes up to 250~mL.  We will document dispense
        accuracy, repeatability, and cross-contamination behaviour on
        five to seven representative L-PBF feedstock powders.  CAD
        sources, firmware, bill of materials (BOM), assembly
        instructions, characterization data, and the AI prompts and
        transcripts that produced each design revision will all be
        released openly so other groups can adopt and extend the
        design.
  \item \textbf{Generative-CAD-first design process.}  As little
        traditional, hand-driven CAD as we can manage: ideally the
        engineer never opens a CAD GUI to author or edit geometry,
        only to inspect what the agentic loop produced.  Realistically
        -- and as a deliberate part of the experiment -- the engineer
        will be guiding the agent significantly, given the current
        state of the technology.  The goal is to characterize where
        that human steering is essential, where it is optional, and
        where the agent can run unaided.
  \item \textbf{Documented capability/limitation study and follow-on
        paper.}  A reproducible record of where agentic AI succeeded,
        where it failed, where engineer intervention was required, and
        what tooling unlocked each capability.  This serves as a
        litmus test for the current state of generative CAD applied to
        a real electromechanical instrument, and as the basis of a
        peer-reviewed paper released with the open-source design as
        supplemental material.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Approach and Methods}

\subsection{Generative CAD pipeline and AI--engineer roles}

The design loop is anchored on GitHub Copilot in agent mode, free to
invoke multiple underlying language models and to call out to auxiliary
tooling for code-CAD scripting, slicing, and printer control.  Each
iteration follows a build--test--feedback cycle.  The engineer (Sam)
describes a requirement or failure mode in natural language plus
reference data; the agent edits CAD sources, electrical layouts, and
firmware in scriptable, version-controlled formats; and prototypes are
printed in-house on a Bambu Lab~H2D used as the dispenser-prototyping
printer.  The near-term workflow has the engineer triggering prints
manually; a longer-term goal is for the agent to drive the printer
directly.

Every prompt, model output, and engineer correction is committed
alongside the design so the history is fully auditable -- it is the
dataset for the capability/limitation study, not an afterthought.  A
first iteration of this loop has already produced the rotating powder
tube shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:posetube}, prototyped in 24~hours during
the 2026 POSE workshop using the same agentic pipeline this proposal
will scale up.

\subsection{Hardware design targets}

Initial functional targets, to be refined as bench data accumulate:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0.05em,topsep=0.1em]
  \item up to 30 individually-addressable powder reservoirs;
  \item per-blend total volume up to 250~mL;
  \item per-powder dispense accuracy of approximately $\pm 1$~mg, with
        a stretch goal of $\pm 0.1$~mg for the smaller component
        powders that drive minor-alloying-element studies;
  \item negligible cross-contamination between adjacent reservoirs;
  \item compatibility with reactive aerospace-alloy powders.
\end{itemize}
An inert-atmosphere enclosure (needed for the most reactive powders
and for runs that must avoid oxidation) is treated as a v2
deliverable.  The v1 prototype is designed to accept such an enclosure
but will be characterized in ambient conditions.

\subsection{Validation and integration with the discovery loop}

Validation has two layers.  The first is a bench-level test of the
dispenser in isolation: gravimetric repeatability over hundreds of
doses, plus cross-contamination checks on five to seven representative
L-PBF feedstock powders.  The second is an integration test in which
the dosed elemental blends are fed into the BYU Vertical Cloud Lab's
in-house ultrasonic atomizer to produce spherical, L-PBF-grade
feedstock, which is then printed and characterized
(Fig.~\ref{fig:workflow}b--c).  This places the dispenser directly on
the critical path of the autonomous AM aerospace-alloy discovery loop
and ensures the validation regime matches the real downstream use
case.

\section{Broader Impact and Relevance to NASA}

The most direct line to NASA is through aerospace structural alloys.
A research-grade, low-cost, open-source multi-powder dispenser
shortens the compositional-discovery loop for the L-PBF alloys that go
into launch vehicles, in-space structures, and propulsion components.
The same dispensing problem appears, with different chemistries, in
solid and hybrid rocket propellant formulation, and in handling of
\emph{regolith} (the loose, fragmented surface material of the Moon
and Mars) and regolith simulants for in-situ resource utilization
(ISRU) and lunar/Martian regolith additive manufacturing.

The agentic-AI portion of the work also speaks to NASA's interest in
AI-assisted engineering for cost- and schedule-constrained missions:
a documented, honest assessment of where generative CAD currently
helps and where it currently fails is exactly the kind of evidence
mission engineering teams need before adopting these tools.  Releasing
the dispenser openly lowers the barrier of entry for other Utah and
NASA-affiliated labs that want to run AM alloy-discovery campaigns
without committing to industrial-scale capital equipment.
