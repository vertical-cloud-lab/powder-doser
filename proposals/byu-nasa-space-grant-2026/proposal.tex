\section{Introduction and Motivation}

Additively manufactured (AM) structural alloys are an increasingly important
class of materials for space applications, where launch-mass reduction,
topology-optimized geometries, and rapid iteration on bespoke parts all
favor laser powder-bed fusion (L-PBF) over conventional subtractive
processes \citep{wei2021multimaterial}.  Discovering and qualifying
\emph{new} aerospace alloys for these processes -- rather than only
printing established compositions -- is bottlenecked not by the printers
themselves, but by the upstream workflow that prepares the metal powder
feedstock from which each candidate composition is built
\citep{vecchio2024htread,liu2024htprep}.  That upstream workflow is
exactly the focus of the BYU Vertical Cloud Lab, a self-driving-laboratory
group that uses Bayesian optimization to choose and run the next-best
alloy experiment \citep{tom2024sdl,lo2024frugal}.  This style of
closed-loop discovery is gated by how quickly and reproducibly the lab
can prepare new compositions, which makes accurate, scriptable powder
dosing the front-end on which everything downstream depends.

The central, repetitive step that dominates that front-end is
\emph{powder dosing}: weighing out and combining individual elemental or
pre-alloyed powders into a target blend before each downstream alloy
experiment (Fig.~\ref{fig:workflow}a).  In a research lab this is
almost always done by hand, one powder at a time, with a balance and a
spatula.  This is slow, error-prone, exposes researchers to fine
reactive metal powders, and -- critically -- it caps the
\emph{compositional throughput} of any downstream alloy-discovery
campaign \citep{vecchio2021htread}.

Powder dispensing is therefore an unmet need in the research-lab
setting.  The few automated dispensers that do exist are validated
mainly on pharmaceutical powders and aimed at industrial high-throughput
experimentation: Mettler Toledo Quantos/CHRONECT, Chemspeed
SWING/FLEX, and Unchained Labs systems all run roughly
\$50\,k--\$150\,k+ when fully integrated, and even at those price
points their precision degrades sharply below 10~mg per dose and varies
strongly with powder properties \citep{bahr2018collaborative,bahr2020recent}.
Lower-cost gravimetric trickler dispensers do exist -- our group has
benchtested an Autotrickler unit -- but they are designed for the coarse,
free-flowing grain sizes of reloading powders and are simply unusable for
fine, cohesive metal powders such as silicon.  At the research-AM end of
the spectrum, the most complete published integration of automated
dosing with metal AM (the HT-READ platform from Vecchio at UC San Diego)
relies on a ChemSpeed-class system, a custom 16-hopper feeder, and a DED
print head \citep{vecchio2024htread,vecchio2021htread}.  No sub-\$10\,k
multi-powder doser validated for L-PBF aerospace alloy feedstocks has
been identified in either the commercial or open-hardware landscape,
and DIY designs in the self-driving-lab community have not been
rigorously characterized for the volumes, cohesive flow, and
cross-contamination constraints of L-PBF
\citep{tom2024sdl,lo2024frugal,pelkie2025democratizing}.

This project will close that gap with two interlocking contributions.
First, we will produce an \textbf{open-source, accurate, low-cost,
reliable multi-powder dispenser} purpose-built for L-PBF research
workflows -- handling up to \textbf{30 unique powders} with total
per-blend dispense volumes up to \textbf{250~mL}, designed to slot in as
the front-end of an autonomous AM aerospace-alloy discovery loop.
Second, we will use the design of that dispenser as a real, non-trivial
test article for an \textbf{honest study of the capabilities and
current limitations of agentic AI} applied to electromechanical and
generative CAD design.  Existing peer-reviewed work on AI-driven CAD
either restricts itself to single-component generation or to short
``can the model produce a bracket?'' demonstrations
\citep{wu2021deepcad,xie2025text2cadquery,rukhovich2025cadrecode}.
Whether a tightly-supervised agentic loop can deliver an integrated
mechanical~+~electrical~+~firmware design for a real research instrument
is an open question, and that is the question this project is built to
answer.  We are not testing whether AI can build a product alone; we
expect to guide it heavily.  We are testing how much further the
human--AI partnership can push that boundary on a problem that actually
matters for NASA-relevant materials research.

\section{Project Objectives}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0.05em,topsep=0.1em]
  \item \textbf{Open-source multi-powder doser.}  A working hardware
        prototype handling up to 30 distinct powders with per-blend total
        volumes up to 250~mL, with documented dispense accuracy,
        repeatability, and cross-contamination characterization on five
        to seven representative L-PBF feedstock powders.  CAD sources,
        firmware, BOM, assembly instructions, characterization data, and
        the AI prompts/transcripts that produced each design revision
        will all be released openly so other groups can adopt and extend
        the design.
  \item \textbf{Generative-CAD-first design process.}  As little
        traditional, hand-driven CAD as we can manage: ideally the
        engineer never opens a CAD GUI to author or edit geometry, only
        to inspect what the agentic loop produced.  Realistically -- and
        as a deliberate part of the experiment -- the engineer will be
        guiding the agent significantly given the current state of the
        technology; the goal is to characterize where that human
        steering is essential, where it is optional, and where the agent
        can run unaided.
  \item \textbf{Documented capability/limitation study and follow-on
        paper.}  A reproducible record of where agentic AI succeeded,
        where it failed, where engineer intervention was required, and
        what tooling unlocked each capability -- a litmus test for the
        current state of generative CAD applied to a real
        electromechanical instrument, and the basis of a peer-reviewed
        paper released with the open-source design as supplemental
        material.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Approach and Methods}

\subsection{Generative CAD pipeline and AI--engineer roles}

The design loop is anchored on GitHub Copilot in agent mode, free to
invoke multiple underlying language models and to call out to auxiliary
tooling for code-CAD scripting, slicing, and printer control.  Each
iteration follows a build--test--feedback cycle: the engineer (Sam)
describes a requirement or failure mode in natural language plus
reference data, the agent edits CAD sources, electrical layouts, and
firmware in scriptable, version-controlled formats, and prototypes are
printed in-house on a Bambu Lab~H2D used as the dispenser-prototyping
printer.  The near-term workflow has the engineer triggering prints; a
longer-term goal is for the agent to drive the printer directly.  Every
prompt, model output, and engineer correction is committed alongside
the design so the history is fully auditable -- it is the dataset for
the capability/limitation study, not an afterthought.  A first
iteration of this loop has already produced the rotating powder tube
shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:posetube}, prototyped in 24~hours during the 2026
POSE workshop using the same agentic pipeline this proposal will scale up.

\subsection{Hardware design targets}

Initial functional targets, to be refined as bench data accumulate:
up to 30 individually-addressable powder reservoirs; per-blend total
volume up to 250~mL; per-powder dispense accuracy of approximately
$\pm 1$~mg, with a stretch goal of $\pm 0.1$~mg for the smaller
component powders that drive minor-alloying-element studies; negligible
cross-contamination between adjacent reservoirs; and compatibility with
reactive aerospace alloy powders.  An inert-atmosphere enclosure is
treated as a v2 deliverable; the v1 prototype is designed to accept
such an enclosure but will be characterized in ambient conditions.

\subsection{Validation and integration with the discovery loop}

Validation combines bench-level tests of the dispenser in isolation
(gravimetric repeatability over hundreds of doses; cross-contamination
checks on five to seven representative L-PBF feedstock powders) with
an integration test in which the dosed blends are fed into the BYU
Vertical Cloud Lab's in-house ultrasonic atomizer to produce spherical,
L-PBF-grade feedstock, which is then printed and characterized
(Fig.~\ref{fig:workflow}b--c).  This places the dispenser directly on
the critical path of the autonomous AM aerospace-alloy discovery loop
and ensures the validation regime matches the real downstream use case.

\section{Broader Impact and Relevance to NASA}

The most direct line to NASA is through aerospace structural alloys: a
research-grade, low-cost, open-source multi-powder dispenser shortens
the compositional-discovery loop for the L-PBF alloys that go into
launch vehicles, in-space structures, and propulsion components.  The
same dispensing problem appears, with different chemistries, in solid
and hybrid rocket propellant formulation, and in handling of regolith
(the loose, fragmented surface material of the Moon and Mars) and
regolith simulants for in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) and
lunar/Martian regolith additive manufacturing.  The agentic-AI portion
also speaks to NASA's interest in AI-assisted engineering for cost- and
schedule-constrained missions: a documented, honest assessment of
where generative CAD currently helps and where it currently fails is
exactly the kind of evidence mission engineering teams need before
adopting these tools.  Releasing the dispenser openly lowers the
barrier of entry for other Utah and NASA-affiliated labs that want to
run AM alloy-discovery campaigns without committing to industrial-scale
capital equipment.
